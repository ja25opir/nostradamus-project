McCoy</a></div><div class="art-PostContent"><p><img class="alignright" title="songwriter comp" src="http://aclu-nm.org/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/songwriter-comp.jpg" alt="songwriter comp Border Singer/Songwriter Competition" width="170" height="200"><strong>(DEADLINE AUGUST 20, 2010</strong>)</p><p>The Southwestern Chapter of the ACLU of New Mexico is sponsoring a <strong>singer/songwriter competition</strong> in connection with its annual meeting to be held Sept. 17. Due to growing controversy regarding immigration and other border issues, the ACLU is looking for songs that deal with issues of civil liberties relating to border issues, immigration and/or the Arizona law, SB 1070. The competition is open to all musicians in the border regions of West Texas, New Mexico and Arizona who write and perform in any musical genre. The prize will be a four-hour recording session provided by the co-sponsor, Barefoot Studio. All submissions must be original compositions of the artist/performer (maximum two entries per artist) made on an official entry form and submitted with a tape or CD recording of the entry along with a written copy of the lyrics.</p><p><a href="http://aclu-nm.org/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/Rules1-1-2.pdf"></a><a href="http://aclu-nm.org/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/Rules1-1-2.pdf">Contest Rules</a></p><p><a href="http://aclu-nm.org/wp-content/uploads/2010/07/ContestForm.pdf">Official Entry Form</a></p><p>Mail entries to:</p><p>ACLU/Competition</p><p><span style="font-size: 13.2px;">c</span><span style="font-size: 13.2px;">/o Peter Falley</span></p><p><span style="font-size: 13.2px;">521 E. <em>Communications Specialist</em></p><p>I woke up Friday morning to read some good news: <a href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2010/07/15/argentina-gay-marriage-la_n_647129.html" This historic vote makes Argentina the tenth nation in the world to recognize the right of LGBT people to demonstrate their love and&nbsp;commitment&nbsp;to one another through marriage.</p><p><em>Wait, tenth?</em></p><p><span style="font-size: 13.2px;">Yep. The good old U.S. of A, leader of the free world and champion of democracy and human rights is lagging that far behind. Here are the countries that led the way for basic fairness in marriage:</span></p><p style="padding-left: 60px;">Netherlands (2001)</p><p style="padding-left: 60px;">Belgium (2003)</p><p style="padding-left: 60px;">Spain (2005)</p><p style="padding-left: 60px;">Canada (2005)</p><p style="padding-left: 60px;">South Africa (2006)</p><p style="padding-left: 60px;">Norway (2009)</p><p style="padding-left: 60px;">Sweden (2009)</p><p style="padding-left: 60px;">Portugal (2010)</p><p style="padding-left: 60px;">Iceland (2010)</p><p style="padding-left: 60px;">Argentina (2010)</p><p><span style="color: #ffffff;">.</span></p><p>This honor roll includes not just the usual suspects (the socially liberal Netherlands and Nordic countries) but also heavily Catholic countries (Spain, Portugal and Argentina). Add in South Africa, which legalized same-sex marriage a mere twelve years after the fall of Apartheid, and it just starts to get embarrassing. When did we stop leading?</p><p>Now instead of looking up to America as an example of freedom and equality, other nations look back at us through their rear-view mirror as they speed past us. How did this happen? I would argue that it is a result of our drift away from the principles in our most important founding documents: the Constitution and the Bill of Rights.</p><p>Under the 14th Amendment, all American citizens are&nbsp;guaranteed&nbsp;equal protection under the law. This essentially means that you cannot recognize the civil rights of one group of people and deprive another group of those same rights without due process of law. &nbsp;Under federal law, married couples receive over 1000 benefits and responsibilities that unmarried couples do not enjoy. Currently, same-sex couples are denied these benefits under the law simply because of who they love.</p><p>This is about caring, responsibility and commitment. Marriage would provide a way for committed same-sex couples to protect and care for the people they love. Denying committed couples basic legal protections hurts them; it‚Äôs wrong to make it harder for committed couples to take care of and be responsible for each other.&nbsp;<span style="font-size: 13.2px;">Hospital visitation rights, pension benefits and inheritance rights are just a few of the many legal protections same-sex couples do not have access to. </span></p><p>Opponents of marriage equality wish to deny the protections and societal acceptance that come with marriage because they sincerely believe they are protecting their concept of ‚Äútraditional marriage.‚Äù But what is a traditional marriage? Polygamy, dowries, arranged marriages, forced marriages of pre-adolescents, legal ownership of spouses: all once an integral part of ‚Äútraditional marriage.‚Äù Over time our understanding of what marriage should be has changed, and it‚Äôs time for us to change once more.</p><p>We‚Äôve fallen behind in this particular issue, but it‚Äôs never too late to start leading again. We need to reaffirm our commitment to fairness and equality by making the United States of America number 11 on that list.</p><p><span style="color: #ffffff;"><br> </span></p></div><div ACLU-NM applauds this principled move on the part of the Las Vegas,  NM Police Department, and in honor of their decision, we‚Äôd like to take a moment to talk a bit more about why other cities and counties should reexamine their policies on the use of this weapon as well.</p><p>Tasers are projectile weapons that use compressed nitrogen gas to propel two darts that deliver a 50,000 volt electric shock into the body of a target, causing temporary loss of muscle control and disablement. These weapons are marketed as a ‚Äúless than lethal‚Äù alternative for police officers and armed security guards to incapacitate a dangerous individual. However, there is a growing body of evidence that puts this ‚Äúless than lethal‚Äù brand in serious question.</p><p>Just for starters, Amensty International estimates that more than 350 individuals have died after being shocked by these ‚Äúless than lethal‚Äù devices since 2001. They also warn that we know dangerously little about the probable health risks associated with Taser use:</p><blockquote><p>Medical studies so far on the effects of Tasers have either been limited in scope or unduly influenced by the weapons‚Äô primary manufacturer. No study has adequately examined the impact of Tasers on potentially at-risk individuals ‚Äî people who have medical conditions, take prescription medications, are mentally ill or are under the influence of narcotics. Rigorous, independent, impartial study of their use and effects is urgently needed to determine what role Tasers may have played in the 351 deaths and to determine appropriate guidelines for future Taser use.</p></blockquote><p>In 2005, the ACLU of Northern California published an extensive study of Taser use in California entitled ‚Äú<a href="http://www.aclunc.org/issues/criminal_justice/police_practices/asset_upload_file389_5242.pdf">STUN GUN FALLACY: How the Lack of Taser Regulation Endangers Lives</a>,‚Äù which paints a compelling picture of how Tasers are used inappropriately and excessively in law enforcement. The report examines the deceptive marketing practices by the manufacturer (Taser International), lack of proper law enforcement training on the weapon‚Äôs use and a general lack of acknowledgement of the associated medical risks:</p><blockquote><p>In light of these concerns and the rising death toll associated with Taser use, the ACLU of Northern California (‚ÄúACLUNC‚Äù)has conducted a thorough survey of 79 law enforcement agencies throughout northern and central California to determine how Tasers are being used. A close review of thousands of pages of policy and training materials used by departments reveals that, despite the growing number of deaths, increasing concern from medical and other experts about Taser safety, and extensive media coverage of problems associated with Taser use, the weapon remains largely unregulated. Of the 79 departments surveyed, 56 have added Tasers to their weapons arsenals. Of those, 54 provided their Taser-use policies and/or training materials to the ACLU-NC, which concluded the following:</p><ul><li>Only four departments      regulate the number of times an officer may use a Taser on an individual.      The others place no restriction on the number of times a suspect can be      shot. This is particularly troubling considering that several of the targets      in California      died after being jolted multiple times.</li><li><span style="font-size: 13.2px;">Only four departments      created any of their own training materials for their officers. The rest      relied exclusively on materials produced by Taser International.</span></li></ul><ul><li>The training materials produced by Taser International and relied on by local law enforcement grossly exaggerate the safety of Tasers, downplay their risks, and misrepresent medical studies on their effects.</li></ul></blockquote><p>Tasers are certainly a legitimate alternative to conventional firearms in a life-threatening situation, but according to Amnesty International, 80% of the time they are used against people who are unarmed. Like the 14-year-old girl who was <a href="http://carlosmiller.com/2009/07/14/new-mexico-police-chief-tasers-girl-leaves-gaping-wound-on-her-head/">shot in the head</a> with a Taser in Tucumcari,  NM last year, &nbsp;sending 50,000 volts directly into her brain. Or someone like 72-year-old great grandmother Kathryn Winkfein, who was <a href="http://carlosmiller.com/2009/06/02/texas-deputy-uses-taser-on-72-year-old-great-grandmother/">tasered by a Texas constable</a> after refusing to sign a traffic ticket. Or like Anastasio Hernandez Rojas who died on May 28<sup>th</sup> after U.S. Border Patrol agents <a href="http://www.signonsandiego.com/news/2010/jun/14/stun-gun-death-adds-fuel-to-fire/">shocked him multiple times</a> even though he was already restrained in handcuffs.</p><p>If you would like to ask your local police or sheriff‚Äôs department to stop using Tasers or to reexamine their Taser use
