				<img src="http://s2.wp.com/wp-content/themes/pub/neat/images/h1.gif?m=1224279792g" class="lefth2img" alt="h1"><h2 id="post-4321"><a href="http://ajnoffthecharts.com/2010/04/16/are-nursing-strikes-ethical-new-research-raises-the-stakes/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Are Nursing Strikes Ethical? New Research Raises the&nbsp;Stakes">Are Nursing Strikes Ethical? New Research Raises the&nbsp;Stakes</a></h2>
				<small>April 16, 2010</small>
				
				<div class="entry">
					<div class="snap_preview"><p><em>By Shawn Kennedy, MA, RN, </em>AJN<em> interim editor-in-chief</em></p>
<div id="attachment_4324" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/love4loaded/3023677803/"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-4324" title="ToughDecision" src="http://ajnoffthecharts.files.wordpress.com/2010/04/toughdecision.jpg?w=150&amp;h=100" alt="" width="150" height="100"></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Tough Decision/by love4loaded, via Flickr </p></div>
<p><strong>Nurses at Temple University Hospital in Philadelphia</strong> have been <a href="http://www.kyw1060.com/Mediator-Calls-Nurses--Temple-Hosp--Back-to-Table/6812635">on strike</a> since March 31st over a number of issues including wages, health benefits,&nbsp;and a “gag order” that could prohibit nurses from speaking out against the hospital. Nurses walking picket lines is not a new phenomenon. What <em>is</em> new is research showing that patients suffer harm when nurses strike.</p>
<p>In March, <a href="http://www.nber.org/papers/w15855.pdf">a paper</a> (subscription only) published by the <a href="http://www.nber.org/">National Bureau of Economic Research</a> provided some evidence that nurses’ strikes have harmful effects on patients. The authors analyzed strikes (in all, 50 strikes in 43 hospitals) in New York State over a 20-year period and looked at what happens to inpatient mortality rates and 30-day readmission rates for patients admitted during a strike. They found that inpatient hospital mortality increased by 19.4% and that readmission within 30 days increased by 6.5%. The researchers asked, “Is this because [patients] receive less care, or because they receive worse care?”<strong><em> </em></strong>And, in an analysis to see if the results were different in strikes where management hired replacement workers, it showed they were not—outcomes were still worse.</p>
<p><strong>These findings really shouldn’t come as a surprise.</strong> How can care be safe when there are fewer nurses than the normal levels (which often are already less than adequate for providing optimum care)? How can care be safe when replacement nurses—whether newly hired or shifted from other positions—are plopped onto units with little time to get to know the patient or families? (This is the “nurse-is-a-nurse-is-a-nurse” concept—also known as the “just send me a warm body” approach.)</p>
<p>So now I wonder: will employers at hospitals where nurses strike try to make nurses the “bad guys,” claiming striking nurses have no regard for patients or are failing to follow the professional code? This has been the argument that has stopped nurses from striking for years and is still the reason many nurses will cross a picket line or not join a union.</p>
<p><strong>But the alternative question is this:</strong> is it better to take a stand now to change the status quo so that, ultimately, patient care and working conditions and staffing improve—and thus, in the long run, more patients get better care? It’s a question I’ve always struggled with. I have been fortunate in that I’ve not worked in a facility during a labor dispute—but that’s perhaps because the state nurses’ association that negotiated our contracts did a good job and I was a recipient of others’ hard work and hard choices.</p>
<p><strong>What about you?<em> </em>Would you strike now for better conditions in the future, or would you cross the line to provide care for the patients already there?</strong><!-- AddThis Button BEGIN --><br>

				<img src="http://s2.wp.com/wp-content/themes/pub/neat/images/h1.gif?m=1224279792g" class="lefth2img" alt="h1"><h2 id="post-4187"><a href="http://ajnoffthecharts.com/2010/03/31/in-long-term-care-whats-favoritism/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to In Long-Term Care, What’s&nbsp;Favoritism?">In Long-Term Care, What’s&nbsp;Favoritism?</a></h2>
				<small>March 31, 2010</small>
				
				<div class="entry">
					<div class="snap_preview"><p><em>By Sheena Jones, an LPN who is in training to be an RN at Dutchess Community College, Poughkeepsie, NY</em></p>
<div id="attachment_4188" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/eggybird/82241276/"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-4188" title="birthdaycake" src="http://ajnoffthecharts.files.wordpress.com/2010/03/birthdaycake.jpg?w=150&amp;h=112" alt="" width="150" height="112"></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Birthday Cake/by Eggybird, via Flickr</p></div>
<p>Is it really fair when&nbsp;we get&nbsp;the favoritism speech from our superiors when we supply residents who have no family or friends with hygiene supplies? When there are two roommates and one has family and friends who visit daily and bring her all that she could need or want and the other has nothing and no one? Am I wrong for getting a couple&nbsp;of&nbsp;supplies from the dollar store for her? We all know that the hygiene supplies in&nbsp;many facilities are watered down and cheap. Am I wrong for buying someone some socks when they have none? We can’t share supplies or clothing between patients, so do I let someone walk around with nothing? If these people were my family or friends I would want someone to make them comfortable. They can’t leave the facility to go shopping with family or friends, and&nbsp;many of them have lost most of their mental capacity and have no one to help them—but that does not mean that they should walk around less put together than someone with a family? Do we just let these residents go without?<br>

				<img src="http://s2.wp.com/wp-content/themes/pub/neat/images/h1.gif?m=1224279792g" class="lefth2img" alt="h1"><h2 id="post-3915"><a href="http://ajnoffthecharts.com/2010/02/26/the-power-of-no/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to The Power of&nbsp;No">The Power of&nbsp;No</a></h2>
				<small>February 26, 2010</small>
				
				<div class="entry">
					<div class="snap_preview"><blockquote><p><a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2010/02/26/opinion/26krugman.html?em"><img class="alignleft size-thumbnail wp-image-3916" title="afflictingtheafflicted" src="http://ajnoffthecharts.files.wordpress.com/2010/02/afflictingtheafflicted.png?w=150&amp;h=145" alt="" width="150" height="145"></a>. . . . What really struck me about the meeting, however, was the inability of Republicans to explain how they propose dealing with the issue that, rightly, is at the emotional center of much health care debate: the plight of Americans who suffer from pre-existing medical conditions. In other advanced countries, everyone gets essential care whatever their medical history. But in America, a bout of cancer, an inherited genetic disorder, or even, in some states, having been a victim of domestic violence can make you uninsurable, and thus make adequate health care unaffordable.</p>
<p>One of the great virtues of the Democratic plan is that it would finally put an end to this unacceptable case of American exceptionalism. But what’s the Republican answer? Mr. Alexander was strangely inarticulate on the matter, saying only that “House Republicans have some ideas about how my friend in Tullahoma can continue to afford insurance for his wife who has had breast cancer.” He offered no clue about what those ideas might be.</p></blockquote>
<p>That’s from <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2010/02/26/opinion/26krugman.html?em">“Afflicting the Afflicted,”</a> Paul Krugman’s NY<em> Times</em> column about the health care reform summit that took place yesterday. It really is bewildering that so many elected officials can simply refuse to engage one of the major issues of our time. How can their supporters not begin to wonder at this as they themselves in ever greater numbers go into medical bankruptcy, are forced to choose between basic medications for chronic illnesses, and spend days on end struggling with insurance companies over every simple claim they make? Didn’t any of the naysayers notice the increases in insurance premiums announced this week, for example, with some major plans raising premiums by as much as 35%?</p>
<p>Nurses: Are your patients’ lives really improving? Are they getting any easier? Don’t they deserve a sincere effort to solve their problems, rather than more posturing designed to win votes in the next election cycle?</p>
<p><em>-Jacob Molyneux, senior editor/blog editor</em><br>

				<img src="http://s2.wp.com/wp-content/themes/pub/neat/images/h1.gif?m=1224279792g" class="lefth2img" alt="h1"><h2 id="post-3827"><a href="http://ajnoffthecharts.com/2010/02/16/nurses-under-fire-cleared-in-texas-embattled-in-california/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Nurses Under Fire: Cleared in Texas, Embattled in&nbsp;California">Nurses Under Fire: Cleared in Texas, Embattled in&nbsp;California</a></h2>
				<small>February 16, 2010</small>
				
				<div class="entry">
					<div class="snap_preview"><p><em>By Shawn Kennedy, interim editor-in-chief</em></p>
<p><strong><a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2010/02/12/us/12nurses.html"><img class="alignleft size-thumbnail wp-image-3829" title="WhistleblowerNurse" src="http://ajnoffthecharts.files.wordpress.com/2010/02/whistleblowernurse.png?w=150&amp;h=105" alt="" width="150" height="105"></a>By now you’ve probably heard</strong> that last Thursday Anne Mitchell, the Texas nurse who found herself fired from her job and on trial after reporting a physician for what she felt was poor medical practice (see our <a href="http://journals.lww.com/ajnonline/Fulltext/2009/10000/Texas_Nurses_Under_Fire_for_Whistleblowing.8.aspx">report</a> in the October 2009 issue) was found not guilty (her colleague, Vicki Galle, had her case dismissed prior to trial). It only took the jury about an hour to exonerate Mitchell of criminal charges and uphold her right to advocate for patients. (Day-by-day <a href="http://www.texasnurses.org/displaycommon.cfm?an=1&amp;subarticlenbr=509#rested">reports</a> from the trial were made available on the Texas Nurses Association &nbsp;(TNA) Web site.)</p>
<p>The case garnered national attention—at its core was a nurse’s right (duty, really) to safeguard patients in her or his care. It seemed a no-brainer, and almost incredible that the case even came to trial. Last Friday, I spoke with TNA president Susan Sportsman, PhD, RN, who agreed, saying she was surprised the case went forward, especially after the state medical board agreed that what the nurses did was appropriate. Sportsman said, “This is the role of nurses—it’s required that we report what we see to safeguard patients. This nurse was just doing her job, what she was supposed to do.”</p>
<p>Like nurses everywhere, Sportsman was “delighted and ecstatic” about the verdict. Sportsman noted a guilty verdict would have discouraged nurses and others from coming forward about poor care out of fear of losing their jobs and facing prosecution.&nbsp; Perhaps more chilling would be the result that nurses would be powerless and without support to protect patients from unsafe care. (See JParadisi’s excellent <a href="http://jparadisirn.com/2010/02/15/whistle-blowers-patient-advocates-when-the-nurse-stands-alone/">piece</a> on this on her blog.)</p>
<p><strong>Nurses seem to be coming under fire lately</strong>—witness the lawsuit California anesthesiologists have filed against their governor. According to the petition (available on the American Association of Nurse Anesthetists’ <a href="http://www.aana.com/csalawsuit020510.aspx">Web site</a>), the anesthesiologists charge that, in following Medicare policy allowing a state to “opt out” of the physician supervision requirement, the governor is acting outside of California law in allowing nurse anesthetists to practice without physician supervision. It’s another case that bears watching (and rallying!) by all nurses, not just CRNAs. Nurses have a right to practice within the scope allowed under the law and for which they are qualified; a challenge to one nursing group should be a challenge to all of us.</p>
<p><strong>What’s your take on these issues?</strong> Will the Mitchell case (despite the verdict in her favor) discourage potential whistle-blowers among nurses? And should physician groups really be&nbsp;attempting to restrict nurses’ scope of practice?<br>

Listen to Your&nbsp;Patients">Want to Achieve the ‘Greatest Good’? Listen to Your&nbsp;Patients</a></h2>
				<small>February 9, 2010</small>
				
				<div class="entry">
					<div class="snap_preview"><blockquote><p>Ethical dilemmas abound in nursing practice. Consider these commonplace scenarios:</p>
<p>* An angry patient threatens to leave the hospital against medical advice. Should you hold him against his will?</p>
<p>* A cancer patient fears chemotherapy. Should you give less detailed information about the effects of anticancer drugs?</p>
<p>* An obese home care patient with pressure ulcers refuses to cooperate in turning. Should you turn her anyway?</p>
<p>Such conflicts between the patient’s wishes and the nurse’s perception of the patient’s best interests occur regularly. That doesn’t make these ethical dilemmas any easier to resolve, but how nurses approach them can significantly affect clinical outcomes. Taking the time to listen to patients—and to integrate relationship skills with principles of ethical practice—can help nurses achieve solutions that are both ethical and appropriate for individual patients.</p></blockquote>
<div id="attachment_3783" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 160px"><a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/ky_olsen/3133347219/"><img class="size-thumbnail wp-image-3783" title="Listen" src="http://ajnoffthecharts.files.wordpress.com/2010/02/listen.jpg?w=150&amp;h=126" alt="" width="150" height="126"></a><p class="wp-caption-text">ky olsen/via Flickr</p></div>
<p><strong>That’s from</strong>&nbsp;the February issue of <em>AJN,</em> in which nurse–ethicist Doug Olsen (who has in the past written for this blog on&nbsp;ethical issues&nbsp;related to<a href="http://ajnoffthecharts.wordpress.com/2009/08/26/is-it-ethical-for-a-nurse-to-decline-the-h1n1-vaccine/"> mandated H1N1 vaccinations </a>for nurses)&nbsp;offers <a href="http://journals.lww.com/ajnonline/Fulltext/2010/02000/Want_to_Achieve_the__Greatest_Good___Listen_to.40.aspx">a thoughtful discussion </a>that may resonate for all nurses who’ve ever faced a situation like&nbsp;those in the above examples. It may seem obvious or cliched to say that&nbsp;listening to patients can help solve apparently intractable problems—but just because listening as a skill is hard to measure doesn’t mean that it’s not sometimes effective where more rigid tactics would fail.<br>

				<img src="http://s2.wp.com/wp-content/themes/pub/neat/images/h1.gif?m=1224279792g" class="lefth2img" alt="h1"><h2 id="post-3511"><a href="http://ajnoffthecharts.com/2010/01/07/some-observations-in-response-to-the-ny-times-article-on-palliative-sedation/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Some Observations In Response to the NY Times Article on Palliative&nbsp;Sedation">Some Observations In Response to the NY Times Article on Palliative&nbsp;Sedation</a></h2>
				<small>January 7, 2010</small>
				
				<div class="entry">
					<div class="snap_preview"><p><em>By Judy Schwarz, PhD, RN*</em>&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p><a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2009/12/27/health/27sedation.html?_r=2"><img class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-3512" title="TerminalSedationNYTimes" src="http://ajnoffthecharts.files.wordpress.com/2010/01/terminalsedationnytimes.png?w=300&amp;h=281" alt="" width="300" height="281"></a><br>
The <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2009/12/27/health/27sedation.html?_r=1">NY <em>Times</em> article </a>of 12/26/09 that described use of palliative sedation in hospice institutional settings provided helpful and clinically accurate informative—for the most part. These few notes are meant to address those issues raised by the article that may unduly alarm dying patients, their families, and their clinical caregivers.</p>
<p><em><br>
<strong>1)&nbsp;</strong></em><strong>There is a consensus</strong> among palliative care clinicians that “palliative sedation to unconsciousness” (a descriptive&nbsp;term that eliminates some of the visceral reaction elicited&nbsp;by use of the term “terminal sedation”) is an intervention used only when other therapies that do not compromise patient consciousness have failed and the patient continues to experience intolerable and&nbsp;intractable suffering that cannot otherwise be relieved.</p>
<p><strong>2) Use of palliative sedation to unconsciousness</strong> has NOT been shown to cause a hastened death. Research showing that patients at the very end of life who receive palliative sedation do not die more quickly than patients who are not sedated has been published in such peer-reviewed journals as <em>Annals of Oncology</em>, <em>Journal of Palliative Medicine</em>, <em>Journal of Pain and Symptom Management</em>, <em>Archives of Internal Medicine</em>, and <em>Palliative Medicine</em>. (In response to the <em>Times</em> article, the National Hospice and Palliative Care Organization has made available&nbsp;a&nbsp;<a href="http://www.nhpco.org/files/NY-Times_palliative-sedat_biblio.pdf">bibliography of these articles</a>.)</p>
<p>This intervention is generally only provided when patients are “imminently” dying (a condition the recognition of which requires experience and clinical judgment) and is distinct from&nbsp;”respite sedation,” which is used&nbsp;when&nbsp;clinicians plan to awaken a patient from the unconscious state to determine if their suffering has been relieved. It would be clinically inappropriate to awaken a dying patient whose suffering was deemed intractable and intolerable to them.</p>
<p><strong>3)&nbsp;The <em>Times&nbsp;</em>article mentions</strong> an article published by Billings and Block in 1996 in the <em>Journal of Palliative Care</em> (vol 12, pp 21-30), an article intended to highlight the inappropriate use of morphine drips that were ordered by some physicians with the intention of causing a merciful death. These two very skilled and experienced palliative care clinicians were attempting to highlight the difference between the appropriate use of palliative sedation, in which the infusion of opiate and sedative is titrated and set to relieve suffering and cause unconsciousness, and&nbsp;“hanging a morphine drip,”&nbsp;in which&nbsp;the infusion rate is continuously increased in order to cause obtundation, respiratory depression, and death.</p>
<p><strong>4)&nbsp;Finally, the one issue that was not addressed</strong> in this generally excellent article was the difficulties faced by dying, suffering patients whose symptoms merit use of palliative sedation to unconsciousness but who want to be able to die at home. In my experience, most hospices are unable or unwilling to provide this intervention to suffering patients who want to die comfortably and peacefully in their own homes.</p>
<p><em>*Schwarz, the author of </em><a href="http://journals.lww.com/ajnonline/Fulltext/2009/09000/Stopping_Eating_and_Drinking.28.aspx"><em>“Stopping Eating and Drinking,” </em></a><em>published in the September 2009 issue of</em> AJN, <em>is a regional clinical coordinator at Compassion and Choices, a nonprofit end-of-life advocacy and consultative organization.</em><br>

<blockquote><p>Jeanine was in her 60s. She wasn’t a church member and I barely knew her. Trying to get my bearings, I leaned over her and recited the words of the Twenty-third Psalm: “The Lord is my shepherd …. Yea, though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil ….”</p>
<p>“Help me,” Jeanine moaned. Her eyes opened and then closed. I knew she was pleading for release from her pain.</p>
<p>“Jeanine, I’m so sorry,” I whispered. I hurried to the nurses’ station. When a young nurse looked up, I asked if she could do more to relieve Jeanine’s pain.</p>
<p>“Nothing more to do,” she said, looking back down at an open ledger.</p></blockquote>
<p style="text-align:left;">The above is an excerpt from the&nbsp;<a href="http://journals.lww.com/ajnonline/Fulltext/2009/12000/Baring_Witness.35.aspx">Reflections essay</a> in the December issue of <em>AJN</em>. It’s by a retired clergywoman who tells of a moment early in her career that brought her face to face with a doctor who believed in a very different kind of God than her own.&nbsp;Click the&nbsp;link above to read the essay in entirety. &nbsp;</p>
