					<h1>Enterprise 2.0: The Book and the Blurbs</h1>
				</div>
				<div class="format_text">
<p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1422125874?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=andmcaswebsit-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=1422125874"><img src="http://andrewmcafee.org/wp-content/uploads/2009/08/51nANsxCKcL._SL160_3.jpg" border="4" alt=""></a>My book <em><a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1422125874?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=andmcaswebsit-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=1422125874">Enterprise 2.0: New Collaborative Tools for Your Organization’s Toughest Challenges</a><img style="border:none !important; margin:0px !important;" src="http://www.assoc-amazon.com/e/ir?t=andmcaswebsit-20&amp;l=as2&amp;o=1&amp;a=1422125874" border="0" alt="" width="1" height="1"></em> will be published this fall by <a href="http://harvardbusiness.org/?cm_mmc=google-_-Brand+-+Harvard+Business-_-harvard+business+press-_-Broad|-|100000000000000007516&amp;cm_guid=1-_-100000000000000007516-_-3797450325">Harvard Business Publishing</a>. It represents the culmination of research I’ve been doing since the spring of 2006, when I coined the phrase “Enterprise 2.0″ in a <a href="http://sloanreview.mit.edu/"><em>Sloan Management Review</em></a> <a href="http://sloanreview.mit.edu/the-magazine/articles/2006/spring/47306/enterprise-the-dawn-of-emergent-collaboration/">article</a> and started a <a href="http://andrewmcafee.org/blog/">blog</a>. The book is about the business use and business impact of emergent social software platforms (ESSPs). These technologies, which include wikis, blogs, prediction markets, Facebook, and Twitter, have given rise to Web 2.0. <em>Enterprise 2.0</em> relates how ESSPs are now being used within and between organizations, and are delivering novel capabilities and powerful results.</p>
<blockquote><p><strong>With Enterprise 2.0, Andrew  McAfee has identified a truly disruptive innovation. If you want to  profit from it, read this book.</strong></p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><a href="http://www.claytonchristensen.com/"><strong>Clayton Christensen</strong></a>, Robert and Jane Cizik Professor of Business Administration    at the Harvard Business School, and author of <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Innovators-Dilemma-Revolutionary-Business-Essentials/dp/0060521996"><em>The Innovator’s Dilemma</em></a></p>
<p><strong>We are on the cusp of a management  revolution that is likely to be as profound and unsettling as the one  that gave birth to the modern industrial age.</strong> Driven by the emergence  of powerful new collaborative technologies, this transformation will  radically reshape the nature of work, the boundaries of the enterprise,  and the responsibilites of business leaders. &nbsp;In Enterprise 2.0, Andrew  McAfee delivers essential insights into the critical and fast-changing  interface between IT and the organization. &nbsp;<strong>If you’re intent on positioning  your business for the future, and would rather lead than follow, you  need to read this book.</strong></p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><a href="http://www.garyhamel.com/"><strong>Gary Hamel</strong></a>, author    of <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Leading-Revolution-Gary-Hamel/dp/0452283248"><em>Leading the Revolution</em></a> and <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Competing-Future-Gary-Hamel/dp/0875847161"><em>Competing for the Future</em></a></p>
<p>As businesses and organizations leverage the power of networked Web 2.0 technologies, we will see companies and countries drive the next significant phase of Internet productivity. <strong>In <em>Enterprise 2.0</em>, McAfee articulates both the opportunities and the challenges ahead of us that will require a shift in thinking as well as new business models.</strong> This book is a valuable resource that underlines the importance of staying ahead of this market transition or risk missing the opportunity to capture the power of collaboration, new innovations and operational efficiencies.</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><a href="http://newsroom.cisco.com/dlls/execs/chambers-john.html">John Chambers</a>, CEO of Cisco</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><strong>Contents</strong></h2>
<p>The book is divided into two parts: part I explains what Enterprise 2.0 is and why it’s valuable, while part II offers guidance for managers about how to deploy the new tools and practices of collaboration successfully.</p>
<p>Chapter 2 kicks off part I by presenting four recent case studies of organizations facing familiar challenges; these case studies are not resolved in the chapter, but instead left hanging. Chapter 3 then takes a short step backward in time to describe how a group of new collaborative technologies and communities appeared on the Internet over the past few years. Chapter 4then returns to the case studies, discussing how each of the organizations adopted the new technologies and collaborative greatly from them. This chapter also places the case studies within a broader framework that illustrates the value of Enterprise 2.0 for four different categories of user: strongly tied colleagues (in other words close ones), weakly tied colleagues, potential collaborators who are not yet working together, and “professional strangers.” Chapter 5 concludes part I by discussing the concrete business benefits provided by successful Enterprise 2.0 deployments.</p>
<p>Part II explains how to successfully apply the new technologies of collaboration and the work practices that make use of them. Chapter 6 begins by listing the most common concerns that deter organizations and their leaders from embarking on an Enterprise 2.0 effort. Chapter 7 offers both technologists and general managers guidelines for overcoming these concerns and succeeding in the use of Enterprise 2.0 initiatives. Chapter 8 concludes the book by making some grounded projections about the future of collaborative work, taking into account both the largest threats and the greatest opportunities associated with the tools now available.</p>
<blockquote><p><strong>Andrew McAfee is the king of  Enterprise 2.0. He’s written a book that is low on hype and high on  business value. There is much to gain from exploring these participative  technologies, and little to fear.</strong></p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><a href="http://www.tomdavenport.com/"><strong>Thomas H. Davenport</strong></a>,    President’s Distinguished Professor of IT and Management, Babson College,    and co-author of <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Competing-Analytics-New-Science-Winning/dp/1422103323"><em>Competing on Analytics</em></a></p>
<p>Andrew<strong> McAfee coined the term  Enterprise 2.0 to describe a phenomenon that has changed the way the  world does business. Now, he takes it a step further. Whether</strong> your firm  is already deeply embedded in Enterprise 2.0 or you are trying to communicate  its value to your staff and your customers, <strong>you will soon wear out this  book by repeatedly referring to its thoughtful descriptions, advice,  and insights.</strong></p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><a href="http://runningahospital.blogspot.com/"><strong>Paul F. Levy</strong></a>, President    and CEO, Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center</p>
</blockquote>
