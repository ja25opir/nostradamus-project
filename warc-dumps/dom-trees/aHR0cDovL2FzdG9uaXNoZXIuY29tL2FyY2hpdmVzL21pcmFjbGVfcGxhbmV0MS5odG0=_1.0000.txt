						<h1><i><font size="4">From PBS-TV...</font></i><br>
							The Miracle Planet</h1>
						<p><em><font size="4"><strong>MY KIDS REMEMBER </strong>
    <u>The Miracle Planet</u> TV series as a big event in our family because it required the purchase of a TV and VCR.</font></em></p>
						<p><font size="4"><em>When I look back at the six <a href="#script">scripts</a> I wrote for the series, though, what strikes me now is how many hunches KCTS-TV Director Barry Stoner and I played, and how well the science has fallen into line behind in the years that followed.</em></font></p>
    <p><em><font size="4">For instance, Luis and Walter Alvarez's theory that a meteorite-caused natural catastrophe killed the dinosaurs was still highly speculative then, but <u>The
    Miracle Planet</u> understood the importance of the idea, and how it connected to the big
    picture. </font></em></p>
						<p><font size="4"><i>It wasn't until 1991, two years after <u>The Miracle Planet</u> series debuted on PBS-TV and almost four years after this script was written, that scientists located the impact site of the meteor that killed the dinosaurs 65 million years ago, Chicxulub crater off what is now Mexico's Yucatan Peninsula</i></font></p>
						<p><font size="4"><i>And another 20 years would pass before rock samples recovered from Graphite Peak, Antarctica, would indicate that a meteor impact may have also caused the "great dying" that occurred 250 million years ago, the so-called Permian-Triassic (or P-T) mass extinction, which was the world's worst.</i></font></p>
						<p><em><font size="4"><b>IN ADDITION</b> to a TV and VCR, working on <u>The
      Miracle Planet</u> gave me the broad natural sciences education I'd somehow evaded in school.</font></em></p>
						<p><em><font size="4">The challenges of writing for the TV camera proved educational as well. </font></em><em><font size="4">When we started filming the series, for instance, it quickly became apparent that the narrator, Bill Kurtis, couldn't move both ways on the emotional scale. </font></em></p>
						<p><em><font size="4">He could downshift his pacing and mood -- the verbal equivalent of a diminuendo -- but he couldn't move the other way, increasing his pace and emotional intensity as he played out the narrative.</font></em></p>
						<p><em><font size="4">Oh, really? Kurtis, who came from CBS Radio, was essentially a guy who couldn't handle the ball to his forehand at all, and so to make him look credible on camera I had to serve him a steady diet of balls to his backhand, which is a fairly bizarre way to tell a story.</font></em></p>
						<p><em><font size="4"><b>BUT&nbsp;IT&nbsp;ALL</b> worked out well. The <u>Miracle Planet</u> went on to be one of PBS-TV's most popular earth science shows, won a bunch of awards, and spawned </font></em><em><font size="4">t</font></em><em><font size="4">he gorgeous <u>The Miracle Planet</u> book, which was largely the work of my wife Lane Morgan.</font></em></p>
						<p><em><font size="4">The <u>Miracle Planet</u> book was, in fact, a major undertaklng in itself, with its own daunting slew of editorial, scientific and trans-oceanic production challenges (the book was published by an Australian publisher, while Lane and I lived in the United States).</font></em></p>
						<p><em><font size="4">Ultimately translated into four languages and published globally, Lane's <u>Miracle Planet</u> book remains a dazzling demonstration of just how good a coffee table book can be.    </font></em></p>
						<div align="right">
							<p><font size="4"><em>-- Bruce Brown</em></font></p>

					<h4 align="left"><a name="script"></a><em>The First Episode from...</em></h4>
					<h1 align="left">The Miracle Planet</h1>
					<h2 align="left"><em>"The Third Planet"</em></h2>
					<h3 align="left">by Bruce Brown</h3>
					<table border="0" cellpadding="6" width="645">
						<tbody><tr>
							<td bgcolor="#000080"><font color="#FFFFFF"><strong><i>Audio</i></strong></font></td>
							<td bgcolor="#000080"><font color="#FFFFFF"><em><strong>Video</strong></em></font></td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td valign="top">For generations, people have wondered about the earth.
								<p>Could our world once have been bombarded by meteorites like the ones that cratered the surface of the moon?</p>
								<p>Were dinosaurs the victims of a later day meteorite impact that caused mass extinctions?</p>
								<p>Could meteorites strike again and bring about a great cataclysm?</p>
								<p>Join us in a search for the meteorites that have shaped our world. They were here at our planet's birth, and they've left clues of their return.</p>
								<p>"The Third Planet" -- this time on <em>The Miracle Planet</em>.</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_87x.jpg" width="200" height="150">
								<p><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_88x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></p>
							</td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td valign="top">SERIES OPEN -- TITLE MUSIC
								<p>FUNDING CREDITS -- BULL, AMOCO, CBC</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_89x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td valign="top">NARRATOR ON CAMERA: Hello, I'm Bill Kurtis for <em>The Miracle Planet</em>.
								<p>Billions of years of history have brought us to this moment on this evening.</p>
								<p>At any point along the way, the development of the earth might have been diverted, and followed another course.</p>
								<p>Considering the odds, it's miraculous that the earth did not develop differently, becoming a blazing inferno like Venus, or a frozen desert like Mars.</p>
								<p>Our planet is a world of vast and intricate systems; of astonishing extremes: dense forests and barren deserts; volcanic heat and glacial cold.</p>
								<p>The Earth is teeming with a million forms of life that have helped shape the very nature of our world, from the land under our feet to the air we breath.</p>
								<p>In this series, we'll explore the unique combination of events and forces that have formed our planet and continue to shape it today.</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_85x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td valign="top">This huge crater in the heart of the Arizona desert was caused by a meteorite, or rocky meteor which plunged to earth between 25,000 and 50,000 years ago.
								<p>Today, the momentary flash of meteors across the evening sky is a reminder of that world forming process.</p>
								<p>These travelers through space hold a key to understanding how our earth was formed and what our future may hold.</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_86x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td valign="top">NARRATOR VOICE OVER: In the middle of the Pacific Ocean, the island of Hawaii rises above the clouds; from rocky shores and tropical rain forests to a snowy peak. This is Mauna Kea, the "White Mountain."
								<p>From the dormant volcanic crater on its summit, you can look down on the clouds gathered below, and up toward distant galaxies.</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_1x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td valign="top">At sunset, its almost possible to believe that you can see forever. And in fact, the high altitude of Mauna Kea, and the clean, mid-oceanic air -- do make it possible to view the heavens with particular clarity, as the high clouds dissolve in the evening sky.
								<p>Yet as beautiful as it may seem, the sunset is just the prelude to the real show.</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_2x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td valign="top">Almost every night the great doors at the Mauna Kea Observatory roll open and some of the world's most powerful optical telescopes are turned on the night sky. Tonight the telescope scans the pockmarked surface of the moon.
								<p>As early as 1826, the German astronomer von Gruithuisen proposed that lunar craters were the result of meteorite impacts. But his credibility was undermined because he had previously maintained that other lunar features were built by a race of moon creatures he called "Selenites."</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_3x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td valign="top">Gradually, though, the images from sophisticated telescopes and recent space exploration have led to the wide acceptance of the meteorite impact theory.
								<p>During the last two decades, space probes have brought back evidence of tremendous cratering throughout the solar system.</p>
								<p>Craters were discovered on Mars and two of its moons by NASA's Mariner probes between 1965 and 1971.</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_26x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td valign="top">These 1974 images of Mercury from the Mariner 10 probe show the planet closest to the sun. Like our moon, Mercury is scarred with a staggering array of craters. The Voyager probe revealed a huge crater in Mimas, one of the satellites of Saturn. And on Enceladus, an ice-covered satellite, the frozen surface is marked with thousands of meteorite craters.
								<p>The evidence of so many craters on the moons and planets of our solar system raises a basic question -- where did all these meteorites come from? The effort to find an answer has touched the fundamental theories of how the our solar system came into being.</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_4x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td>No one can say precisely what happened more than four and a half billion years ago, but many scientists now believe our solar system was born when dust and gases began to coalesce into a immense disk.
								<p>The center of the disk grew increasingly dense and bright, giving birth to the sun. Around the sun, other solid bodies were coalescing, forming an incredible number of planetesimals.</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_5x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td valign="top">They contained the raw material of the planets and ranged in size from a grain of sand to much larger protoplanets several miles diameter.
								<p>According to one estimate, there were once more than 100 trillion planetesimals in our solar system. Not surprisingly, they began to collide with each other.</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_16x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td>Sometimes the planetesimals fragmented on impact, but more often they combined to form larger aggregate bodies.
								<p>Over millions of years these young planets continued to grow, impact by impact, to nearly their present size. As each one of the protoplanets grew in mass, the increase in its gravity attracted more of the remaining planetesimals.</p>
								<p>This is how Mercury was formed and Venus, and Mars. This is also how the earth was formed.</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_6x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td>At an early stage, the earth was undoubtedly pockmarked with craters as meteorites rained down on the unprotected surface of the primeval planet.
								<p>The scars of those ancient meteorites can still be seen today on the surface of the moon and other planets. But they have long since vanished from the earth, victims of weathering and the continuing development of our world.</p>
								<p>And yet there is evidence of more recent collisions of large meteorites with the earth.</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_9x.jpg" width="200" height="193"></td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td valign="top">In 1904 the first identification was made of a meteorite crater on earth. It's location is in the Arizona desert. David Moreau Barringer, a mining engineer, found meteorite fragments and sedimentary deposits that eventually led to dating the impact at between 25,000 and 50,000 years ago.
								<p>The Barringer crater allows us to explore the kind of craters we see on the moon, and marvel at the power that could create a giant crater like this in the batting of an eye.</p>
								<p>The meteorite responsible for the Barringer Crater was probably no more than 100 feet in diameter. Yet it left behind a crater three quarters of a mile wide and fully 640 feet deep.</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_7x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td valign="top">Since Barringer's discovery, more than 100 terrestrial meteorite craters have been identified. They occur in all parts of the world, but are particularly impressive in northern Canada.
								<p>This ring-shaped lake is the scar of a meteorite impact long after the birth of our world.</p>
								<p>Across the terrain, the shapes of other craters are still discernable. One of the largest and oldest craters lies on the west side of the Ungava Peninsula in northern Quebec. First identified from aerial photographs by a prospector in 1950, the New Quebec Crater is over two miles wide and 1200 feet deep.</p>
								<p>The antiquity of this crater, together with the action of ice age glaciers, have obliterated all trace of the original meteorite. But it is estimated that a meteorite the size of a football stadium crashed into the earth at 50 times the speed of sound.</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_8x.jpg" width="200" height="150">
								<p><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_21x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></p>
							</td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td valign="top">Even before Apollo 11's historic exploration of the moon, NASA began experimenting with craters and how they are formed.
								<p>Here meteorite impacts are simulated in miniature. The target area is a bed of fine sand. This projectile gun will fire an aluminum bullet into the target at 5 miles per second. That's the equivalent of nearly 18,000 miles per hour.</p>
								<p>By analyzing experiments like this, we can understand the dynamics of how craters are formed.</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_10x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td valign="top">A high speed camera records the impact of the projectile. As the simulated meteorite passes through the air in the chamber, it begins to glow brighter than the sun.
								<p>On impact, this tremendous energy is suddenly released as heat which melts the projectile and the surrounding sand in a violent explosion. The shock wave is so powerful that it propels the sand outward at supersonic speed.</p>
								<p>From the point of impact, sand and molten material rise into the air. In the sand of the laboratory, all traces of the impact can easily smoothed away, but when the impact occurs in solid rock, the evidence can persist for millions of years.</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_12x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></td>
						</tr>
						<tr>
							<td valign="top">This is the Wolf Creek Crater in Australia. It was formed from a relatively recent impact only about a hundred thousand years ago.
								<p>The dramatically eroded Gosse's Bluff Crater of western Australia shows how the weathering process eventually must have worn down the traces of our planet's primeval landscape.</p>
								<p>Sometimes after a powerful impact pushes the floor of the crater down into the earth, it rebounds, raising a peak of rock at the center.</p>
							</td>
							<td valign="top"><img src="../images/miracle_planet/mp1_13x.jpg" width="200" height="150"></td>
						</tr>

								<p>We actually know of two asteroids right now...we've not discovered all the earth-crossing asteroids, but there are two of them that we know...that are about that size, each of which could hit the earth. And in fact there are even bigger asteroids. There's an asteroid called Eros which has about a twenty percent chance of hitting the earth in the next several hundred million years, and this asteroid is about 20 km in average diameter, so it would produce an even bigger effect."</p>
							</td>

								<p>Through countless collisions, they formed the primeval Earth. As they continued to fall, the planet changed dramatically with great oceans covering the surface.</p>
								<p>Later, meteorite collisions may have dramatically affected the development of life on our planet, causing the extinction of some species, and allowing others to succeed.</p>
