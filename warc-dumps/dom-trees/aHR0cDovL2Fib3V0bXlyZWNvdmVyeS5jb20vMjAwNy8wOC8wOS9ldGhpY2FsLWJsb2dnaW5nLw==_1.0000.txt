<p class="alert"><strong>Updated</strong></p><strong>
<p> This entry was set to private after Alan Ocab &amp; I talked in August 20, 2007  because we felt that the issue should die down quietly. (<a href="http://aboutmyrecovery.com/2007/08/20/healthy-apologies-part-2/">Click here for more details</a>) . I decided to bring it back after someone questioned my removal without an explanation. Here it is:</p>
<p> It was not my intention to hurt the boy or his family. I apologized to the father and apologies were accepted. However, I explained the purpose of my entry.  I mentioned that authorship of the entries posted in his blog should be cited clearly and not posted at the bottom of the entry. It wasnât my intention to even mention the name but overzealous bloggers decided to spill it out. </p>
<p> At the time I wrote the post, it was not clear who the authors were of some entries.  It was made to appear that Carl wrote the entries because the guest authors or bloggers were not cited.  I know Carl wrote some of them but not all  and it was not clear.  As of today, the guest authors (the byline) are now clearly shown after the title of the entry.  Hope that clears the matter. </p>
</strong><p><strong>Alan Ocab and I understood each other and that is all that matters.</strong></p>
<p>Responsible bloggers recognize that they are publishing words for everyone to read. Does it follow that they have  certain ethical obligations to their readers, the people they write about, and society in general ? The blogâs greatest strength, particularly  its uncensored and  uncontrolled voice,   is  deemed its greatest weakness. Most bloggers are against anything that constrain their freedom. But most bloggers also know that <i>The blogosphere runs on customs and norms â on what the community feels is acceptable</i>  (<a href="http://www.ojr.org/ojr/stories/050217lasica/">The cost of ethics: Influence peddling in the blogosphere</a>).  I presume most of you know the common sense rules on the <a href="http://www.cyberjournalist.net/news/000215.php">Bloggersâ Code of Ethics</a> . Whatever ethics in real life applies in cyberspace, right?</p>
<p>Now what happens when you face a suspicion that the person who created a blog is misinterpreting the facts? </p>
<p> I donât care if you are a man pretending to be  woman or a prime time woman assuming a young womanâs persona. What infuriates me is when a parent uses their minor child to create a blog and misrepresent some facts!  (This is not the same thing as <a href="http://ceskypooh.wordpress.com/">Cessky Pooh</a> where the dad clearly states that his daughter is the voice of the blog.)  What gets to me is that itâs unfair to the child.  Do I continue to be a spectator of this modern day âThe Emperorâs New Clothesâ?  Some of you just like me willingly share in a collective ignorance of an obvious fact, despite individually recognizing the absurdity. I canât blame you but think harder. </p>
<p>Is this morally right?</p>
<p><span id="more-1179"></span></p>
<p>Should I ignore? </p>
<p><i><a href="http://aboutmyrecovery.com/2007/07/25/dedma-or-dead-malice/">Dedmahan</a>?</i></p>
<p>A few of you know who I am talking about. A few of you raised your suspicions to me .  I could easily turn a blind eye just so I donât rock the boat.  In the end, my conscience took control of my âEmperor New Clothesâ thinking.</p>
<p>I probed  and asked one blogger after he talked with the child and the parent blogger. Guess what he said?</p>
<p><i>The secret of the blog is the parent. The parent taught the child and then the  child  writes . So itâs really the child writing.</i></p>
<p>Huh?</p>
<p>Thatâs absurd.</p>
<p>Itâs still not the childâs blog.</p>
<p>First of all, what kind of values is the parent instilling to the child that fudging the truth is OK?</p>
<p>Secondly, what happens to the child? The child grows up confused and cannot meet peopleâs unrealistic expectations. The truth will come out sooner or later. </p>
<p>What next?</p>
<p>We continue to adhere to ethical blogging standards.</p>
<p>The moral standards which guide us in real life should be the same standards that we observe in cyberspace. There should be no dichotomy. </p>
<p>Therefore, moral values like honesty, adherence to truth and respect for other people (even your minor child) should also be observed in cyberspace.</p>
<p>I cannot condone  blogs like this. There is no fault on the innocent child. Gosh, itâs an insult to my intelligence that some people expect me to believe blindly a questionable circumstance. </p>
<p>I cannot turn a blind eye and ignore an obvious falsehood. This type of blog is not acceptable to the blogosphere.</p>
<p>Pray tell me, would you do this to your own child?</p>
