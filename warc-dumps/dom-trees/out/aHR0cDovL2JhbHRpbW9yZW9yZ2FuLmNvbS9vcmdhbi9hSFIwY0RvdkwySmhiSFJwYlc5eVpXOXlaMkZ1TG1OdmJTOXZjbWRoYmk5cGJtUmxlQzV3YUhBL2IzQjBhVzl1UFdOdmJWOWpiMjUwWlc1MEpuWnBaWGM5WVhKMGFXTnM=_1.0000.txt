<div class="article-content">
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 10pt;"><span style="font-family: &quot;Calibri&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;;">Tuesday, September 14<sup>th</sup> was primary day, and the winners and losers are now either licking their chops or crying in their beer.</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 10pt;"><span style="font-family: &quot;Calibri&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;;">The key races that were the most highly contested and notable for their media attention were the Baltimore County Executive’s race between the winner Kevin Kamenetz and his challenger Joseph Bartenfelder, and the Baltimore City States Attorney’s race, where newcomer Greg Bernstein pulled off a mild upset to defeat Patricia Jessamy. </span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 10pt;"><span style="font-family: &quot;Calibri&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;;">Kamenetz ran a hard-charging campaign that drew considerable commentary from all of us pundits by going “comparative” on his record versus his opponent’s. In most circles, this would be called going negative. Kamenetz dredged up a lot of ancient history by bringing to light some of Bartenfelder’s votes from his days in the legislature over 16 years ago.<span>&nbsp; </span></span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin-bottom: 10pt;"><span style="font-family: &quot;Calibri&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;;">Another factor that contributed largely to Kamenetz’s victory was his huge advantage in raising money and his ability to advertise. Kamenetz went on TV approximately three weeks prior to Bartenfelder, and hired a professional staff to run his campaign. Bartenfelder never hired a PR expert or campaign spokesperson, and when the proverbial shit hit the fan and Kamenetz started to attack him, he was unprepared. He decided to take the high road by not responding with his own counter-tirades against Kamenetz—a&nbsp;type of move that would be considered as negative.

<div class="article-content">
<p style="text-align: center;"><img src="http://baltimoreorgan.com/organ/images/stories/dobbs.JPG" border="0" alt="dobbs" width="213" height="175"></p>
<p class="MsoNormal"><span>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>Navy’s senior quarterback Ricky Dobbs had set lofty goals for himself before the 2010 season opener against state rival Maryland at M&amp;T Stadium on Labor Day.<span>&nbsp; </span>He envisioned himself a legitimate candidate for the prized Heisman Trophy, an award last won by a Midshipman when Roger Staubach was calling signals at the Naval Academy in 1963. He would then emulate Roger’s success in pro football. But Dobbs had an even greater ambition. He predicted he would make a successful run for President of the United   States in 2050.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal"><span>&nbsp; </span>Everyone can dream big, but Dobbs’ turned into a humiliating nightmare after his abysmal performance in a 17-14 loss to the Terps in a nationally-televised game that will best be remembered for major blunders and questionable decisions by both sides, including both Navy boss Ken Niumatalolo and Maryland’s Ralph Friedgen, who was trying to redeem himself in the eyes of new athletic director Kevin Anderson after a 2-10 finish last season.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal"><span>&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>The fact that this was only the second meeting between these close neighbors since 1965 had been blamed on outspoken Maryland fullback/linebacker Lew Fishman, who had twice given the finger to the Brigade of Midshipmen in the 1964 clash that was won spectacularly<span>&nbsp; </span>by Terp Kenny Ambrusko’s late 101-yard kickoff return. Fishman, now a successfully-retired lawyer living in upscale Boca Raton, Fla., explained repeatedly that his actions were prompted by Navy’s snobbish belief that they belonged to a higher institution.</p>

<p class="MsoNormal"><span style="color: black;">A hoop trivia quiz; </span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal"><span style="color: black;">Who holds the NCAA record for highest season scoring average and single game point mark?</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal"><span style="color: black;">(a)Frank Selvy</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal"><span style="color: black;">(b)Johnny O’Brien</span></p>

<p class="MsoNormal"><span style="color: black;">(e)None of the above</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal"><span style="color: black;"><span>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>If your answer was (e), you’re a real “basketball Jones”. The records belong to the tall fellow posing with Pat Conway, a playmate of mine at the </span><span style="color: black;">Bykota</span><span style="color: black;"> </span><span style="color: black;">Senior</span><span style="color: black;"> </span><span style="color: black;">Center</span><span style="color: black;"> in </span><span style="color: black;">Towson</span><span style="color: black;">. The name of the all-but forgotten basketball legend is Bevo Francis, who averaged 48.points a game way back in 1954 and scored 113 against Hillsdale College of Michigan for tiny </span><span style="color: black;">Rio Grande</span><span style="color: black;"> </span><span style="color: black;">College</span><span style="color: black;">, a small </span><span style="color: black;">Ohio</span><span style="color: black;"> school that boasted only 38 male students.</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal"><span style="color: black;"><span>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>And just think how much more mind-boggling these figures would be if the three-point shot was in effect at the time.<span>&nbsp; </span>Marty Blake, longtime scouting director for the National Basketball Association, put things in perspective. “”Bevo was one of the greatest shooters who ever lived,’’ said Blake. “He could not only shoot, but shoot with range.’’</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal"><span style="color: black;"><span>&nbsp; </span>Added former </span><span style="color: black;">Georgetown</span><span style="color: black;"> coach John Thompson, who was never one to heap praise on a non-Hoya, said of Francis on an ESPN Classic retrospective, “He was one of those drum-beat stories.<span>&nbsp; </span>You sit by a tree, you hear people talking about the great legends of the game, then you hear people talk about Bevo, yeah.’’</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal"><span style="color: black;"><span>&nbsp; </span>How </span><span style="color: black;">Conway</span><span style="color: black;"> spent an afternoon reminiscing with Francis is a story in itself.<span>&nbsp; </span>It starts with Pat’s wife, Carol, who attends an annual family gathering held by her cousin, Robert Murphy, in the </span><span style="color: black;">Ohio</span><span style="color: black;"> town of </span><span style="color: black;">Salineville</span><span style="color: black;">.<span>&nbsp;

<div class="article-content">
<p class="MsoNormal">Our Founding Fathers and the framers of the Constitution sure knew their business when they mandated the separation of church and state. For although many of us believe in God and Country, the blending of the two is a volatile and combustible mixture. The latest gasoline on this political fire is the contention that President Obama is a Muslim.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">Constitutionally and theoretically speaking, our president’s religious beliefs should be irrelevant. Practically speaking though it does matter, starting with the simple fact—an almost extinct entity in current day partisan politics—that Barack Obama isn’t and never was a member of the Islam faith.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">Although this latest charge is a pure fabrication, much like the claim from the Birthers, a lunatic fringe group that contends Obama was not born in this country—regardless of his birth certificate—it is a brilliant political jiu-jitsu tactic.<span>&nbsp; </span>Much like the no-win question, “Do you still beat your wife?”, by acknowledging the question the accusation gains some semblance of credence. Remember, it was just two years ago that this same motley crew was gleefully pointing their sanctimonious fingers at Obama’s Christian minister, the Reverend Wright. This latest religious conflagration also takes our collective political eye off the real issues we’re facing.</p>

<div class="article-content">
<p class="MsoNormal"><span>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>Now this may take letting your imagination run wild, but our two major sports franchises may have something in common with the 1939 film classic The Wizard of Oz.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal"><span>&nbsp; </span>First, let’s consider the early success of the Birds’ new skipper Buck Showalter, who we feel justified in calling “The Wizard of O’s’’ after posting a 12-8 record and giving the <span>&nbsp;</span>Texas Rangers fits including a shocking sweep of the Western Division leaders on their home turf.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal"><span>&nbsp; </span>Everyone thought Showalter, who had enjoyed past successes as boss of Texas, Arizona and the New York Yankees, would wait until the Orioles had completed their 13<sup>th</sup> straight losing campaign and evaluate the team from afar before coming on board. A cautious approach was certainly justified. But the man who says he thrives on major challenges surprised everyone by taking over in late July.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal"><span>&nbsp; </span>With one wave of his magic wand, suddenly everything turned positive in Birdland. The youthful starters—Jake Arrieta, Brian Matusz and Brad Bergesen—began to live up to all the hype and were aided by an unexpected surge of offense spearheaded by Luke Scott, Ty Wigginton and a rejuvenated Felix Pie.<span>&nbsp; </span>To further help matters, the defense began making crucial plays while the bullpen, led by a now healthy and seemingly<span>&nbsp; </span>unhittable Koji Uehara, helped win several nail-biters.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal"><span>&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>But before someone tears away the curtain to reveal Showalter, like the Wizard of Oz, simply pushing all the right buttons, it could be time for a reality check. Next month, save for six games with Detroit, the Orioles’ schedule will be exclusively against the four teams ahead of them in the intimidating Al East-New York, Tampa Bay, Boston and the combative Toronto Blue Jays. Even Darth Vader and Harry Potter would cringe fighting these odds.</p>

		<td valign="top"><p><img src="http://baltimoreorgan.com/organ/images/stories/wildbill.JPG" border="0" alt="wildbill" width="180" height="270"></p>
<p class="MsoNormal">On August 2<sup>nd</sup>, 1876, James Butler “Wild Bill” Hickok was shot and killed while playing poker in a saloon in Deadwood,<span>&nbsp; </span>located in the Dakota Territory. Prior to his final stop in Deadwood, he’d been a Union soldier during the Civil War, specifically as a scout and a marksman. After the war he became a stagecoach driver and then a lawman in Nebraska and Kansas. After turning in his badge Hickcok had toured the East with Buffalo Bill and after giving an interview with Harper’s magazine was now famous as a gunslinger – Wild Bill claiming that he had killed at least 100 men. If the number seems preposterous, it should be noted no one argued with Hickok’s claim.</p>
