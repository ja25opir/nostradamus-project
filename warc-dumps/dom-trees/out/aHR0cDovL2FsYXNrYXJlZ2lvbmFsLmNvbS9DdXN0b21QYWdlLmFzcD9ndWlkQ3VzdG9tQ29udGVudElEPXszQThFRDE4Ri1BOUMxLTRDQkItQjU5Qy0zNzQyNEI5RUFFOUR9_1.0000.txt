<h3>GENETIC TESTING</h3>A small percentage of patients who develop breast cancer (5-10%) do so on the basis of an inherited mutation in one of two breast cancer susceptibility genes. A woman can inherit a cancer-causing gene from either her mother or her father. 
<p></p>
<p>Families with an inherited cancer syndrome generally have multiple members who are affected in multiple generations. There are people with bilateral breast cancer and there are family members who are affected by cancer at young ages. Sometimes there are also family members who have had other forms of cancer, particularly ovarian cancer. If you have several close relatives with breast cancer, you should speak with your healthcare provider about genetic counseling. Two genes have been found which can lead to a lifetime risk of acquiring breast cancer that approaches 90%. They are called BRCA1 and BRCA2. Sensitive tests can be done to see whether these mutations are present in a person who has been affected by cancer. The tests can also be done on family members who are not affected. </p>
<p>The information obtained from genetic testing can be useful in many ways. It can allow a person who has had cancer in one breast to be extremely vigilant about screening tests on the other breast. It can also allow a patient to choose to have preventive surgery to reduce cancer risk. Knowledge of genetic testing results can help advise family members who are unaffected by cancer but worried about their chances for developing it in the future. </p>
<p>Just because testing has shown that a specific person has the gene, it cannot be determined when, or even if, cancer will inevitably follow. Before making the decision to have genetic testing, contact your physician and genetic counselor to help you make an informed decision. The possible risks, benefits, and alternatives must be addressed. </p>
