             <h1>"A Christmas Carol" manuscript at the Morgan Library</h1>
           <p class="byline">
                     Thursday November 27, 2008 12:49 PM                              By <span class="name">Emily Hulme</span>
             </p>                                                                                                                   <img src="http://cdn.newsday.com/polopoly_fs/1.817450.1247112816!/image/1820998463.jpg_gen/derivatives/feature_416/1820998463.jpg" class="" height="200" width="416" alt=" ">
   
                                <p class="caption">Photo credit: The Tangent</p>
                                                       
          <p><a href="mailto:ehulme@am-ny.com">By Emily Hulme</a></p><p>• At <a href="http://www.themorgan.org/">The Morgan Library</a>, through Jan. 4.</p><p>John Leech, "Third Visitor or the Ghost of Christmas Present," (detail) original watercolor illustration for Charles Dickens', "A Christmas Carol," first edition, 1843. </p><p>Everyone knows the story of Charles Dickens’ “A Christmas Carol.” Whether it’s from Disney’s cartoon interpretation, Bill Murray in “Scrooged” and other countless movie versions, or a reading of the actual novella, people are familiar with the tale of a haunted miser who finds redemption on Christmas Day.</p><p>Each year around the holidays, The Morgan Library invites the public to come straight to the source with its display of Dickens’ original manuscript, acquired by Pierpont Morgan between 1890-1900. It’s a perennial favorite with museum-goers, and, in fact, the book has been popular since it was first published in 1843.</p><p>“In a week it sold 6,000 copies [the entirety of its first print run],” says Declan Kiely, The Morgan’s curator of literary and historical manuscripts. “It became a sort of instant classic.”</p><p>More after the jump.The story has stuck with us through the ages thanks to its heartwarming message of hope, but Dickens wasn’t motivated solely by the Christmas spirit.</p><p>“He was in financial difficulties,” Kiely says. “He wanted to try to cash in on the Christmas market.”</p><p>A look at the manuscript gives a clue as to how quickly he worked. It’s the sole draft, as Dickens didn’t have time to rewrite a new one to correct his errors. Lines are crossed out on the page, with new text written in above. Almost every other line has a correction.</p><p>“It really reveals the man in full creative flight, as it were, making those very quick but extremely important decisions about plotting and structure and character, line by line,” Kiely says. “You can just see the tremendous speed and effort and the pressure under which he’s writing.”</p><p>Dickens was used to working this way, however. His novels were serialized, with each new section coming monthly. His writing process was adapted,or forged, to deal with constant pressing deadlines. </p><p>While what’s on the pages serves as a glimpse into Dickens’ mind, the physical manuscript itself is a thing of beauty. When he got it back from the printer, he had it bound in red Morocco leather. The cover is stamped in gold with the name of Dickens’ lawyer, Thomas Mitton Esqre, from whom he had borrowed some money. </p><p>“ ‘A Christmas Carol’ itself becomes a Christmas present to his solicitor,” Kiely says. </p><p>Mitton treasured the object until his death. </p><p>The fascinating coda to this chapter in Dickens’ career is that though “A Christmas Carol” was immensely popular, it didn’t immediately make any money for the author. </p><p>“He wanted the book to look really good. And so they printed it to such high standards that he actually lost money,” Kiely says. </p><p>But of course, in the end “A Christmas Carol” has proved to be an enduring success. Dickens’ wrote four more Christmas stories over the course of his career, but none of them resonated with the public like his first.</p><p><strong>SIDEBAR: From the Manuscript</strong></p><p>Each year, the Morgan displays a different page of “A Christmas Carol.” This year, page 37 is on view. According to curator Declan Kiely, here’s what to look for:</p><p>“[On this page] Scrooge meets the ghost of Christmas Present, he’s taken to the Cratchit’s house and we’re introduced to the whole Cratchit family, and to Tiny Tim, of course, who is ... one of the focal points of the reader’s sympathy. The ghost of Christmas Present tells Scrooge, ‘The Child will die.’</p><p>“This is kind of a major revelation at that point. Dickens [changes his mind about it] to develop some tension, and he decides to withhold that information. He strikes it out with a kind of box and puts a line through it. He doesn’t cancel it with a squiggly line [as he does in other instances] which obliterates the text; he boxes it out as a sort of mental note to say, ‘You’re actually going to keep this in, but you’re going to deploy that fact later on.’ </p><p>“It’s not until the very last part of the book, with the Ghost of Christmas future that Dickens tells the reader that Tiny Tim is going to die. It’s much more subtly handled. … We’re not actually told that Tiny Tim has died in the future, he’s just not there. Dickens really generates an enormous amount of sympathy for the Cratchit family by doing that. Whereas if he’d left it in the second chapter, ‘the child will die,’ he’s given that opportunity away.”</p>
