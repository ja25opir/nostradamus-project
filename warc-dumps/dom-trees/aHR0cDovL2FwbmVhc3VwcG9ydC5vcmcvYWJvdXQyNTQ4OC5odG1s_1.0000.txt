		<div class="post-subject"><a href="post-168789.html#168789"><img src="templates/ca_aphrodite/images/icon_post.gif" width="12" height="9" alt="Post" title="Post"></a> &nbsp;</div>

		<div class="post-text">
						First, let me welcome you to the forum and encourage you to ask lots of questions and not to google too much!
<br>

<br>
Second, we need a little more information...Do you have the results of her last sleep study? What are they (the more details the better)?&nbsp;&nbsp;Does she see a pediatric sleep specialist (for us, I prefer a neurologist, since both of my kids have central sleep apnea)? Is her ENT following her sleep? If so, you need a different doc. ENTs are NOT sleep specialists. You will need to find a pulmonologist or a neurologist to fulfill this role. Does she have other health concerns?
<br>

<br>
Third, there are lots of treatment options available for kids with central sleep apnea. In our house, we have one 4 year old who sleeps on C-Pap and a 17 month old daughter who sleeps with oxygen each night.&nbsp;&nbsp;
<br>

<br>
As for the outgrowing thing, we've heard that line a thousand times by unknowledgable doctors.&nbsp;&nbsp;Isaac, my 4 year old, will likely have central sleep apnea all his life and require some sort of breathing assistance when he sleeps at night.&nbsp;&nbsp;Esther Rose, the 17 month old, sleeps with oxygen, and also has central sleep apnea.&nbsp;&nbsp;It is not known yet what the true origin of her apnea is and whether it will resolve itself over time or not, only time will tell..
<br>

<br>
I hope this helps you some, please ask more questions until your curiosity is better satisfied...There are lots of moms here with good insights!
<br>

						A sleep specialist is likely to be a pulmonologist.&nbsp;&nbsp;My son also has central apnea and he's being treated with a more advanced version of the cpap (vpap by resmed).&nbsp;&nbsp;His AHI was &gt;10 and he had pretty significant desats (as low as upper 70's).&nbsp;&nbsp;When his apnea was at its worst he would wake up screaming several times each night likely from going 40+ seconds without taking a breath, which sounds a lot like what you were talking about seeing in your dd....&nbsp;&nbsp;He had both a neuro and pulmo at the time and the neuro deferred to the pulmo.&nbsp;&nbsp;She's the one who continues to follow up and who initially ordered treatment (failed cpap, failed bipap, moved on to vpap which is designed for the purpose of treating central apnea).&nbsp;&nbsp;
<br>

<br>
I guess what I'm wondering about your dd's sleep study report is whether the lowest o2 sat of 86% was a true desaturation or if it was artifact...?&nbsp;&nbsp;
<br>

<br>
If it's been a couple of years since her last sleep study, I actually think it would be worthwhile to take another look just to make sure things aren't getting worse.&nbsp;&nbsp;Can you ask for another sleep study?&nbsp;&nbsp;If she's starting school and has untreated apnea, it could significantly affect how well she does on many levels.<br><br>
