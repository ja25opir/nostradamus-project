			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/29/vote-for-the-7-gastronomic-wonders-of-peru/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Vote for the 7 Gastronomic Wonders of Peru">Vote for the 7 Gastronomic Wonders of Peru</a></h2>
			<h4>January 29th, 2009<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/29/vote-for-the-7-gastronomic-wonders-of-peru/#comments" title="Comment on Vote for the 7 Gastronomic Wonders of Peru">14 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/food/" title="View all posts in Food &amp; Dining" rel="category tag">Food &amp; Dining</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<div class="mceTemp"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/7gastro.png"></a><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/7gastro.png"></a><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/7gastro.png"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-1225" style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="7gastro" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/7gastro.png" alt="" width="300" height="150"></a></div>
<p>Peru has discovered the marketing power of the voter-powered “Best of” list.</p>
<p>The trend began in 2007&nbsp;when&nbsp;a global poll of 100 million voters chose Machu Picchu as&nbsp;one of&nbsp;the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Seven_Wonders_of_the_World" target="_blank">New 7 Wonders of the World</a>.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The prestige and tourism revenues generated by that honor inspired Peru to mount its own contest to name the <a href="http://gosouthamerica.about.com/b/2008/03/27/seven-wonders-of-peru.htm" target="_blank">7 Wonders of Peru</a>. Winning sites include the Inca baths of Cajamarca, Colca Canyon and the Kuelap Fortress in the Amazon. (Given its status as a World Wonder, Machu Picchu was not eligible for nomination.)</p>
<p>Now a group called Acidoungusto Comunicaciones is sponsoring an online poll to determine the Seven Gastronomic Wonders of Peru (<a href="http://www.7maravillasgastronomicas.com/" target="_blank">las 7 maravillas Gastronómicas del Perú</a>).</p>
<p>The first round of voting encompasses&nbsp;an eye-popping&nbsp;list of 100 typical Peruvian dishes, including&nbsp;<em>asado de res</em>, <em>lomo saltado</em> and <em>picante de cuy</em>. No desserts are among the nominees, which means that a contest to determine&nbsp;Las 7 Mejores Postres del Peru is probably slated for 2010.</p>
<p>Log onto <a href="http://www.7maravillasgastronomicas.com/votacion/" target="_blank">Votas por tus 7</a> before February 15 to cast your vote for your seven favorites.</p>
<p>Twenty-one semifinalists will be chosen from the original 100 dishes to compete in the final round of voting, which closes May 15. The seven winning dishes will be announced July 27, the day prior to Peru’s <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independence_Day" target="_blank">Independence Day</a>. (I suspect the judges chose July 27, rather than Independence Day itself, to give chefs and foodies a 24-hour-window to shop for ingredients for a celebratory meal.)</p>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/28/bad-news-for-some-canadian-expats-who-want-to-procreate-abroad/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Bad News for Some Canadian Expats Who Want to Procreate Abroad">Bad News for Some Canadian Expats Who Want to Procreate Abroad</a></h2>
			<h4>January 28th, 2009<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/28/bad-news-for-some-canadian-expats-who-want-to-procreate-abroad/#comments" title="Comment on Bad News for Some Canadian Expats Who Want to Procreate Abroad">3 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/crossing-cultures/" title="View all posts in Crossing Cultures" rel="category tag">Crossing Cultures</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<p>Skimming through <a href="http://expatwomen.blogspot.com/" target="_blank">Expat Women </a>today, I learned that a new citizenship law about to go into effect in Canada will make some <a href="http://expatwomen.blogspot.com/2009/01/calling-all-canadians.html" target="_blank">Canadian children born abroad ineligible for Canadian citizenship</a>.</p>
<p>Expat Women writes:</p>
<blockquote><p>Hi Everyone, If you are a Canadian, especially if you are an adult TCK or you have children who one day will be adult TCKs (Third Culture Kids), <a href="http://www.expatexpert.com/about" target="_blank">Robin Pascoe </a>has asked us to pass this urgent message on to you:</p>
<p>“Did you know that the new Canadian citizenship rules which go into effect April 17 state that:</p>
<p>“…children born in another country after the new law comes into effect will not be Canadian citizens by birth if they were born outside Canada to a Canadian parent who was also born outside Canada to a Canadian parent.”</p>
<p>To read more, and to take action, please visit my <a href="http://www.expatexpert.com/index.php?section=c.blog&amp;page=blog/blog.php" target="_blank">blog post </a>today. Thanks!”</p></blockquote>
<p>The new law doesn’t affect children born to Canadians who were born in Canada themselves. But if either parent was born outside of Canada, their child’s citizenship could be in jeopardy.</p>
<p>&nbsp;Please share the news with any Canadian expats you know in Peru. It can be hard keeping up with the legalities of living abroad.</p>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/27/goodbye-to-john-updike/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Goodbye to John Updike (1932 – 2009)">Goodbye to John Updike (1932 – 2009)</a></h2>
			<h4>January 27th, 2009<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/27/goodbye-to-john-updike/#comments" title="Comment on Goodbye to John Updike (1932 – 2009)">3 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/art/" title="View all posts in Art, Film, Music &amp; Dance" rel="category tag">Art, Film, Music &amp; Dance</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/barb-updike.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-1211" style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="barb-updike" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/barb-updike.jpg" alt="" width="360" height="526"></a></p>
<p>He was one of my favorite novelists, a person who wrote bracingly honest books about being male, being American, being human.</p>
<p>Now El Fotografo walks into my office and tells me John Updike is dead. Of lung cancer. I had no idea he smoked.</p>
<p>The obituaries are appearing now online, and I’ve snagged one below from Reuters.</p>
<p>I feel sad. I thought he had at least another 10 years of writing in him.</p>
<p>Updike became part of my life at around age 20, when I plunged into the Rabbit series for the first time. I was an Updike junkie from the word Go. It was his powers of observation that did it for me, along with his sympathies for ordinary, flawed human beings.</p>
<p>In the mid-1990s I attended a writer’s conference in Key West, where Updike gave a workshop on fiction writing and then a keynote address at a dinner. It was thrilling to be just a few feet from him, this man who sloughed off short stories and novels as easily as a snake sheds its skin (U. famously suffered from psoriasis).</p>
<p>Between the workshop and the evening dinner, I approached him to sign a copy of <em>Rabbit At Rest</em>. I told him how much I liked the Rabbit series and the Harry Angstrong/Rabbit character and he looked up, surprised.</p>
<p>“But you’re a woman,” he said, hinting at reviewers’ recent&nbsp;criticism that Rabbit is crass and sexist (he is). “And you like him?” <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/27/goodbye-to-john-updike/#more-1206" class="more-link">[Read more →]</a></p>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/27/inca-princess-photo-project-celebrates-indigenous-beauty/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Inca Princess Photo Project Celebrates Indigenous Beauty">Inca Princess Photo Project Celebrates Indigenous Beauty</a></h2>
			<h4>January 27th, 2009<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/27/inca-princess-photo-project-celebrates-indigenous-beauty/#comments" title="Comment on Inca Princess Photo Project Celebrates Indigenous Beauty">8 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/art/" title="View all posts in Art, Film, Music &amp; Dance" rel="category tag">Art, Film, Music &amp; Dance</a>, <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/race-matters/" title="View all posts in Race Matters" rel="category tag">Race Matters</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<div id="attachment_1201" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 410px"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/munaywarmikuna-1_jpg.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-1201" style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="munaywarmikuna-1_jpg" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/munaywarmikuna-1_jpg.jpg" alt="" width="400" height="400"></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Beautiful women -- three Inca noblewomen face the sunset in Jimenez Mercado's series "Munay Warmikuna" (Inca Princess Project)</p></div>
<p>A tourism company in Cusco is sponsoring an intriguing photo project to highlight indigenous beauty and women’s growing influence in South America. No prize money or payment is involved, but chosen photographers have their projects featured in a select Web-based gallery.</p>
<p>The <a href="http://www.incaprincess.org/concept.html" target="_blank">Inca Princess Photo Project </a>invites&nbsp;professional photographers to submit ideas for “spectacular open-theme material celebrating the beauty and unique nature of South American women.” The website notes that</p>
<blockquote><p>Although women will be the central subjects, the photographer can choose any techniques and/or settings and themes, including…</p>
<p>- Historical recreations or re-enactments<br>
- Indoor shoots or landscapes<br>
- Nude art, body painting, erotica<br>
- Fashion and advertising</p></blockquote>
<p>Three projects have been produced so far: <a href="http://www.incaprincess.org/primerahistoria/index.htm" target="_blank">First Story of the Queens </a>(photography by Einar Jiménez Troncoso), based on the chronicle drawings of Guaman Poma; <a href="http://www.incaprincess.org/sesions/TheNostalgia/index.htm" target="_blank">The Nostalgia </a>(also by Jiménez Troncoso), a black-and-white series shot in Qoricancha, the Temple of the Sun, that recreates the lament of an Inca noblewoman over&nbsp;the desecration of her culture by conquering Spaniards; and <a href="http://www.incaprincess.org/sesions/MunayWarmikuna/index.htm" target="_blank">Munay Warmikuna</a>/Beautiful women (photography by&nbsp; <a href="http://www.morfijimenez.com/" target="_blank">Morfi Jiménez Mercado</a>), a color series that features young and old women in historical clothing of the Incas, photographed in natural settings. (See photo above).</p>
<p>Some of the photographs in the series are too pretty for my tastes — not surprising, since the project’s director has a background in fashion photography. I prefer edgier, more candid images, although I admit that the sepia colors in the Munay Warmikuna series are ravishing. (Not surprisingly, they were shot with a Hasselblad.)</p>
<p>What I applaud is the focus on the beauty of indigenous women. To participate in the project, models must be 18 year or older and must be “at least half South American Indian in ethnic origin.”&nbsp;That requirement enables both mestizo and native women to take part, and puts the focus on&nbsp;the majority of Peruvian women, who are not Caucasian.&nbsp;The beauty of local women has been overlooked for hundreds of&nbsp;years in this country. You’ll find women of the puna&nbsp;used as icons for the tourism trade but&nbsp;not as paradigms of beauty.</p>
<p>The Inca Princess Project is a step in the right direction for correcting&nbsp;long-standing racist perceptions that women of indigenous origin are unattractive.&nbsp;The women photographed in these series have a proud, haunting beauty that is both modern and eternal.</p>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/26/global-warming-claims-another-peruvian-glacier/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Global Warming Claims Another Peruvian Glacier">Global Warming Claims Another Peruvian Glacier</a></h2>
			<h4>January 26th, 2009<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/26/global-warming-claims-another-peruvian-glacier/#comments" title="Comment on Global Warming Claims Another Peruvian Glacier">1 Comment</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/climate-change/" title="View all posts in Climate Change &amp; Disappearing Glaciers" rel="category tag">Climate Change &amp; Disappearing Glaciers</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<div id="attachment_1190" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 410px"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/nevadoquilca.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-1190 " style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="nevadoquilca" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/nevadoquilca.jpg" alt="" width="400" height="300"></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Bald &amp; ugly: not a patch of ice is left on Nevado Quilca, in Puno, Peru </p></div>
<p>Awful news. <a href="http://enperublog.com/2009/01/23/another-victim-of-global-warming-quilca-glacier-disappears/" target="_blank">Quilca Glacier (5,250 meters above sea level) in Puno has disappeared completely</a>, reports Peru’s National Institute for Natural Resources (IRENA).</p>
<p>The disappearance of Quilca is part of an ongoing recession of tropical glaciers throughout the Andes, where climate change (mainly rising air temperatures) is making it impossible for&nbsp;the ice to regenerate.</p>
<p>Experts such as Marco Zapata, of IRENA, predict that no glaciers will be left in Peru by 2025.</p>
<p>Please do the math. That’s 16 years away.</p>
<p>Why should people care that tropical glaciers are melting in this Andean country? The reasons are both local and global.</p>
<p>First, Peru derives 80% of its water for drinking, agriculture and&nbsp;hydropower from glacier melt-off. A major drought is looming, and no large-scale strategy to find and use other water sources has been implemented.</p>
<p>Uh oh.</p>
<p>“No water, no life,” as the campesinos in Ausangate told me in September ’08.</p>
<p>Secondly, the loss of glacier meltoff&nbsp;will have a devastating effect on the Amazon region, which is fed by streams that originate in the Andes. Two years ago,&nbsp;NASA pinpointed <a href="http://news.mongabay.com/2007/0703-amazon.html" target="_blank">the origin of the Amazon River itself at snow-capped Mount Mismi</a>, in southern Peru, and was predicting major impacts by 2050:</p>
<blockquote><p>Tracing the origin of the Amazon river back to a glacier highlights the vulnerability of the river system to climate change. Glaciers and snowmelt in the Andes are the source for as much as 50 percent of the water in the upper Amazon, yet global warming puts these at risk: the Peruvian government estimates that the country’s glaciers have shrunk by more than 20% in the past 30 years… Further downstream, models show that climate change, combined with deforestation, will leave the Amazon rainforest considerably drier by 2050, further impacting water availability in the river basin.</p></blockquote>
<p>Given that <a href="http://www.blueplanetbiomes.org/amazon.htm" target="_blank">Amazon rainforest produces 20% of the earth’s oxygen</a>, the depletion of its meltwater sources is catastrophic news for everyone on the planet.</p>
<p>Please share this information with anyone you know who (1) drinks water; (2) breathes oxygen; and (3) gives a damn about the people who will be alive in 2025 or 2050.</p>

			<div class="entry">
				<h6 class="mceTemp mceIEcenter">
<dl id="attachment_1181" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 415px;">
<dt class="wp-caption-dt"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/andersoncooperside.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-1181 " style="margin: 10px 15px;" title="andersoncooperside" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/andersoncooperside.jpg" alt="" width="405" height="321"></a></dt>
<dd class="wp-caption-dd">Anderson Cooper wants to inform Americans&nbsp;about pollution at La Oroya, but viewers appear more interested in&nbsp;his hairstyle</dd>
</dl>
</h6>
<p>I learn some surprising&nbsp;things managing this blog.</p>
<p>Like: Many&nbsp;people&nbsp;spend time&nbsp;online researching men’s haircuts.&nbsp;I think these people are mainly&nbsp;guys looking for a new look, although they could be search-engine-savvy hairstylists hoping to replicate certain cuts in their salon.</p>
<p>This blog strayed into Men’s Hairstyles SEO when I posted on <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/06/the-haircut-el-fotografo-gets-sheared/" target="_blank">El Fotografo’s search for the Anderson Cooper haircut </a>in a Lima salon, a quest that resulted in his getting scalped last month. As my post describes, EF secretly decided to go for The Silver Fox look after misinterpreting a remark I made&nbsp;while watching the CNN “<a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/12/16/reaction-to-cnns-planet-in-peril-battle-lines-on-toxic-la-oroya-mines/" target="_blank">Planet in Peril: Battle Lines” segment on La Oroya</a>.</p>
<p>As EF later found out, my secret wish was for him to get his hair cut like Daniel Craig, not like AC.</p>
<p>Fast-forward one month later: El Fotografo’s military cut has finally, finally grown out to resemble the Anderson Cooper ‘do he originally requested, and my blog keeps attracting traffic from people whose Google searches read “I want a Daniel Craig haircut” and “Anderson Cooper + haircut + hot.”</p>
<p>Sigh.</p>
<p>This blog covers serious stuff like <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/26/global-warming-claims-another-peruvian-glacier/" target="_blank">melting glaciers </a>and <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/12/16/reaction-to-cnns-planet-in-peril-battle-lines-on-toxic-la-oroya-mines/" target="_blank">toxic smelting plants </a>but more often than I plan, it veers into the realm of the absurd. It would be easy to blame those digressions on “weird” Peru but that wouldn’t be fair.&nbsp;I am the culprit. I digress because I’m intrigued by how one idea engenders another, and because I’m curious about&nbsp;what prompts&nbsp;people to&nbsp;visit An American in Lima, intentionally or not.</p>
<p>For the sake of the 127 people who visited this blog hoping to take a few grooming tips from Anderson Cooper and Daniel Craig, here are 10 random factoids about AC’s&nbsp;short cut and his grey hair&nbsp;(I’ll post on Craig soon):&nbsp; <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/25/10-facts-about-anderson-coopers-hair-plus-a-super-extra-bonus-grooming-link-for-guys/#more-1179" class="more-link">[Read more →]</a></p>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/24/blogging-fieldwork-peru/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Blogging on Fieldwork in Peru: Melting glaciers, camera-shy bears and nasty stomach viruses">Blogging on Fieldwork in Peru: Melting glaciers, camera-shy bears and nasty stomach viruses</a></h2>
			<h4>January 24th, 2009<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/24/blogging-fieldwork-peru/#comments" title="Comment on Blogging on Fieldwork in Peru: Melting glaciers, camera-shy bears and nasty stomach viruses">2 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/climate-change/" title="View all posts in Climate Change &amp; Disappearing Glaciers" rel="category tag">Climate Change &amp; Disappearing Glaciers</a>, <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/daily-life/" title="View all posts in Daily Life in Lima" rel="category tag">Daily Life in Lima</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<div id="attachment_1176" class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 189px"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/andean-bear.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-1176" title="andean-bear" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/andean-bear.jpg" alt="The rarely seen Andean bear" width="179" height="268"></a><p class="wp-caption-text">The rarely seen Andean bear, whom biologists have tracking in Peru</p></div>
<p>Excuse the radio silence. I’ve been holed up writing grant applications to fund an expedition that El Fotografo and I want to do this year in the dept. of Cusco. Keep your fingers crossed.</p>
<p>Expeditions are not the only projects in Peru that are eligible for funding. Research projects undertaken by&nbsp;investigators in&nbsp;the “hard”&nbsp;and&nbsp;social sciences, as well as the humanities,&nbsp;also attract seed money and, in some cases, hefty grants that can sustain grantees abroad for up to a year or more.&nbsp;A substantial number of these grantees come from the United States, whose major&nbsp;grant-giving entities include the U.S. government (National Science Foundation, National Institutes of Health), corporations and nonprofit&nbsp;foundations.</p>
<p>Foreign researchers and scholars tend to be extremely busy while&nbsp;they’re&nbsp;in Peru, but a few manage to&nbsp;blog from the field or from Internet cafes, mainly for the benefit of colleagues and family back home. These blog entries&nbsp;offer intriguing glimpses into what it’s like to pursue finite goals in a developing country rich with varied eco-systems, indigenous&nbsp;languages and partially excavated ruins, as well as&nbsp;maddeningly slow bureaucracies, hazardous roads&nbsp;and dubious sanitation standards.</p>
<p>I first stumbled onto <a href="http://www.sarahfortner.blogspot.com/" target="_blank">Crampons and Cornfields </a>half a year ago, when I was trolling for news about Peru’s glaciers. Subtitled “Adventures of an environmental geochemist,” the blog is authored&nbsp;by newly minted&nbsp;Ph.D. Sarah Fortner, who describes four years of fieldwork&nbsp;in such far-flung places as <a href="http://sarahfortner.blogspot.com/search?updated-min=2005-01-01T00%3A00%3A00-05%3A00&amp;updated-max=2006-01-01T00%3A00%3A00-05%3A00&amp;max-results=4" target="_blank">McMurdo, Antarctica</a>, Huaraz, Peru, and <a href="http://sarahfortner.blogspot.com/2008/06/yanamarey-glacier-pampas.html" target="_blank">Yanamarey Glacier</a>, contrasted with her&nbsp;coursework in the “cornfields” of Ohio State University. (<a href="http://sarahfortner.blogspot.com/2008/11/thank-you.html" target="_blank">Sarah’s dissertation committee </a>included the eminent climatologists Lonnie Thompson and Bryan Mark, who have spent years documenting glacier recession in southern Peru.)</p>
<div id="attachment_1174" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 209px"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/sarahfinperu.jpg"><img class="size-medium wp-image-1174" title="sarahfinperu" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/sarahfinperu-199x300.jpg" alt="" width="199" height="300"></a><p class="wp-caption-text">A view from the field, from Crampons &amp; Cornfields</p></div>
<p>Sarah writes about a friend discovering that she’s had a<a href="http://sarahfortner.blogspot.com/2008/10/welch-crag.html" target="_blank"> crag named after her</a>&nbsp;in Victoria Island; coping with <a href="http://sarahfortner.blogspot.com/2008/06/day-for-no-pictures.html" target="_blank">intestinal snafoos </a>while doing fieldwork, and being overcome with sadness and rage witnessing environmental contamination by mining operations in Peru. (And you thought scientists cared only about collecting data.)</p>
<p>Here’s a notewothy entry from June 2008 (<a href="http://sarahfortner.blogspot.com/2008/06/day-for-no-pictures.html" target="_blank">A Day for No Pictures</a>), when she reflects on the prior day’s research sampling water from a toxic river below Yanamarey Glacier:</p>
<blockquote><p>I am thinking of the stinging frothy red water we sampled from a llaves pile (mine tailings) before it entered the river. Most devastating, the barren dust-field stretched out in front of it was marked by children’s footprints. A flock of uniformed kids dutifully bounced to school across this parched poisonous land. The source of a pervasive local cough is likely the pumping of this flour into the air.</p>
<p>This day was a mix of emotions with the kids burned into my head sharply contrasting with images from the Andean headwaters we sampled earlier.  <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/24/blogging-fieldwork-peru/#more-1163" class="more-link">[Read more →]</a></p></blockquote>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/20/peru-rules-ok-to-drink-on-the-job/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Peru Rules OK to Drink on the Job">Peru Rules OK to Drink on the Job</a></h2>
			<h4>January 20th, 2009<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/20/peru-rules-ok-to-drink-on-the-job/#comments" title="Comment on Peru Rules OK to Drink on the Job">2 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/food/" title="View all posts in Food &amp; Dining" rel="category tag">Food &amp; Dining</a>, <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/money-econ-poli/" title="View all posts in Money, Economics, Politics" rel="category tag">Money, Economics, Politics</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<p>The Lima-based Reuters crew just filed this <em>tipico</em> Peru story about a strange court ruling.</p>
<p>A judge on Peru’s top court has ruled that companies cannot fire workers for being drunk on the job. The Peruvian government is fuming about the decision, which it says sets a dangerous precedent.</p>
<p>Oy vey! Only in Peru.</p>
<p>Here’s the entire news item:</p>
<blockquote><p>LIMA (Reuters) – Peru’s top court has ruled that workers cannot be fired for being drunk on the job, a decision that was criticized by the government on Wednesday for setting a dangerous precedent.</p>
<p>&nbsp;The Constitutional Tribunal ordered that Pablo Cayo be given his job back as a janitor for the municipality of Chorrillos, which fired him for being intoxicated at work.</p>
<p>&nbsp;The firing was excessive because even though Cayo was drunk, he did not offend or hurt anybody, Fernando Calle, one of the justices, said on Wednesday.</p>
<p>&nbsp;Calle said the court would not revise its decision, despite complaints from the government.</p>
<p>&nbsp;”It’s not a good idea to relax rules at workplaces,” said Labor Minister Jorge Villasante.</p>
<p>&nbsp;Celso Becerra, the administrative chief of Chorrillos, a suburb of Lima, denounced the ruling.</p>
<p>&nbsp;”We’ve fired four workers for showing up drunk, and two of them were drivers,” he said. “How can we allow a drunk to work who might run somebody over?”</p>
<p>&nbsp;(Reporting by Marco Aquino; Writing by Terry Wade; Editing by Dana Ford)</p></blockquote>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/19/peru-plants-half-a-million-trees-a-day-to-combat-climate-change/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Peru Plants Half a Million Trees a Day to Combat Climate Change">Peru Plants Half a Million Trees a Day to Combat Climate Change</a></h2>
			<h4>January 19th, 2009<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/19/peru-plants-half-a-million-trees-a-day-to-combat-climate-change/#comments" title="Comment on Peru Plants Half a Million Trees a Day to Combat Climate Change">1 Comment</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/climate-change/" title="View all posts in Climate Change &amp; Disappearing Glaciers" rel="category tag">Climate Change &amp; Disappearing Glaciers</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/greenforest.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-1159" style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="greenforest" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/greenforest.jpg" alt="" width="400" height="300"></a></p>
<p>Here’s some good news for the environment:</p>
<p>According to ANDINA and other news outlets, Peru’s Ministry of&nbsp;Agriculture has initiated an ambitious&nbsp;<a href="http://ecoworldly.com/2009/01/11/peru-planting-512820-trees-a-day-to-fight-climate-change/" target="_blank">countrywide tree-planting campaign&nbsp;to counter the effects of climate change</a>.</p>
<p>The project&nbsp;began in December with the goal of planting&nbsp;40 million trees&nbsp;by February 20. That translates to half a million trees per day or more than 40 million trees in a little less than&nbsp;three months.</p>
<p>The trees will capture more than 570,000 metric tons of carbon dioxide annually, a representative for the Ministry of Agriculture estimates.</p>
<p>Notes El Comercio:</p>
<blockquote><p>“A workforce of 130,000 people has been recruited, and each person will be planting about 4.5 trees per day. Eucalyptus, pine, cypress and pepper trees will be planted in 18 Peruvian regions with suitable soil and rainfall.”</p></blockquote>
<p>This is wonderful news, not only for the environment, but for employment in Peru.</p>
<p>Perhaps developed nations in the world will be inspired to take a cue from low-income, low-carbon-footprint Peru, and create similar programs of their own.</p>
<p>The time to act is now.</p>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/18/no-surprise-obamas-nix-peruvian-hairless-dog/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to No Surprise: Obamas Nix Peruvian Hairless Dog">No Surprise: Obamas Nix Peruvian Hairless Dog</a></h2>
			<h4>January 18th, 2009<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/18/no-surprise-obamas-nix-peruvian-hairless-dog/#comments" title="Comment on No Surprise: Obamas Nix Peruvian Hairless Dog">7 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/animals-in-peru/" title="View all posts in Animals in Peru" rel="category tag">Animals in Peru</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<div id="attachment_1152" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 410px"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/obamasdoggifkg8.gif"><img class="size-full wp-image-1152  " style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="obamasdoggifkg8" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/obamasdoggifkg8.gif" alt="Image of Obama and Peruvian hairless dog created by Canadian blogger LeDaro (http://ledaroblogspot.com) " width="400" height="299"></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Image of Obama and Peruvian hairless dog created by Canadian blogger LeDaro (http://ledaroblogspot.com) </p></div>
<p>I knew it wouldn’t happen. Everyone in Peru knew it was a long shot. But somehow, when it was officially announced this month that&nbsp;the Obamas were not adopting a Peruvian hairless dog,&nbsp;I felt keenly let down.</p>
<p>No Peruvian in the White House. Rats.</p>
<p>The <a href="http://www.usatoday.com/news/washington/2009-01-11-obama-dog_N.htm" target="_blank">new presidential pooch will be a Labradoodle or a Portuguese water&nbsp;hound</a> sourced from a shelter, Obama’s office says.&nbsp;</p>
<p>That seems a tall order — finding a little-known breed in your corner ASPCA — but it’s inevitable that one will be found, or rather, will be taken from a breeder, inserted in a humane shelter and then handed to Obama at a photo op <em>[note of cynicism in a usually sincere blog].</em></p>
<p>As uncommon as Labradoodles and Portuguese water hounds are, they aren’t all-out rare, like the Peruvian hairless dog. There are so few in the United States that about ten people have contacted&nbsp;me at this blog, asking if I can help them find one. That’s how desperate they are to find a little bald Peruvian.</p>
<p>Anyone who visits <a href="http://americaninlima.com" target="_blank">An American in Lima&nbsp;</a>regularly knows that I am positioned on the sidelines of this Peruvian hairless dog thing, watching with amusement. I don’t know breeders but I do see Peruvian hairless dogs out and about in Lima, which makes me think it wouldn’t be too hard to fly to Lima and buy one here to take back to England or to the States.</p>
<p>Now that the news is in about The Dog Decision, the Peruvian hairless dog’s 15 minutes of fame are over. The dog will go back to being yet another typical oddity on the Peruvian landscape — like frog milkshakes, spitting llamas, men dancing with scissors and entrees of roast guinea pig — beloved by Peruvians and consistently misunderstood by outsiders.</p>
<p>While I’m&nbsp;let down about the Hairless Decision, I’m also relieved for Obama’s sake. It was obvious for political reasons that Obama&nbsp;needed to make a statement relevant to the United States with his choice of a pet, and there was no possibility of him being able to do so with a Peruvian hairless, with is 100% Peruano — pre-Colombian Peruano, at that.</p>
<p>No, better that the Peruvian hairless remain a cult favorite abroad and a symbol of national pride in Peru.</p>
<p>But, for the record, I wasn’t a lone voice&nbsp;in the blogsphere rooting for the Obamas to adopt the Peruvian hairless dog. A <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/15/87-percent-say-yes-to-peruvian-hairless-dog-obama/" target="_blank">poll that ran on American in Lima in November shows that 87% of this blog’s&nbsp;readers favored the pet </a>for the new First Family.</p>
<p>Perhaps in the future, a U.S. citizen of Peruvian origin will make it to the White House, and then it will be time for a <a href="http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/americas/7721784.stm" target="_blank">dog like Ears </a>to curl up on the rug on the Oval Office.</p>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/11/doe-run-poisoning-herculaneum-speaks-out/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Doe Run Still Poisoning U.S. Citizens: A Resident of Herculaneum Speaks Out">Doe Run Still Poisoning U.S. Citizens: A Resident of Herculaneum Speaks Out</a></h2>
			<h4>January 11th, 2009<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/11/doe-run-poisoning-herculaneum-speaks-out/#comments" title="Comment on Doe Run Still Poisoning U.S. Citizens: A Resident of Herculaneum Speaks Out">10 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/money-econ-poli/" title="View all posts in Money, Economics, Politics" rel="category tag">Money, Economics, Politics</a>, <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/perus-andes-mountains/" title="View all posts in Peru's Andes Mountains" rel="category tag">Peru's Andes Mountains</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<div id="attachment_1146" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 344px"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/herculaneum.jpg"><img class="size-medium wp-image-1146" style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="herculaneum" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/herculaneum.jpg" alt="Street sign in Herculaneum, Missouri" width="334" height="381"></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Street sign warning of lead poisoning in Herculaneum, Missouri, site of Doe Run smelter</p></div>
<p>My posts on the <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/12/16/reaction-to-cnns-planet-in-peril-battle-lines-on-toxic-la-oroya-mines/" target="_blank">CNN Planet in Peril: Battle Lines </a>program that spotlighted the Doe Run smelting plant in La Oroya, Peru, have elicited&nbsp;comments from a range of people involved in the catastrophe: a journalist, activists and Peruvians whose families&nbsp;live&nbsp;near the toxic plant.</p>
<p>As American&nbsp;investigative reporter&nbsp;Sara Shipley Hines&nbsp;points out, the CNN special gave the false impression that Doe Run’s sister plant, in Herculaneum, Missouri, has cleaned up its operations so thoroughly that the town is a healthy place to live.&nbsp;That’s not the case, as people&nbsp;familiar with the Missouri plant can testify.&nbsp;Yesterday in the Comments section of on <a href="http://americaninlima.com" target="_blank">An American in Lima,</a> a resident of Herculaneum&nbsp;painted a devastating portrait of the town’s contamined state and what it’s like to grow up breathing air laced with heavy metals.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Rather than paraphrase, I’ll let Sharon speak for herself and for the people of Herculaneum:</p>
<blockquote><p>I am thankful that anyone [CNN]…anyone at all did a story on the lead contamination in Peru. However, I would like to see a story done with the title, ‘The Two Faces of Doe Run’.</p>
<p>You see, Doe Run is a beloved company in the communities that they operate in. This is orchestrated by Doe Run, in order to keep people quiet, smooth them over, and slap a smile on their face. They sponser so many things in the community that people get to thinking about all the ‘goodies’ that would be lost if Doe Run packed up and moved away. I was shocked to find out how many people would defend the company that was contaminating them and their families. It seemed that they were in some sort of denial not unlike something you would see on the ‘Twilight Zone’.</p>
<p>The story that was done with the people in Peru showed much the same sort of thing. Doe Run gives them a school and employs so many of their people, that if the company pulled out, the community would be finacially impacted very heavily.</p>
<p>I live in the Herculaneum, Missouri school district here in the United States where the ‘other’ Doe Run facility is located. They still do not have that site cleaned up to the satisfaction of the EPA. They continue to pollute and we continue to let them.</p>
<p>It has been such an uphill battle to go against Doe Run here, that I feel very sorry for the people of Peru. They may never get any real help.</p>
<p>I went to Herculaneum Schools from 1984-1989. (There is a Junior High and a High School located in close proximity to the lead smelter.) This is the period of time just before Doe Run got concerned enough to build a new smoke stack, and put in the bag house that is supposed to filter out the sulphur dioxide and turn it into sulphuric acid that is a sellable product.</p>
<p>During that time, the contamination was visible in the air, you could taste it in your mouth as you breathed it in, you had to wipe the yellow residue off your car everyday, and people just accepted it as a reality.</p>
<p>There was a yellow cloud that laid like a thick fog in the air choking the environment to the point that gym teachers would not allow class to be held outdoors. When a student, like myself, would run the track around the football field, it tasted like sucking on a book of matches, because the sulphur dioxide was so very thick in the air. What we didn’t know, was that there were other harmful heavy metals that came along with the sulphur, …like arsenic, zinc, nickel, cadmium … <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/11/doe-run-poisoning-herculaneum-speaks-out/#more-1142" class="more-link">[Read more →]</a></p></blockquote>

			<h4>January 8th, 2009<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/08/peruvian-cross-country-skier-sets-sights-on-2010-games-reports-ny-times/#comments" title="Comment on Peruvian Cross-Country Skier Sets Sights on 2010 Games, Reports NY Times">1 Comment</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/sports/" title="View all posts in Sports" rel="category tag">Sports</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<p>The New York Times’ <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2009/01/03/sports/othersports/03olympics.html?_r=1&amp;em" target="_blank">Inge Sheve reports on an underdog contender </a>for the 2010 Winter Olympic Games. He’s 38-year-old Roberto Carcelen, a surfer and runner from Peru, who hopes to become the best cross-country skiier in South America, as well as&nbsp;his country’s first Olympic cross-country skier. (Carcelen trains in Seattle, where he lives with his American wife and their 16-month-old daughter.)</p>
<div id="attachment_1137" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 310px"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/carcelen.jpg"><img class="size-medium wp-image-1137" style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="carcelen" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/carcelen-300x165.jpg" alt="" width="300" height="165"></a><p class="wp-caption-text">photo by Kevin P. Casey, for NY Times</p></div>
<h2>A Skier’s Singular Pursuit</h2>
<p>by Inge Sheve, pub. Jan. 2, 2009</p>
<p>Roberto Carcelen is a runner and a surfer, which does not make him unusual in his home country, Peru, but his newest sport does. He is aiming to be the only Peruvian cross-country skier in the 2010 Olympics.</p>
<div id="articleInline" class="inlineLeft">
<div id="inlineBox">
<div class="image">
<blockquote>
<div class="enlargeThis">The 38-year-old Carcelen hardly fits the stereotype of an Olympic hopeful. He took up his pursuit only three years ago, after learning the sport from his American wife, Kate. Carcelen, who lives and trains in Seattle, is one of only a handful of South American skiers with sights set on the Vancouver Games.</div>
<p>If so, Carcelen’s singular quest will become a little less solitary.</p>
<p>“I read a lot, I talk to people I know in the Seattle racing community and I study the principles of training and specificity through online blogs,” he said. “But this season, I think I will hire a coach.”</p></blockquote>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<blockquote><p>“Cross-country skiing is considered the hardest sport in the world, and that aspect really attracted me,” Carcelen said.</p>
<p>Although Peru has mountains and snow, it has no culture of cross-country skiing. But Carcelen has joined a small subset of skiers from nations like Kenya, Ethiopia, Brazil, Argentina and Chile, as well as not-so-snowy European countries like Portugal and Ireland, creating a separate competition within international races.</p>
<p>“I want to become the best cross-country skier in South America,” he said. “I can’t come close to the Norwegians and the Italians, but I can become the top of the developing nations.” <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/08/peruvian-cross-country-skier-sets-sights-on-2010-games-reports-ny-times/#more-1135" class="more-link">[Read more →]</a></p></blockquote>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/08/surquillo-market-no-1/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Surquillo Market No. 1">Surquillo Market No. 1</a></h2>
			<h4>January 8th, 2009<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/08/surquillo-market-no-1/#comments" title="Comment on Surquillo Market No. 1">6 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/food/" title="View all posts in Food &amp; Dining" rel="category tag">Food &amp; Dining</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/12/surquillo4.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-1008" style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/12/surquillo4.jpg" alt="" width="400" height="379"></a></p>
<p>The photo above was taken in Surquillo Market No. 1, in Miraflores. The vendor is arranging her eggplants and tomatoes as the day winds down.</p>
<p>Peruvians prefer to prepare their meals from fresh fruits and vegetables, rather than buy canned, frozen or processed foods.</p>
<p>Doing so it more economical and tasty, Peruvians agree. As an added bonus, preparing meals from scratch is healthier.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Eating healthier foods enables many Peruvians to stay fitter than their U.S. counterparts.</p>
<p>According to the World Health Organization, obesity in developed and developing nations is soaring, notes <a href="http://www.acap-peru.org/newsletter/2007-03/fat-countries.html" target="_blank">ACAP.</a> An astonishing 74.1% of U.S. citizens over age 15 are classified as overweight. The United States ranks #9 among fattest nations (Nauru is #1, with 94.5% of its population considered overweight).</p>
<p>Where does Peru rank? At #46, with slightly fewer overweight people than Canada (60%).</p>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/06/the-haircut-el-fotografo-gets-sheared/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to The Haircut (El Fotografo gets sheared)">The Haircut (El Fotografo gets sheared)</a></h2>
			<h4>January 6th, 2009<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/06/the-haircut-el-fotografo-gets-sheared/#comments" title="Comment on The Haircut (El Fotografo gets sheared)">3 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/crossing-cultures/" title="View all posts in Crossing Cultures" rel="category tag">Crossing Cultures</a>, <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/daily-life/" title="View all posts in Daily Life in Lima" rel="category tag">Daily Life in Lima</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<p>El Fotógrafo has an erratic relationship with his hair. He goes for weeks&nbsp;without doing much of anything to it, letting it sprout in extravagent clumps,&nbsp;and then one day, he will be struck by a severe urge to cut his hair Now.</p>
<p>This can be hazardous because EF has a cowlick. It’s not something a person can trim on his own, but EF doesn’t see it that way. I’ve found him in the bathroom with a pair of not-very-sharp scissors hacking at his temples, silver and black hairs falling into the sink.</p>
<p>He’ll step back and admire his handiwork in the mirror, raking his fingers through the cropped cowlick. He doesn’t take a mirror to the choppy back.&nbsp;</p>
<p>I will mention something about the unevenness,&nbsp;and he will give me an annoyed look.</p>
<p>Later&nbsp;at that day, or perhaps the next, he will disappear for several hours, giving himself over to a professional who will even things out. Taming&nbsp;the Wild Tuffet,&nbsp;I call it.</p>
<p>I didn’t pay attention to EF’s hair as we got ready to leave for vacation last month. But something I said in relation to the CNN <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/12/16/reaction-to-cnns-planet-in-peril-battle-lines-on-toxic-la-oroya-mines/" target="_blank">“Planet in Peril: Battle Lines” program </a>must have activated EF’s grooming gene because he pulled the disappearing act the day before we flew to Miami.&nbsp;</p>
<p>At 7 p.m.&nbsp;he&nbsp;returned to the house looking like a Marines recruit, sheared to within 1 millimeter of his scalp.</p>
<p>What happened? I screamed.</p>
<p>“I told the guy at the salon to cut it like that guy on CNN.”</p>
<p>” ‘That guy on CNN’?” It took&nbsp;a few seconds to sink in. “Oh, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anderson_Cooper" target="_blank">Anderson Cooper</a>. Why would you want your hair cut like Anderson Cooper?”</p>
<p>“Why not?” EF said defiantly.<a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/andersoncooper.jpg"><img class="alignleft size-full wp-image-1122" style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="andersoncooper" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2009/01/andersoncooper.jpg" alt="" width="121" height="150"></a></p>
<p>Then I remembered. While watching the segment on La Oroya, I had mentioned that I had thought Anderson Cooper was cute. “Like a bunny,” I had said, meaning that Anderson Cooper was adorable in a well-seasoned, nonthreatening way (nonthreatening for EF) but EF hadn’t understood. He thought I meant Anderson Cooper was hot. <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2009/01/06/the-haircut-el-fotografo-gets-sheared/#more-1117" class="more-link">[Read more →]</a></p>
