      <h2><a name="top"></a>Biomed Certification...New Chairman Weighs in on New Requirements, Future of Program</h2>
      <p><em><img src="../../../art/anews/2010.sep/nowak.jpg" alt="Chris Nowak" width="150" height="180" hspace="10" border="1" align="right">Much like the profession itself, biomed certification is changing. Recent changes include the addition of exam questions on information technology (IT) and new requirements for military biomeds. Sharing his insight on these changes, Chris Nowak spoke with </em>AAMI News<em>. In June, Nowak became the chair of the U.S. BMET Board of Examiners, which develops and administers certification exams three times a year: certified biomedical equipment technician (CBET), certified radiology equipment specialist (CRES), and certified laboratory equipment specialist (CLES). </em></p>
      <p><strong>How do you feel about your new leadership role? </strong></p>
      <p>It is a very exciting opportunity, especially as our career continues to transition. We are getting heavily into the computer and networking architecture. To ensure that our certification program remains relevant, it is important that the chairman continues to direct the board in the appropriate direction. </p>
      <p><strong>What are your main goals as chairman?</strong></p>
      <p>There are many things we are looking at, including ensuring the exam covers enough computer and networking technology questions to meet the evolving focus of our profession. The U.S. BMET Board of Examiners will be evaluating and participating with the International Certification Commission (ICC) and U.S. Certification Commission (USCC) regarding the opportunity to create a new certification for biomedical technology engineering managers.</p>
      <p><strong>There are new eligibility requirements for military personnel. What are they, and what prompted them?</strong></p>
      <p>In the past, the eligibility requirements for military personnel to take the certification exams were significantly different than civilians. The military was required to gain a civilian associate’s degree or have time in the field. It created a disparity of several years before military personnel could be certified in our field, versus a civilian.</p>
      <p>As time progressed, it became increasingly apparent that the military program was really providing candidates with a lot of education and hands-on opportunities. Over the last 18 to 24 months, the certification board of examiners did a lot of research. We called numerous institutions around the country to see exactly what their programs offered as far as educational credits and contact hours.</p>
      <p>We found that the military program was very extensive. We presented this information to the U.S. Certification Commission, which approved an equivalency this June that if you go through the U.S. military biomed program, you are treated as if you earned an associate’s degree. The updated eligibility requirements will be listed in the 2011 Handbook for Applicants under Full Certification as follows: certification of completion of a military program and two years of full-time BMET work experience.</p>
      <p><strong>What do you see ahead for certification?</strong></p>
      <p>Certification, in general, I think will become more relevant as states continue to look at who is servicing medical equipment technology, and look at opportunities to validate those individuals who work on life-saving medical equipment in their state. I don’t think it will come from a national position, but I think it will be more state driven. The Texas Legislature, for example, considered a bill last year that would mandate qualifications for biomeds. </p>
      <p><strong>Do you see certification becoming mandatory in the future?</strong></p>
      <p>I would hope that more and more individuals in our career field seek out certification for their own professional interest, rather than have the government force us to straighten out our field or legitimize our field. I would hope that career professionals would seek out certification regardless of government intervention. </p>
      <p class="text"><em>AAMI News</em>: September 2010, Vol. 45, No. 9</p>
