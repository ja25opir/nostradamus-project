        <p align="center" style="margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 0"><font size="6"><span style="line-height: 100%; font-weight: bold; language: EN">On
        the Issue of Creeds</span></font></p>
        <p align="center" style="margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 0">&nbsp;</p>
        <p align="center" style="margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 0"><font size="4"><span style="line-height: 100%; font-weight: bold; language: EN">Charles

        <p align="center" style="margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 0"><font size="4"><i><span style="line-height: 100%; language: EN">Alexandria,

        <p align="center" style="margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 0">&nbsp;</p>

      <td width="19%"></td>
      <td width="81%" bgcolor="#ffffff">
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-indent: 0; line-height: 100%; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5; margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 0" align="center"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">“All things change, creeds and philosophies
        and outward <o:p>
        </o:p>
        systems—but God remains.”<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-indent: 0; line-height: 100%; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5; margin-top: 0; margin-bottom: 0" align="center"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">—</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Mary Augusta Ward<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">&nbsp;
        </span>(1851-1920) </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">in </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Robert Elsmere</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">, Bk 4, Chp 26.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoSubtitle" style="text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
font-weight:bold;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Introduction<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoSubtitle" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Growing up Catholic, I was exposed
        to creeds and sundry dogma from an early age. It seemed comforting at
        the time to know that learned people in the Church had worked everything
        out for me, and all I had to do was pay attention in catechism class.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoSubtitle" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">When I became an adult, however,
        this system did not seem quite as appealing as it once had. Following
        the example of the Apostle Paul (“When I was a child, I spoke like a
        child, I thought like a child, I reasoned like a child; when I became a
        man, I gave up childish ways”—1 Corinthians 13:11), I looked
        elsewhere.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoSubtitle" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">I then found a group that claimed
        it operated under maxims such as “what’s true is what’s true for
        you” (meaning actually that one must examine what “authorities”
        say and avoid blind acceptance) and “reality is what we agree it
        is;” this was all within certain limits, however, as the group did
        have a loose, stated creed. Even though taking on the responsibility
        necessary to operate like this might be difficult, it should have led to
        an exciting time of exploration and freedom. Unfortunately, this group
        did not practice what it preached and in actuality insisted that one’s
        examination should conclude that their Founder’s way is the only way;
        thus, there could be no real independent thinking. The situation was
        dogmatic to such an extent that it made my Catholic Church experience
        seem very liberal by comparison, so I went searching again.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoSubtitle" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">This is when I found Unitarian
        Christianity. What I read really made sense to me, and it was quite
        freeing, but there was this issue of creeds, or perhaps more clearly
        stated, the lack of a creed. It confused me, and I was even a little put
        off by it. I mean, how can you have a church (even in the broadest sense
        of the word) without even a loose creed? Was I missing something?
        Instead of just moving on to another religion or philosophy or ignoring
        the issue, I did some study on the subject. What I found follows.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoSubtitle" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">&nbsp;<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-weight:bold;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Freedom<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;
mso-ansi-language:EN">The Unitarian objection to creeds seems to be in part an
        objection to a resulting loss of freedom when a person or (especially) a
        group strongly adheres or demands adherence to them. A few points
        relating to freedom will now be examined before going to the main topic.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyText" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Freedom is normally considered to
        be a good thing, but when freedom becomes license, it is a bad thing.
        License in this context is lack of due restraint, excessive freedom or
        heedlessness for the precepts of proper behavior, even to the point of
        licentiousness (see the </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Houghton Mifflin Dictionary</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">). A useful analogy here might be: freedom is to license as growth is to
        decadence.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;
mso-ansi-language:EN">Another point regarding freedom can be seen by considering
        that the power to do something does not necessarily bring with it the
        right to do it. That is, one may have the power (as in ability/strength)
        to do something without necessarily having the right (as in action
        having no legitimate, adverse consequences for the actor) to do it.
        Thus, just because one has the freedom (the power) to do something does
        not mean that one should do it, because if the one lacks the right, then
        there will be bad consequences for him.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">It is also useful to consider a spectrum based
        on amounts of freedom. One could say that too much freedom is chaos, a
        little </span><span style="language:EN"><o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;
mso-ansi-language:EN">less is revolution, much less is evolution, and none is
        stagnation. An ideal situation would seem to lie somewhere on the
        segment from revolution to evolution with the exact point depending on
        the particular situation, maybe even personal choice.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;
mso-ansi-language:EN">The spectrum spoken of here might actually be better
        thought of as a circle, given that in situations that are otherwise
        similar, the consequences where there is too much freedom are very often
        identical to the consequences where there is too little freedom. For
        example, it can be said that either too much or too little freedom
        within a group eventually leads to schism. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;
mso-ansi-language:EN">As an illustration of this, first consider that the
        Protestant Reformation resulted in part from too little freedom existing
        within the Catholic Church. This schism continued. The Unitarian
        Movement could be said to have arisen due, at least in part, to too
        little freedom among Calvinists. Later, it could be said that too much
        freedom amongst Unitarian Universalists (the successors so to speak of
        the earlier Unitarian Movement) lead to the formation of the American
        Unitarian Conference. This is not to say that schisms are necessarily
        bad or good, but rather that they can happen on either end of the
        freedom spectrum or where its ends join into a circle.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;
mso-ansi-language:EN">Some other relevant points or statements regarding freedom
        are as follows. Mankind hates chains, but nature abhors a vacuum. God
        may have given mankind individual free will, but there seems to be
        unhappy consequences for bad choices, at least in the long run, so there
        is some control mechanism to keep freedom in some sense within limits or
        rules. Mankind dislikes, even fears (excessive) control, yet will
        usually seek guidance from a higher power or authority when things get
        bad enough; guidance is not (necessarily) the same thing as an
        (arbitrary or capricious) authoritarian pronouncement, however. With
        freedom comes responsibility, or there is really only license. The
        Golden Mean comes to mind: moderation in all things.</span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">I’ll close out this section with
        a quote by Robert Ross from page 107 of the September 2004 issue of </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The American Unitarian</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">: “[Freedom] is to be wielded, intelligently, to achieve a goal. Mark
        Twain once wrote, as I recall, that a man who has a hammer is constantly
        looking for a nail. Freedom can be like that.”<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;
mso-ansi-language:EN">&nbsp;<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-weight:bold;
mso-font-kerning:.5pt;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Creeds</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"><o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;
mso-ansi-language:EN">Now on to the main topic, the issue of creeds, or maybe
        more properly, the issue</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">s</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"> of creeds. First, some useful definitions (from the </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Houghton Mifflin Dictionary</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">):<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:
italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Christianity:</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"> The Christian religion, founded on the life and teachings of Jesus.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:
italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Church:</span><span style="font-size:
11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"> The
        company of all Christians [or co-believers] regarded as a spiritual
        body.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:
italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Confession:</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"> An avowal of belief in the doctrines of a particular faith; a creed.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:
italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Creed:</span><span style="font-size:
11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"> A formal
        statement of religious belief; a confession of faith; a system of
        belief, principles, or opinions.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: 6.0in; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Dogma:</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"> A doctrine or a corpus of doctrines relating to matters such as morality
        and faith, set forth in an authoritative manner by a church; an
        authoritative principle, belief, or statement of ideas or opinion,
        especially one considered to be absolutely true.</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"><o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: 6.0in; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Group:</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"> A number of individuals or things considered together because of
        similarities.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: 6.0in; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Liberal:</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"> Not limited to or by established, traditional, orthodox, or authoritarian
        attitudes, views, or dogmas.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: 6.0in; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Orthodox</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">: Adhering to the accepted or traditional and established faith, especially
        in religion. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The Bible seems like a good place to start
        when considering creeds. Some relevant words on the subject by the
        Apostle Paul </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">are:<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Not that we are competent of
        ourselves to claim anything as coming from us; our competence is from
        God, who has made us competent to be ministers of a new covenant, not in
        a written code but in the Spirit; for the written code kills, but the
        Spirit gives life.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">&nbsp; </span></span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">2 Corinthians 3:5-6</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"> (RSV).<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">But now we are discharged from the
        law, dead to that which held us captive, so that we serve not under the
        old written code but in the new life of the Spirit. What then shall we
        say? That the law is sin? By no means! </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Romans
        7:6-7</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"> (RSV).<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyText" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Paul appears to
        have been at least very suspicious of, but maybe not totally against,
        written religious codes or laws. However, following the Spirit seems to
        have been more important in his view. Perhaps the problem Paul had with
        the law was the rigidity of it as had existed up to that time.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyText" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Beginning with
        the early Christians, various creeds have been proposed to define
        Christianity, and creedal controversies have frequently resulted, with
        some of these controversies surviving today, even if no longer “front
        page news.” Given the great complexity of these historical events, the
        fact that their details are not of great importance to the discussion
        here and that most readers are probably very familiar with them anyway,
        little more will be said directly on this subject. Readers who are
        unfamiliar with this history may wish to study a sampling, including the
        controversies over Arianism, the filioque and predestination.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">There has been much written on the
        subject of creeds in literature. A perusal of </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Bartlett’s Familiar Quotations</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"> under creeds yielded the following:<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Great God! I'd rather be a Pagan
        suckled in a creed outworn, so might I, standing on this pleasant lea,
        have glimpses that would make me less forlorn; have sight of Proteus
        rising from the sea, or hear old Triton blow his wreathed horn. </span><span style="font-size:
11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-weight:bold;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">William Wordsworth</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"> (1770-1850) from </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Miscellaneous Sonnets. Part i. xxxiii.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Shall I ask the brave soldier who
        fights by my side in the cause of mankind, if our creeds agree? </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-weight:bold;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Thomas Moore</span><span style="font-size:
11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">
        (1779-1852) from </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Come, Send Round
        the Wine.</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"> <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">There lives more faith in honest
        doubt, believe me, than in half the creeds. </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-weight:bold;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">Alfred Tennyson</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"> (1809-1892</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">) </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">from </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">In Memoriam xcvi. Stanza 3.</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"> <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Ah! What if some unshamed
        iconoclast crumbling old fetish raiments of the past, rises from dead
        cerements the Christ at last? What if men take to following where He
        leads, weary of mumbling Athanasian creeds? </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;font-weight:bold;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Roden Noel</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"> (1834-1894) from </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The Red Flag.</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"> <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Men have dulled their eyes with
        sin, and dimmed the light of heaven with doubt</span><span style="font-size:
11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">,</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"> and built their temple-walls to shut thee in,</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"> and framed their iron
        creeds to shut thee out.</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"> </span><span style="font-size:
11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-weight:bold;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">Henry van Dyke</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"> (1852-1933) from </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">God of the Open Air.</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"> <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent2" style="text-indent: 0; tab-stops: 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">Wordsworth shows an appreciation for the magical side, or at least the
        comforting aspect, of a creed, even an outmoded one, while Moore writes
        on the folly of deciding on whether or not to associate with a person
        based on similarity of creed. Tennyson and Noel seem to offer words of
        praise for being rational regarding ones beliefs and are less
        sympathetic to the opposite approach. Ward speaks of changing
        understanding or beliefs over time regarding an immutable God. Van Dyke
        writes on mankind’s corruption and the use of creeds in perpetuating
        it. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyTextIndent2" style="text-indent: 0; tab-stops: 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">The thoughts expressed by these authors seem for the most part to show
        hostility to creeds. But, maybe the misuse of creeds was the real issue
        for them.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">As an interesting side note, Mary Augusta Ward
        had at least some interest in Unitarianism in her lifetime. The book
        from </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">which her quote comes concerned
        the conversion of an Anglican to something akin to Unitarianism. The
        full story is given in the article on Ward in the </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">Dictionary of UU Biography</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"> available on the web. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Of course, many great Unitarians
        of the past wrote on the subject of creeds. A notable example of such
        writing is William Channing’s </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">A Letter on
        Creeds. </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">It is clear from this letter that Channing was
        no great fan of creeds:</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"><o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">My aversion to human creeds as
        bonds of Christian union, as conditions of Christian fellowship, as
        means of fastening chains on men's minds, constantly gains strength.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">***<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">My first objection to them is,
        that they separate us from Jesus Christ…. This is what shocks me in
        the creed-maker. He interposes himself between me and my Saviour.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">***<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Christian truth is infinite. Who
        can think of shutting it up in a few lines of an abstract creed?… It
        is a spirit rather than a rigid doctrine…. It cannot be reduced to a
        system…. It is to be felt rather than described. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyText" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">***<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">From the infinity of Christian
        truth, of which I have spoken, it follows that our views of it must
        always be very imperfect, and ought to be continually enlarged.… Need
        I say how hostile to this growth is a fixed creed, beyond which we must
        never wander? <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">***<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">It is another and great objection
        to creeds that, wherever they acquire authority, they interfere with
        that simplicity and godly sincerity on which the efficacy of religious
        teaching very much depends.… The minister must seek words which will
        not clash with the consecrated articles of his church.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyText" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">***<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyText" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Another sad
        effect of creeds is, that they favor unbelief. It is not the object of a
        creed to express the simple truths of our religion, though in these its
        efficiency chiefly lies, but to embody and decree those mysteries about
        which Christians have been contending. I use the word
        "mysteries," not in the Scriptural but popular sense, as
        meaning doctrines which give a shock to the reason and seem to
        contradict some acknowledged truth. Such mysteries are the staples of
        creeds. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyText" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Channing’s
        words demonstrate his general aversion to creeds, but would he have been
        against a personal creed that evolved as a person’s understanding
        increased? Or one for a group that involved no coercion, contained only
        the most basic and commonly shared beliefs of the group, served merely
        as a statement of what united them and was meant to evolve over time?
        Were creeds or the misuse of creeds really the issue for Channing?
        Perhaps he saw misuse as an inevitable consequence of creeds.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">&nbsp;
        </span><o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyText" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Some years after
        Channing, another Unitarian, Frederic Henry Hedge wrote </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Reason in Religion </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">(1865). In the passage that follows from page 408 of this work, Hedge makes
        a point about revelation and dogma. For him it seems, revelation is for
        the spiritual uplifting of mankind as opposed to simply establishing
        dogmas which he saw as never more than imperfect reflections of the
        principles revealed: <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyText" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The purpose of
        revelation is not to settle speculative questions depending on the nice
        interpretation of words, but to infuse a new spirit into human things,
        to illustrate great principles of practical import with new sanctions.
        The principles are eternal; the dogmas in which they are embodied are
        limited and transient.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">At about the time that Hedge wrote this last
        passage, the Unitarian minister, James Martineau was active in England.
        According to the </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Dictionary of UU
        Biography, </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Martineau believed in the importance of the
        church and insisted that it must </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">be more than an
        association for free inquiry. He believed that the primary purpose of
        the church is worship, and a common bond or a consensus of purpose in
        worship must exist among the members, or individualism will promote
        anarchy. Martineau did not preach doctrine, but delivered pastoral
        sermons aimed at bringing people in closer relation with God. Advocacy
        for social reform and the like was handled in forums separate from his
        preaching.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyText" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Based on this
        assessment, Martineau was not in favor of creeds, yet saw a need for
        consensus when it came to worship—a practical, middle of the road
        approach it seems. Perhaps Martineau was attempting to stem the tide
        that, at least in the United States, eventually led to the current
        chaotic situation in UU churches.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The last great Unitarian from
        the past that we will hear from will be James Freeman Clarke. In his </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Manual of Unitarian Belief</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"><span style="mso-spacerun:yes">&nbsp; </span>(1884), Clarke offers what
        might be characterized as one of the milder views on creeds and related
        subjects that we have seen so far.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Preface<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">As Unitarians have no creed
        … no one among them has any right to define the views of others.&nbsp;&nbsp;A
        Manual such as this is meant simply to express what, in the opinion of
        the writer, is the general belief of the majority of Unitarians.&nbsp;…&nbsp;No
        one is bound by it; and it does not attempt to limit thought, but rather
        to stimulate and rouse inquiry.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">***<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">§ 54.&nbsp;A creed (from the
        Latin "credo," I believe) is simply a belief.&nbsp;In this
        sense creeds are good, useful, and desirable for individuals.&nbsp;If
        two or three who hold the same belief unite to convince others of its
        truth, this also is natural and right.&nbsp;If they state their creed in
        propositions and articles, this also may be useful.&nbsp;To such creeds
        Unitarians do not object.&nbsp;Many of their churches have adopted such
        statements of opinion.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"><span style="mso-spacerun:yes">&nbsp;</span>§
        55.&nbsp;But Unitarians object to religious creeds under the following
        circumstances: 1.&nbsp;When they are made a test of character; 2.&nbsp;When
        they are made a condition of fellowship; 3.&nbsp;When they become an
        obstacle to progress.&nbsp;&nbsp; Most of the creeds of the Christian
        Church have been liable to these objections.&nbsp;&nbsp;… They have
        been an obstacle to progress, imposing the opinions of past centuries
        upon present belief. …<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">§ 56.&nbsp;Liberal
        Christianity, or freedom in religion, does not mean liberty to believe
        what we choose, but freedom to seek the truth anywhere, everywhere, and
        always. …<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">§ 57.&nbsp;Rational
        Christianity does not mean that we are to reject all beliefs which we do
        not now see to be reasonable, or to make reason the only source of
        truth.&nbsp;&nbsp;But it means that we are to test every belief by the
        light of our reason, and seek to understand clearly what we think and
        why we think it.&nbsp;&nbsp;<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">In a way, it is odd that
        Clarke would write such a manual given that Unitarians maintain that at
        least as a group that they are creedless. Perhaps this is why he took
        such pains with in the Preface. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Clarke goes on to distinguish
        “good” creeds from “bad” creeds in the excerpt presented here.
        He is in agreement with Channing as to the pitfalls or misuse of creeds,
        but does admit that there are some instances where creeds are even
        desirable. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">It seems that Clarke is right
        on this latter point. If nothing else, a Liberal/Rational Christian, a
        person who places a great deal of import on applying reason in the realm
        of religion, would seem to find it useful to catalog where he or she
        stands on major points, at least at any particular moment. A creed might
        provide a good summary. A manual like Clarke’s might provide a
        detailed account or a basis of comparison for others.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Clarke’s comments on freedom and reason are
        interesting.</span><span style="language: EN"> </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">His view expressed here is
        that there are or should be limits to freedom, and that reason is not
        the be-all and end-all, but one tool to get at the truth. (See the
        discussion on freedom given above.)<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">I find Clarke’s view to be
        well balanced and constructive. On a personal note, his </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Manual</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"> was one of the
        first Unitarian works that I ever read, and it impressed me greatly. It
        was a main reason that I chose to join the American Unitarian Conference
        and to write this article.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">In the interim from
        Clarke’s time to our own, Unitarian groups for the most part embraced
        secular humanism and related philosophies. We now have the Unitarian
        Universalist Association (UUA) and its (at least stated) all-inclusive,
        “big tent” philosophy. It is instructive to see how creeds are
        viewed in a UUA context.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">In her </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Meditation On The Unitarian Universalist
        Principles</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">, which was recently on the UUA website,
        Beverley Boke writes: <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Historically, both the
        Unitarians and Universalists developed various statements describing
        their beliefs—and were careful to separate these from requirements for
        membership in a congregation. The Principles and Purposes are in the
        Bylaws of the UUA (Article II), a document that can evolve and change as
        the movement evolves. The process for developing and changing them
        includes intense discussion in local congregations and at General
        Assembly over several years. The Principles and Purposes (or "the
        Principles"), adopted in 1985, are ideals toward which we work,
        rather than requirements for membership; guidelines for religious and
        ethical living; and sources of inspiration for religious education,
        worship, and social action.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">“The Principles” to which
        she refers, or the “7 Principles” as they are also called, can be
        found on the UUA website and are:<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">1. The inherent worth and
        dignity of every person.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">2. Justice, equity and
        compassion in human relations. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">3. Acceptance of one another
        and encouragement to spiritual growth in our congregations. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">4. A free and responsible
        search for truth and meaning. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">5. The right of conscience
        and the use of the democratic process within our congregations and in
        society at large. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">6. The goal of world
        community with peace, liberty, and justice for all. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">7. Respect for the
        interdependent web of all existence of which we are a part. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-weight:normal;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">Most fair-minded people would probably not have a problem with any of these
        statements. They do not appear to form a creed, at least not in the
        classical sense, but this is probably as close to a creed as the UUA can
        get, given their diverse membership (other than a “creed” of
        left-wing politics as some might contend). In any case, as Ms. Boke’s
        explains, these statements are not part of a requirement for membership
        in the UUA. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Ms. Boke’s cautionary
        comments do appear a bit overdone after one actually reads the
        principles themselves; they are not exactly daring or controversial.
        Going even further, Larry Reyka stated in an article recently out on the
        web entitled, </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Why I Am Not a Unitarian Universalist</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">:<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyText" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">However, as a Humanist, I find certain aspects of Unitarian Universalism to
        be frustrating. The principle of affirming no creed is, I believe, less
        than forthright.<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">&nbsp; </span>Agreeing to
        disagree is an appropriate principle for our pluralistic society as a
        whole, but it is not appropriate for a religious community dedicated to
        celebration and action as a community. Groups that stand for everything
        stand for nothing, or else they deceive.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">It might be said that Mr. Reyka’s comments
        would also apply to many Unitarian Christian writers and groups of the
        past (and maybe even present), but then again, they did not go as far as
        the Unitarian Univeralists (UU’s) of today in being “all-</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-weight:normal;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">inclusive.” The Unitarian Christians at least professed to be followers of
        Jesus, even if they did not, as a group, explain what that meant, at
        least not in the form of a creed to which they required adherence.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-weight:normal;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">The “7 Principles” are not all that religious, except in a very general
        sense. Perhaps this is why Marcus Hamilton-Smythe wrote in a letter
        published in the June 2004 issue of </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;font-style:italic;font-weight:normal;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">The American Unitarian: </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;font-weight:normal;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">“The UU
        Church is a nice club, but a church without God leaves a lot to be
        desired.” I would venture to say that describing the UU’s as
        belonging to a church, a club, or even a group of any kind for that
        matter, may be stretching it a bit.”</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
font-weight:normal;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"><o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-weight:normal;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">After all, what do the UU’s have in common? As can be seen in a definition
        given earlier, a basic requirement for a group is that the members have
        similarities. The UU’s are all human beings, but it gets pretty
        difficult to come up with other similarities, given their extreme
        diversity. In any case, good for them if the UU’s find comfort in
        banding together, and to any UU’s that may be reading this, I am quite
        sincere about my best wishes. Many people, including myself, just cannot
        understand how UU’s can, or why they want to, be together given the
        wide spectrum represented. Their outlook seems more appropriate to be
        the basis for a nation rather than a church, even a “Church
        Universal.”<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-weight:normal;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">A strange phenomenon among ultra-non-creedal/super all-inclusive groups has
        been observed wherein they define themselves via what they don’t
        believe in, rather than by what they do believe in. In January 2005,
        there appeared in the now defunct web-based publication, </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;font-weight:normal;language:
EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The Unitarian Christian Journal </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-weight:normal;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">(UK), an article entitled, “The Forward
        March of the Non Brigade,” where a writer gave the name “Non
        Brigade” to such groups of the Unitarian persuasion. This writer
        explains:<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The Non Brigade – A group
        of people who negatively define themselves by what they don’t believe
        in - to them the Unitarian church provides nothing more than a forum for
        religious debate (or more accurately, carping about the perceived
        intolerance of Christians). Members of this group object to anyone with
        faith, fearing what they do not know or understand. These are the real
        obstruction to those Unitarians in both Britain and the United States
        who are working hard to re-establish Unitarianism as a radical,
        progressive alternative to ‘mainstream’ Christianity.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Apparently, the writer felt a
        great deal of frustration with the people he places among the “Non
        Brigade”. In my limited experience among Unitarians, I have felt
        similar frustration at times with this subgroup; fortunately for me,
        such contacts have been only from a distance and not very frequent. At
        the risk of seeming uncharitable, I think a term like “spiritual
        vampire” might also be appropriate for such people, at least for the
        most extreme ones. It seems like that they derive their entire spiritual
        existence from feeding on the ideas of others in order to give them a
        purpose, which involves little more than tearing down these ideas in an
        extremely menacing manner. A very sad situation for all concerned,
        including the “vampire.”<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">One reaction to the confusion
        in the UUA as to where it stands as a religious group might be found in
        the formation and continued existence of a Christian group among the
        UU’s, the UU Christian Fellowship (UUCF). Article II of the UUCF
        bylaws states the purposes of that group as “to diffuse the knowledge
        and promote the interests of Christianity; to encourage and aid the
        spread of the Christian religion and to support and promote religious
        activities.” The statement may be general, but there is at least some
        identifiable religious direction in it.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">A more recent and stronger reaction to the
        confusion in the UUA may of course be seen in the formation of our very
        own American Unitarian Conference (AUC) in 2000. This is a God-centered
        group, apart from the UUA, and although not exclusively for Christians,
        has many strongly Christian people among its members. The AUC wishes to
        promote and restore classical Unitarian traditions and philosophy in the
        United </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">States and even beyond.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">A good example of what the
        AUC supports can be found in D. R. Miano’s recent work, </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">An Explanation of Unitarian Christianity. </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">It is in essence an update of Clarke’s </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">Manual</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"> of 1884. The following excerpts are
        instructive and relevant to the topic at hand. Similarity with Clarke
        will be apparent, but note that more of the Miano work is excerpted here
        than that of Clarke, so a direct comparison of the two should not be
        attempted from the excerpts presented.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Preface<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">A manual such as this is
        meant simply to express the general belief of the majority of Unitarian
        Christians … <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Briefly described, Unitarian
        Christianity is, like other forms of Christianity, a religion that
        asserts the divine character, divine spirit, and divine foundation of
        the teaching of Jesus Christ. It places particular emphasis on reason,
        conscience, and free will in religion … it seeks ever to form surer
        and nobler understandings of God and of the world by a conscientious
        search for truth. ... <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The author hopes that it
        [this manual] will stimulate and arouse inquiry and deeper reflection on
        Unitarian Christianity.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">***<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">§ 15. The union that exists
        among Unitarians is one of sympathy and cooperation, not of formulas. No
        one among them has the right to decide what the others should believe. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">§ 16. A creed is simply a
        statement of belief or a list of points on which people can agree. In
        this sense creeds are good, useful, and desirable for individuals. If a
        number of persons who hold the same belief unite to convince others of
        its truth, this also is natural and right. If they state their beliefs
        in propositions and articles, this also may be useful. To such creeds
        Unitarians do not object. Many of their churches and organizations have
        adopted such statements of opinion. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">§ 17. But Unitarians object
        to religious creeds under the following circumstances: (a) When they are
        made a test of character; (b) When they are made a condition of
        fellowship; (c) When they become an obstacle to the spiritual progress
        of the Church or of an individual. Most of the creeds of the Christian
        Church have been liable to these objections. … They have been
        obstacles to progress, imposing the opinions of past centuries upon
        present belief. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">§ 18. Some object that
        Unitarians’ aversion to creeds results in too great a divergence of
        opinion in their religious views. But Unitarian Christians agree on the
        fundamental aspects of their religion, which include the indispensable
        tenets of Christianity (in their most basic, unrefined form), and which

line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The Universalists
        are so named for making universal salvation the cornerstone of Christian
        faith, a religiously democratizing proposition, traceable to the third
        century theologian Origen and the apostolic age.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Our Declaration of Faith was adopted by the
        Universalist General Convention in 1899 and reflects the language and
        beliefs of Universalists at that time. Recitation of the Declaration of
        Faith, or any part of it, is optional in this church. Many members of
        the church, however, recite some or all </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">of it each Sunday
        to honor early Universalists, to carry on their traditions and beliefs,
        or because it embodies elements of their own faith.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-weight:bold;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">***<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Universalist Declaration of
        Faith </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">(1899)</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"><br>
        <br>
        </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:
EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">We believe in the universal fatherhood of God<br>
        The spiritual authority and leadership of his son Jesus Christ<br>
        The trustworthiness of the Bible as containing a revelation from God.
        The certainty of just retribution for sin<br>
        And the final harmony of all souls with God.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Even though it is
        clearly stated that this Universalist creed is optional, it is still
        found on this church’s website. The statements made about it are
        respectful and some even reverential. Does this indicate that this creed
        is needed in some fundamental way by this group, if only in an
        unconscious way for some members?<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Maybe such a need
        is recognized in Matt Grant’s article, </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">The Unitarian Christian Shahada?</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">, which was
        published recently on the web in the </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">Journal of Unitarian Christianity</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"> and in the March
        2005 issue of </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The American
        Unitarian</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">. In this article, Grant makes the point that
        even though Unitarian Christian may disagree on many points of faith and
        may individually change their minds quite often as to what they believe
        concerning many issues, there are certain core beliefs that are shared
        by all and unshakable. He suggests a statement of very basic belief for
        Unitarian Christians like the Muslims have in their Shahada, which would
        express that which unites the group together as a community of faith. It
        is not meant to stifle free thought, but could serve as a starting point
        for debate and inquiry as to other points of faith. Grant suggests the
        following for this “Shahada”:<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">We believe that
        there is One God; we affirm the unity of all creation and take the
        example and teachings of the human Jesus as our Way in life.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Grant makes the
        point that this statement might be very important to end the
        divisiveness that has occurred recently among Unitarians. It would also
        be useful in attracting new members; that is, prospective members could
        easily get some idea what the core beliefs of the group are through it.
        Grant seems to be saying that really “big tents” like that of the
        UUA are a little too big for many people, and adoption of a simple creed
        might help alleviate this problem.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">A point of view
        very similar to Grant’s is taken by Robert Ross in an article in the
        September 2004 issue of </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The American Unitarian</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"> starting at page 106:<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoBodyText2" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-weight:normal;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN;mso-fareast-language:JA">In the process of
        moving towards a consistent and whole Unitarian Christianity, the nature
        of faith and of the faith transmitted from one generation to another,
        from believer to believer, from the church to the world, needs
        clarification and organization. The classic word for this is
        ‘dogma.’ Dogma refers to the organized understandings that can be
        taught and learned. In practice, all dogma is flexible and changing. But
        the word dogma became a byword for evil to the Enlightenment Christians
        … [but] should not blind us to the need for organizing a picture of
        God’s realities, as we can best understand them, that we can use to
        transmit our faith, and also as a basis for discussion of our faith. We
        should not be trapped by an 18th century reactive definition of dogma.
        Without dogma as organized thought we have no common language, nor a
        language that allows us to communicate with other Christians. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Perhaps this
        concept of or use for dogma is what eluded Channing in his quest for
        Christian unity and the Universal Church. (See the discussion on
        Channing in David Dulin’s </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">On Being Non-Sectarian</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"> at pages 133-134 of the September 2004 issue of </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The American Unitarian</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">.)<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">&nbsp;&nbsp; </span><o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The issue of creeds comes up in modern,
        non-Unitarian writing also. Excerpts from a few such works are given
        below. </span><span style="language:EN"><o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The first is from
        pages 178-179 of Kelly Elstrott’s 1998 book, </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The Fifth Revelation</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">, which presents highlights of and commentary on </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The Urantia Book</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">. This excerpt expresses thoughts similar in many ways to those of the
        Apostle Paul discussed earlier. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">[W]e can no
        longer control religion by harnessing it with the reins of ritual;
        people today are simply not willing to be told what to believe. What we
        seek is a </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">living faith</span><span style="font-size:
11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">. Just as
        scientists formulate theories based on the observable mechanics of the
        universe, so do spiritual seekers turn to the divine spirit within for
        an understanding of truth and right actions.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Certainly, it is
        more comfortable to follow a rigid set of guidelines spelling out
        appropriate beliefs and conduct, and to show up for an hour a week to
        recite a prayer heard many times before. But it is not as rewarding.
        Jesus proclaimed that the kingdom of God is within, and the more meaning
        we find in these words, the more inclined we will be to draw on the
        Spirit of Truth in determining the will of the Father.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">This does not
        mean to abandon the religion of your forebears. To the contrary, it is
        exhilarating to attend a religious service among fellow believers in God
        and feel his spirit fill the room. When you step back out into the
        world, however, trust in his spirit to guide you.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Our primary task
        for the future is to foster a spiritual renaissance in ourselves. Only
        then will it permeate the world.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The second
        excerpt from a non-Unitarian source comes from page 194 of a moderate
        Christian-oriented book of 2003, </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">Healing the Masculine Soul</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">, by Gordon Dalbey. It is
        reminiscent of Channing in some respects: “Doctrines and creeds may
        mark the trail, but they cannot substitute for Jesus, who alone is the
        Way.”</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN"><o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The third excerpt
        comes from an article by James Dillet Freeman at page 27 of the
        January/February 2005 issue of the </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">Unity Magazine</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;
color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">:<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 25; margin-right: 25" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The ideas of
        science change and change. Would we have our religion less open to new
        ideas? Unity is not a dogmatic approach to Truth; Unity does not have a
        creed which was written down centuries ago. Truth is eternal, but our
        idea of it changes as we change. Religion needs to be an expanding
        faith, to leave room for new truths, to leave us free to find the truth
        in and for ourselves. In an age of so many doubts and doubters, we need
        to leave room for them too. Tennyson was right when he said: There lives
        more faith in honest doubt, believe me, than in half the creeds. <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">It is interesting
        that these last writers from some fairly diverse orientations are so
        very much in line with Unitarians on the issue of creeds. Unitarians
        worried about the survival of the movement may wish to take some comfort
        from this.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">As a (nearly)
        final point, there seems to be a trend regarding creeds in that they
        tend to get more complex and/or rigid over time. Is this because people
        crave structure? Or is it that many people (especially today) don’t
        want to think and need a crutch?<span style="mso-spacerun:yes">&nbsp;&nbsp;</span></span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">This brings to
        mind the flaw in the American Unitarian tradition that David Burton
        mentioned in his </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">President’s Letter</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"> in the September 2004 issue of </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN">The American Unitarian. </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:
100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Burton put forward
        suggestions that this flaw was an inability to cope with people who did
        too much thinking and then went off on atheistic tangents, and/or simply
        a failure to evangelize. Would it be more accurate to say that the flaw
        was actually a failure to adequately take into account most people’s
        desire for structure, and/or their intellectual inabilities, or even
        laziness? Maybe the key to why the AUC grows slowly is hidden away in
        not looking at what most people are ready for/need at this moment; maybe
        they need to be taught or encouraged in thinking for themselves as to
        religion before they will be willing to be part of groups like the AUC.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:5.0pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">&nbsp;<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
font-weight:bold;mso-font-kerning:.5pt;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Conclusions</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"><o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-indent: 0; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Readers may draw many, perhaps even diverse,
        conclusions</span><span style="language: EN"> </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">from what I have
        presented here, and I will leave that to them as maybe a good Unitarian
        should. For my part, I take from this study of mine that one probably
        should have a personal creed and that a group creed is useful, but they
        all should be allowed to evolve, and particularly in the case of a group
        creed, not be allowed to become overly rigid or complex and eventually
        divisive. This is not in conflict with basic Unitarian principles.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">In the case of a
        group creed, perhaps we should look at it like secular laws or the
        Constitution, where we don’t always agree with every provision, but
        still support and uphold them for the common good and cohesiveness in
        society – after all, too much freedom leads to chaos. Such a creed
        might even be “unwritten” like the British constitution; that is,
        not totally formal nor in one discrete place. A group creed could be set
        up to exist alongside a statement of welcome or tolerance.<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">Lest we go too
        far with creeds though, we should keep three things in mind:<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">(1)
        Non-conformists were the true movers of history (see Steve Jones’
        article in </span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">The American Unitarian</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:
EN"> of September 2004);<o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">(2) The Golden
        Mean – moderation in all things; and <o:p>
        </o:p>
        </span></p>
        <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-justify: newspaper; text-kashida-space: 50%; text-indent: 0; tab-stops: .5in 5.0in 6.0in; margin-left: 5; margin-right: 5" align="justify"><span style="font-size:11.5pt;
line-height:100%;color:black;language:EN;mso-ansi-language:EN">(3) The quote by
        Mary Augusta Ward presented at the very start of this article:</span><span style="font-size:11.5pt;line-height:100%;color:black;font-style:italic;
