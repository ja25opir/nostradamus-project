May 1, 1928 was the first Lei Day in Hawaii and is customarily celebrated by organizing lei-making contests, festivals, parades, concerts and coronating a Lei Day King and Queen throughout the Hawaiian Islands. After the festivities have ended, the flower leis are taken to the tombs of the Aliâ€™i (Hawaiian royalty) and left there as offerings.<p>

The drift of the lei back to the shore indicates that the person will someday return to the Hawaiian islands. The custom of wearing leis originated with the indigenous Hawaiians, who wove necklaces of leaves or ferns or sometimes strung dried shells, fruits, beads, or bright feathers for personal adornment. Hawaiians celebrate Lei Day on May 1st, symbolizing their tradition of friendliness and the "Aloha Spirit".</p><p>
