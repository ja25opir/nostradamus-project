			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/12/03/superhero-santiago/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Newest Superhero: A Soccer-playing Car Mechanic from Lima">Newest Superhero: A Soccer-playing Car Mechanic from Lima</a></h2>
			<h4>December 3rd, 2008<!-- by Barb --> · <span>Comments Off</span>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/art/" title="View all posts in Art, Film, Music &amp; Dance" rel="category tag">Art, Film, Music &amp; Dance</a>, <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/sports/" title="View all posts in Sports" rel="category tag">Sports</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<p>I was so absorbed by the APEC brouhaha last month I missed the news that <a href="http://www.nbc.com/Heroes/create_your_hero/" target="_blank">the world’s newest superhero hails from Lima</a>.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/12/cast_santiago.jpg"><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-988" style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="cast_santiago" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/12/cast_santiago.jpg" alt="" width="415" height="221"></a></p>
<p><a href="http://www.nbc.com/Heroes/create_your_hero/" target="_blank">Santiago</a> is a viewer-created superhero character who stars in four micro-episodes in Sprint’s online series <a href="http://www.nbc.com/Heroes/heroesdestiny/" target="_blank">Heroes: Destiny</a>.&nbsp;</p>
<p>More than one million people participated in the online series by selecting attributes for a male and a female character and voting for the winning hero. Scriptwriters fashioned a four-part story around the character and shot the series with live actors.</p>
<p>Evidently, viewers were most intributed by the idea of a young working class guy from Lima possessing superpowers:</p>
<blockquote><p>Santiago is an ordinary young man living in Lima, Peru. Soccer is his passion, and he leads a simple life. Recently, he feels surrounded by a suffocating pressure to find purpose in his life. Influenced by his Catholic faith, his mother, and his friends, Santiago has been searching for his destiny.</p>
<p>When he discovers his extraordinary gifts of Accelerated Probability and Super Speed, things begin to become clear. He realizes he is no longer helpless, and that he can control his own destiny. But with power comes great responsibility and now, Santiago must make a choice. Will he use his newfound ability for good or for evil?</p>
<p>When a mysterious organization with an intimate knowledge of the secret life of Santiago’s father pressures him to use his powers for evil, it’s up to Santiago and his newfound allies to fight for what is right. Santiago’s choices will affect the destiny of everyone around him and change the world.</p></blockquote>
<p>&nbsp;Colombian actor Roberto Urbina plays Santiago in the online live-action series. Click <a href="http://www.nbc.com/Heroes/heroesdestiny/santiago/" target="_blank">here</a> to read more.</p>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/12/02/peru-through-the-eyes-of-peace-corps-volunteers/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Peru through the Eyes of Peace Corps Volunteers">Peru through the Eyes of Peace Corps Volunteers</a></h2>
			<h4>December 2nd, 2008<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/12/02/peru-through-the-eyes-of-peace-corps-volunteers/#comments" title="Comment on Peru through the Eyes of Peace Corps Volunteers">1 Comment</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/crossing-cultures/" title="View all posts in Crossing Cultures" rel="category tag">Crossing Cultures</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<p>From time to time, I like to check in with the blogs of U.S. Peace Corps volunteers working in Peru. The volunteers are mainly young, single&nbsp;people who are both idealistic and pragmatic, and their blogs provide&nbsp;fascinating glimpses into volunteer life in the provinces.</p>
<p>The blogs also are useful roadmaps for&nbsp;armchair adventurers&nbsp;who daydream about “someday” making a difference&nbsp;in Peru. If you’ve ever wondered what it’s like to live in an isolated village two days’ walk from the nearest road, with no running water or electricity,&nbsp;helping locals run a cooperative or improve their sanitation, these blogs are&nbsp;eye-opening resources.</p>
<p>About the Peace Corps: The Peace Corps was active in Peru from 1962 to 1967, and returned to Peru in 2002 after an absence of 27 years. It has&nbsp;now grown to 160 volunteers, according to the Peace Corps’&nbsp;<a href="http://www.peacecorps.gov/welcomebooks/pewb527.pdf">Welcome Book</a>.&nbsp;(Note: The Wecome Book is a massive 90+ page PDF download.) The volunteers complete an 11-week training session outside of Lima before being dispatched the various communities in Peru, where they&nbsp;live with local families and work on&nbsp;community projects. These projects&nbsp;focus on health, sanitation, agriculture and&nbsp;community development, among other areas.</p>
<p>Peruvian PC volunteers have been blogging about their experiences since at least March 2006, according to a<a href="http://www.peacecorpsjournals.com/pe.html"> list of online Peru PC journals</a>. That page provides&nbsp;links to 108 blogs, listed in descending chronological order. Some blogs are no longer active, just archives, but many others&nbsp;are in full swing.</p>
<p>Some of the posts are detailed, carefully crafted essays; others read like late-night ramblings. Feelings range from excitement and joy, to loneliness,&nbsp;sadness&nbsp;and weariness.&nbsp;All the blogs include a prominent disclaimer: “This blog does not reflect the opinions of the U.S. government or the Peace Corps.”</p>
<p>Here’s a sampling of what you’ll find:</p>

<dl class="wp-caption alignleft" style="width: 226px;">

<dd class="wp-caption-dd">Peace Corps volunteer Frank dressed as the wise mountain Huascaran</dd>

</h6>
<p>Frank in Ancash (<a href="http://www.wonderingfrank.blogspot.com/" target="_blank">The World through Frank’s Eyes</a>) works in youth development programs. He and fellow volunteers are attempting to educate locals about gender equality, a new concept in the Cordillera Huayhuash. In April <a href="http://wonderingfrank.blogspot.com/2008/06/healthy-gringosroad-trip.html" target="_blank">he and some fellow volunteers dressed up as local apus </a>(mountain spirits) in a theatrical production that showed how men have much to learn from women and vice versa.</p>
<p><a href="http://kondrath.blogspot.com/" target="_blank">Kondrath in Peru </a>(since&nbsp;Oct. 2007) is a&nbsp;blog by Michael Kondrath, who notes:&nbsp;”I am currently living in the second highest mountain range in the world as a Peace Corps health volunteer. I get sick a lot and have fleas, but I still have fun.”&nbsp;That&nbsp;droll humor informs a lot of Kondrath’s entries, including “<a href="http://kondrath.blogspot.com/2008/11/wanna-live-in-mansion-ok.html" target="_blank">Wanna Live in a Mansion? OK</a>“:</p>
<blockquote><p><span>“Things keep on getting better and better here.<span> </span>Yesterday I was invited to a guinea pig lunch, and then invited to live in a three story house.<span> </span>The new place on the plaza in town, </span><span>and will serve as a hotel in a few years when it is ready.<span> </span>But for the next year, I will have free reign.<span> </span>I now have the luxury of choosing which bedroom I will sleep in and which toilet I will puke in. <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/12/02/peru-through-the-eyes-of-peace-corps-volunteers/#more-982" class="more-link">[Read more →]</a></span></p></blockquote>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/29/peruvian-hairless-dog-nap/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Peruvian Hairless Dog Nap">Peruvian Hairless Dog Nap</a></h2>
			<h4>November 29th, 2008<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/29/peruvian-hairless-dog-nap/#comments" title="Comment on Peruvian Hairless Dog Nap">2 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/animals-in-peru/" title="View all posts in Animals in Peru" rel="category tag">Animals in Peru</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<div style="text-align: left; padding: 3px;"><a title="photo sharing" href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/esenrique/2176859705/"><img class="aligncenter" style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" src="http://farm3.static.flickr.com/2211/2176859705_35d5d12481.jpg" alt="" width="450" height="300"></a></div>
<p><span style="font-size: 0.8em; margin-top: 0px;"><a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/esenrique/2176859705/">Peruvian Hairless Dog Nap</a>, originally uploaded by <a href="http://www.flickr.com/people/esenrique/">GitanoLatino</a>.</span></p>
<p>Photographer GitanoLatino knelt in the dry sands of northern Peru to capture this marvelous dog’s eye view of a sleeping Peruvian hairless (taken in January 2007).</p>
<p>The little dog is sleeping near the ruins of Chan Chan, an adobe city built by the Chimu people around 1300 AD. It is the largest adobe complex of the pre-Columbian Americas.</p>
<p>Peruvian hairless dogs roam the Chan Chan complex to this day.&nbsp; Wouldn’t it be remarkable if one of these ancient dogs, who sat on the laps of Moche and Inca rulers, were to take up resident in the U.S. Casa Blanca?</p>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/28/laura-bush-does-peru-toilets-for-the-people/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Laura Bush Does Peru: Toilets for the People!">Laura Bush Does Peru: Toilets for the People!</a></h2>
			<h4>November 28th, 2008<!-- by Barb --> · <span>Comments Off</span>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/earthquakes/" title="View all posts in Earthquakes" rel="category tag">Earthquakes</a>, <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/money-econ-poli/" title="View all posts in Money, Economics, Politics" rel="category tag">Money, Economics, Politics</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<p>Laura Bush had an interesting itinerary this past weekend in Peru. I think it merits more public attention than does her husband’s hobnobbing with APEC leaders at the final summit meeting.</p>
<h6 class="mceTemp mceIEcenter">
<dl id="attachment_972" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 393px;">
<dt class="wp-caption-dt"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/laura-bush-san-clemente-peru.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-972" style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="laura-bush-san-clemente-peru" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/laura-bush-san-clemente-peru.jpg" alt="Mrs. Laura Bush looks on Friday, Nov. 21, 2008, as Ms. Maria Salguero Trillo, Community Health Educator Volunteer at the San Clemente Health Center in San Clemente, Peru, demonstrates how families are trained to treat contaminated water for safe drinking. In the town of 25,000, nearly 89 percent of the homes were affected by the August 2007, 8.0-magnitude earthquake. White House photo by Joyce N. Boghosian " width="383" height="263"></a></dt>
<dd class="wp-caption-dd">Laura Bush looks on as aid worker demonstrates how to decontaminate drinking water in earthquake zone (San Clemente, Peru, Nov. 21)</dd>
</dl>
</h6>
<p style="text-align: left;">Of note:</p>
<p>(1) She didn’t come with W. (He flew with Arroz.) Instead of arriving at Lima in Airforce One with her husband, Laura Bush flew from Panama to southern Peru. Some bloggers have read into the separate travel itineraries (strained marital relations), but I’m skipping those speculations because…</p>
<p>(2) Laura Bush should be commended for <a href="http://www.wtopnews.com/?nid=105&amp;sid=1523753" target="_blank">visiting Pisco</a>, one of the towns ravaged by the August 2007 earthquake. Her visit drew attention to an area that’s faded from&nbsp;most people’s&nbsp;memory. One and a half years later, Pisco and Ica and nearby towns still are in ruins, without many of the sanitary and social services that make life bearable.</p>
<p>About 520 people were killed, 1,844 injured in the August 2007 earthquake in southern Peru. Nearly 53,000 houses were destroyed, <a href="http://www.reliefweb.int/rw/rwb.nsf/db900sid/TBRL-772U2X?OpenDocument" target="_blank">according to INDECI records</a>. An additional 50,000 were badly damaged.</p>
<p>I give the outgoing First Lady a thumbs-up for ditching Lima on Friday, Nov. 21, in favor of this dusty disaster area. She visited a housing reconstruction project partially funded by <a href="http://www.reliefweb.int/rw/rwb.nsf/db900sid/TBRL-772U2X?OpenDocument" target="_blank">USAID</a>&nbsp;and gave a press conference in which she praised the “warmth” of the Peruvian people (see transcript below).</p>
<p>She also mentioned that she was pleased to see that the&nbsp;new houses have bathrooms and toilets. That comment might sound strange to American ears, but it makes sense&nbsp;if you know Peru:&nbsp;many homes&nbsp;don’t have&nbsp;toilets and sewage connections. Whoever briefed Laura on the USAID project had the intelligence to&nbsp;make&nbsp;a talking point out of&nbsp;the toilets; the comment gives the impression&nbsp;that the First Lady is in touch with the realities of life in southern Peru.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Yes, I know the information is fed to her, but consider this: How many U.S. citizens do you know who have even an inkling of what life is like for people in developing countries?</p>
<p>(3) Laura and one of her daughters also tried ceviche in Peru — not a surprise, but they did sample it at Pescados Capitales, one of my favorite restaurants. (See my post from last week on the restaurant’s APEC menu.)</p>
<p>Owner Nguyen Chavez tells me that Mrs. Bush and one of her daughters ate&nbsp;cebiche de lenguado, grilled octopus,&nbsp;salmón en salsa de alcaparras con risotto de legumbres (the dish known as “vanidad” on the menu) y grilled tuna with garbanzo bean salad (“impaciencia”).</p>
<p>She also drank a&nbsp;pisco sour.</p>
<p>Here’s the official White House transcript of a Q&amp;A section&nbsp;at the Adobe Housing Reconstruction Project, in Pisco, which Laura Bush visited last Friday. The project uses reinforced adobe, which is superior to pure-mud adobe but inferior to brick and mortar (which is expensive in Peru); <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/28/laura-bush-does-peru-toilets-for-the-people/#more-961" class="more-link">[Read more →]</a></p>

			<h4>November 27th, 2008<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/27/overstressed-americans-binge-on-junk-food-drive-up-sales-cheers-snack-food-industry/#comments" title="Comment on Overstressed Americans Binge on Junk Food, Drive up Sales, Cheers Snack Food Industry!">3 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/crossing-cultures/" title="View all posts in Crossing Cultures" rel="category tag">Crossing Cultures</a>, <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/food/" title="View all posts in Food &amp; Dining" rel="category tag">Food &amp; Dining</a>, <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/looking-back-at-the-united-states/" title="View all posts in Looking Back at the United States" rel="category tag">Looking Back at the United States</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<p><img class="size-full wp-image-1875 alignleft" title="junkfood" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/junkfood.jpg" alt="junkfood" width="300" height="300">The slow-motion collapse of the U.S. economy is&nbsp;harming consumers and businesses across the country&nbsp;– well, almost every&nbsp;business that is.</p>
<p>One industry is enjoying big sales gains thanks to the stress experienced by ordinary working people. North American sales of “savory snacks” (potato chips, Cheetos, etc.) by Frito-Lay are up 9% for the third qurater, reports Potato Pro, “your source for all things potato.”</p>
<p>Sales rose despite hikes in gas and ingredient prices and lowered earnings for Americans. (Some companies made up for the higher costs by switching to smaller bags.)</p>
<p>Potato Pro <em>jefe</em> Paul van Eijck thinks&nbsp;the trend is only going to&nbsp;accelerate. As the U.S. economy worsens, stressed-out Americans will&nbsp;binge more on junk food. To quote van Eijck:</p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">&nbsp;</span>Hooray! Americans are being booted out of&nbsp;their homes and losing their retirement savings, but as long as they continue to&nbsp;stuff themselves with Pringles, all is right with the U.S.A. –&nbsp;right folks?</p>
<blockquote><p><span style="font-size: 10pt; color: #666666; font-family: &quot;Arial&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;; mso-fareast-font-family: 'Times New Roman';">&nbsp;And if you believe that stress result in more excessive consumption of snacks, the market may start to look even better.</span></p></blockquote>

(Read my post <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/10/22/we-want-to-be-your-solution-for-all-things-potato/" target="_blank">here</a>.) I never intended to feature their insiders’ newsletter on my blog, but since they rushed me into their fraternity last month, I haven’t had a choice but to receive bulletins on the state of the savory snack industry.</p>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/25/climate-change-threatens-perus-native-potatoes/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Climate Change Threatens Peru’s Native Potatoes">Climate Change Threatens Peru’s Native Potatoes</a></h2>
			<h4>November 25th, 2008<!-- by Barb --> · <span>Comments Off</span>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/climate-change/" title="View all posts in Climate Change &amp; Disappearing Glaciers" rel="category tag">Climate Change &amp; Disappearing Glaciers</a>, <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/perus-andes-mountains/" title="View all posts in Peru's Andes Mountains" rel="category tag">Peru's Andes Mountains</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<p>Here’s a <a href="http://http://forests.org/shared/reader/welcome.aspx?linkid=106623&amp;keybold=global%20AND%20%20environment%20AND%20%20lack%20AND%20%20financing" target="_blank">timely article from Eliza Barclay </a>on a topic close to many Peruvians’ hearts: potatoes.</p>
<h6 class="mceTemp">
<dl id="attachment_938" class="wp-caption alignright" style="width: 210px;">
<dt class="wp-caption-dt"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/treasure-of-the-andes.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-938" style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="treasure-of-the-andes" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/treasure-of-the-andes.jpg" alt="" width="200" height="300"></a></dt>
<dd class="wp-caption-dd">Cover photo from The Potato, Treasure of the Andes</dd>
</dl>
</h6>
<p>&nbsp;Extreme weather shifts&nbsp;caused by global warming are interrupting millenia-old agricultural cyles in the high Andes, reports Barclay for the Miami Herald. That turmoil is having a devastating effect on crops of native Peruvian potatoes, which grow at altitudes of 3,000+ meters above sea level.</p>
<p>Barclay notes in “Peru’s Potato Farmers Adapt to Climate Change” (Sept. 11, 2008):</p>
<blockquote><p>For the first half of his life, Gregorio Huanuco farmed to a rhythm that dictated the survival of his grandparents and ancestors for thousands of years. He waited for the rains to fall on his small parcel of land in this village at 11,000 feet in the Cordillera Blanca, or White Range, of the Andes in central Peru, and planted native varieties of potatoes as well as cereal crops like quinoa. When the crops ripened, Huanuco, 45, harvested what he needed and sold what he didn’t in the city of Huaraz several hundred feet below in the valley.</p>
<p>Climatologists say global warming’s impact was first documented in the Peruvian Andes in 1970, but 1990 is the year Huanuco says he began to notice disruptions, first in small, bizarre, anomalous forms: a battering hailstorm, two months without rain, a warm winter. Then the quirky weather became more consistent and other oddities began to appear: rats nibbling away at his cereal crops and a fungus, known as late blight, blanketing his potatoes.</p>
<p>LAND ONCE FERTILE</p>
<p>”Before, we planted all year long, any month we wanted to,” Huanuco said, dubiously eyeing his tiny plot, recently sown with potato seed. “Now we only get water a few times a year and so we cannot plant as much, and the pests and diseases keep coming.”</p></blockquote>
<p>Click<a href="http://forests.org/shared/reader/welcome.aspx?linkid=106623&amp;keybold=global%20AND%20%20environment%20AND%20%20lack%20AND%20%20financing" target="_blank"> here </a>to read the entire article.</p>
<p>This news is devastating&nbsp;for Peru because 95 percent of its potato crop is consumed nationally — only 5 percent is exported. People who live in the high sierra especially depend on these native potatoes (which differ from the white potatoes sold in US stores) because they are among the few crops that can grow at very high altitudes.</p>
<p>Without a good supply of potatoes, the people in the <em>puno</em> will starve — and they are growing more and more hungry each year.</p>
<p>The title of Barclay’s article suggests that Peruvian farmers have figured out how to adapt to climate change, but her story reveals that they don’t yet understand how&nbsp;to&nbsp;solve the problem.&nbsp;</p>
<p>NGOs and government organizations are beginning to search for solutions, and some excellent pilot programs are underway. Time is critical, though — farmers have been battling drought and pests since 1990. It will be too late for some.</p>
<p>I think of&nbsp;the farmer/herders I met in Ausangate this September and how vulnerable their crops&nbsp;are to shifts in the weather.&nbsp; “Our lands are producing less and less,” they told me. “We are very sad.”</p>
<p>“We are hungry,” they added. They said it in the simple, matter-of-fact way that people do when they have grown accustomed to no one caring about them.</p>
<p>This year, 2008, has been dubbed the International Year of the Potato. Well-orchestrated publicity campaigns educated people worldwide on the benefits of potatoes, in general, and on the remarkable attributes of Peru’s 3,000 varieties of native potatoes. I don’t think the world has ever had such potato consciousness. I hope that this awareness translates into useful, well-funded programs to save the native potato before it and the people of the high Andes become&nbsp;casualties of climate change.</p>
<p>–Barbara R. Drake</p>
<p>For more information on native Peruvian potatoes and efforts to rescue this ancient crop, visit the <a href="http://www.cipotato.org/" target="_blank">International Potato Center</a>, In Lima. Peru.</p>

			<h4>November 24th, 2008<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/24/bush-stifles-urge-to-wring-asian-leaders-necks-at-summit-just-kidding/#comments" title="Comment on Bush Stifles Urge to Wring Asian Leaders’ Necks at Summit (Just Kidding)">4 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/money-econ-poli/" title="View all posts in Money, Economics, Politics" rel="category tag">Money, Economics, Politics</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<h6 class="mceTemp">
<dl id="attachment_930" class="wp-caption alignnone" style="width: 460px;">
<dt class="wp-caption-dt"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/bush-wrings-asian-leaders-neck.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-930  " style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="bush-wrings-asian-leaders-neck" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/bush-wrings-asian-leaders-neck.jpg" alt="Bush towers over Japanese leader Taro Aso and Korea's " width="450" height="372"></a></dt>
<dd class="wp-caption-dd">Bush towers over Japanese leader Taro Aso (L) and Korea’s Lee Myung-Bak (R), at the Official Group Photo for the APEC Leaders’ Summit, Nov. 23, Lima, Peru; &nbsp;photo c. Jorge Vera 2008</dd>
</dl>
</h6>
<p>El Fotografo snagged this photo of U.S. President George Bush making an incriminating gesture with his hands behind the Japanese and Korean leaders at the APEC Leaders’ Summit, Sunday, Nov. 23, 2008.</p>
<p>Korea’s Lee Myung-Bak looks like he’s flinching in pain.</p>
<p>If&nbsp;Bush did secretly feel like wringing the Asian leaders’ necks, I don’t know why. Nearly everything went his way at APEC, and he got to go home with a nifty $300 alpaca-wool poncho.</p>
<p>The day prior to this photo shoot, the outgoing U.S. president pleaded with Pacific Rim leaders to keep trade doors open — or else.</p>
<p>Result:&nbsp;Everyone agreed to keep buying and selling and investing for the next 12 months. Nobody&nbsp;went on record as saying the&nbsp;global financial crisis was the&nbsp;United States’ fault.</p>
<p>Also on Saturday Bush convinced APEC leaders to&nbsp;push for North Korea to give up its nuclear program — or else.</p>
<p>–Barbara R. Drake</p>

			<h4>November 24th, 2008<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/24/not-trailing-bush-at-the-apec/#comments" title="Comment on (Not) Trailing Bush at the APEC">2 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/food/" title="View all posts in Food &amp; Dining" rel="category tag">Food &amp; Dining</a>, <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/money-econ-poli/" title="View all posts in Money, Economics, Politics" rel="category tag">Money, Economics, Politics</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<h6 class="mceTemp mceIEcenter">
<dl id="attachment_925" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 410px;">
<dt class="wp-caption-dt"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/bush-in-apec-poncho-photo-by-jorge-vera-2008.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-925 " style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="bush-in-apec-poncho-photo-by-jorge-vera-2008" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/bush-in-apec-poncho-photo-by-jorge-vera-2008.jpg" alt="The Texas cowbow-turned-El Chalan, APEC Summit, Nov. 23, 2008; photo c. <dd class="wp-caption-dd">The Texas cowbow-turned-El Chalan, APEC Leaders’ Summit, Nov. 23, 2008; photo c. Jorge Vera</dd>
</dl>
</h6>
<p>It was&nbsp;4 p.m. on the final day of the APEC Summit, and most of the leaders and the journalists had left the Minstry of Defense building where the summit had been held.</p>
<p>El Fotógrafo and I were huddled in a cubicle in the International Media Center, trying to identify which Asian leader was which in a photo EF had taken earlier that afternoon.</p>
<p>“Is that the president of Thailand? No, Vietnam?” We kept checking with a list of published photos of the leaders, most of which appeared to have been taken when the leaders were younger and had more hair.</p>
<p>In the background a television broadcast a late-afternoon press meeting with the president of Singapore.&nbsp;</p>
<p>“Mr. President,” one journalist in the audience asked. “What do you think of the anti-pirating measure being proposed?”</p>
<p>“Oh, yes, pirating is a big problem,” said the president. “Groups of pirates are taking over ships and stealing the oil. This has got to stop.”</p>
<p>“No, excuse me, I mean — the problem with intellectual property infringement,” said the journalist.</p>
<p>“Oh, yes,” said the leader quickly, not losing a beat, “We have got to ensure that property rights are respected worldwide.”</p>
<p>And so it went that afternoon.</p>
<p>That I was at the APEC Summit and filing a story from&nbsp;the Media Center was an unlikely event, from my perspective. I had been contacted by a U.S. paper two days prior&nbsp;to be on “standby” for the&nbsp;meetings&nbsp;in case they needed some “local color.”</p>
<p>I also was supposed to follow President Bush around and watch for something interesting to happen.</p>
<p>Perhaps the idea the editors&nbsp;had in mind was Bush calling the APEC “OPEC,” as he did last year in Sydney, or Bush&nbsp;calling Alan Garcia “gordo” or Bush getting feisty with the president of Russia.</p>
<p>As it turned out, I never did manage to be anywhere near George W. Bush during the two days I was at the Little Pentagon.&nbsp; Like other summits with world leaders, the APEC was highly orchestrated and designed to frustrate both potential terrorists and journalists interested in asking serious questions of the outgoing U.S. president.</p>
<p>I sensed this in advance, of course, but still I hoped that some strange confluence of events (these things happen in Peru) would put me &amp; W together for an impromptu interview op — say, in a stairwell&nbsp;by one of Fujimori’s former torture chambers (the APEC summit was held in the very building where Fuji’s men used to grill and torture suspected Shining Path members). Alas, my interview chance never materialized.</p>
<p>EF had better luck than I did. He was admitted to a pool of photographers who shot the official APEC leaders portrait, which took place around noon on Sunday.</p>
<p>By that time, I think W had had enough of Peru. The night before, the organizers of a gala dinner had plied him and other leaders with pisco sours and liquors made of lucuma, chirimoya and pisco.&nbsp; For a guy who’s supposedly on the wagon, that’s a triple challenge to the willpower center.</p>
<p>So W was looking a little peaked by noon on Sunday, but kind of pleased to be twirling around in an alpaca &nbsp;poncho, and EF got some interesting shots of him, some of which deserve satiric captions.</p>
<p>Where was I while this was happening?</p>
<p>I and 40 other reporters were being held in a very hot and stuffy waiting&nbsp;area, prior to undergoing yet another security check (take off belt, shoes, laptop, etc.) before entering the summit auditorium. We stood there with our heavy gear in a plastic-enclosed corridor for more than an hour and then we were finally admitted.</p>
<p>As I took my seat on the balcony of the auditorium, a guard told me, “No journalists are permitted here.” I can’t think of a stranger thing to say to a&nbsp;journalist you’re escorting to a media event.</p>
<p>I caught&nbsp;the last two minutes of a speech by the “Say No to Pirates” president of Singapore. Then it was all over.</p>
<p>Bush had ditched the event after the official photo&nbsp;session and gone straight to the airport.</p>
<p>It was a strenous two days of Not Trailing Bush. On&nbsp;Saturday afternoon I stood for an hour and a half on a plaza&nbsp;waiting for the US president, only to catch&nbsp;from the corner of my eye the sight of his motorcade leaving from a far gate. He had eluded the press again.</p>
<p>I would have liked to have asked Mr. Bush many questions.&nbsp;I would have liked to have asked&nbsp;what he thought of unfettered free&nbsp;trade now that the US economy is tanking and the global economy along with it. I would have liked to have asked him about Iraq and Guatanamo and the erosion of rights in the United States.&nbsp;I would&nbsp;have liked to have asked&nbsp;how he thought History would judge his presidency.</p>
<p>But none of that was possible.</p>
<p>I did manage to speak to one&nbsp;very important person. though.&nbsp;Hector&nbsp;is&nbsp;a farmer from Tarma who grows potatoes. He stood around in the journalists’ cafeteria next to an elaborate display of native Peruvian potato varieties and handed out samples of LAYS ANDINO potato chips during the weekend.</p>
<p>Hector was dressed in a felt cap, with an Andean-fabric satchel wrapped around his&nbsp;shoulders for toting his potatoes to market. He was&nbsp;small and low&nbsp;key and about as close to&nbsp;an authentic Peruvian as most of the foreign journalists would get.</p>
<p>It was hard for a journalist to get face time with&nbsp;the politicos at APEC but Hector and 100 varieties of papas were there to photograph and interview to our hearts’ content.</p>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/21/bullfighting-thrives-amid-growing-protests-in-peru/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Bullfighting Thrives Amid Growing Protests in Peru">Bullfighting Thrives Amid Growing Protests in Peru</a></h2>
			<h4>November 21st, 2008<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/21/bullfighting-thrives-amid-growing-protests-in-peru/#comments" title="Comment on Bullfighting Thrives Amid Growing Protests in Peru">4 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/animals-in-peru/bullfighting/" title="View all posts in Bullfighting" rel="category tag">Bullfighting</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<h6 class="mceTemp mceIEcenter">
<dl id="attachment_908" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 310px;">
<dt class="wp-caption-dt"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/fernando-roca-rey-2.jpg"><img class="size-medium wp-image-908 " style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="fernando-roca-rey-2" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/fernando-roca-rey-2-300x200.jpg" alt="Peruvian bullfighter Fernando Roca Rey, Acho stadium, Lima, Nov. 2, 2008; photo c. Jorge Vera" width="300" height="200"></a></dt>
<dd class="wp-caption-dd">Peruvian bullfighter Fernando Roca Rey, Acho stadium, Lima, Nov. 2, 2008; photo c. Jorge Vera</dd>
</dl>
</h6>
<p>My news feature&nbsp;on bullfighting in Peru&nbsp;appears on the front page of today’s&nbsp;Miami Herald&nbsp;(“<a href="http://www.nowpublic.com/culture/peru-no-torture-many-protest-bullfighting-peru" target="_blank">Many protest bullfighting in Peru</a>“), with photos by El Fotografo. The Herald edited the story and retitled it to put more emphasis on the <em>antitaurinos</em> (bullfighting protestors) than I did in my original version, which I titled, “Bullfighting Thrives Amid Growing Protests in Peru” (see my original below).</p>
<p>The subject of bullfighting intrigued me because the tradition&nbsp;is undergoing a curious revival in Lima — curious because while attendance is rising at the Acho bullfights, there’s also a growing countermovement to end bullfighting in Peru on the grounds of animal cruelty. The spectacle had faded in popularity&nbsp;about eight years ago, and then Peru’s economy went into an upswing and attendance began to rise.</p>
<p>Given that bullfighting in banned in most developed countries, it’s significant that Peru (which wants to be recognized as a modern country) still clings to this controversial tradition.</p>
<h6 class="mceTemp">
<dl id="attachment_910" class="wp-caption alignright" style="width: 310px;">
<dt class="wp-caption-dt"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/julio-olleea-bull-mask1.jpg"><img class="size-medium wp-image-910 " style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="julio-olleea-bull-mask1" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/julio-olleea-bull-mask1-300x209.jpg" alt="Anti-taurino protester in Lima, Nov. 2, 2008; photo by Jorge Vera" width="300" height="209"></a></dt>
<dd class="wp-caption-dd">Anti-taurino protester in Lima, Nov. 2, 2008</dd>
</dl>
</h6>
<p>El Fotografo and I got to spend time on both sides of the fence, so to speak. We interviewed members of Peru Antitaurino, an anti-bullfighting alliance that’s been protesting in Peru for four years. I also talked to Freddy Villafuerte, one of the promoters of the Senor de los Milagros bullfight festival, who is himself a bullfighter and speaks for aficionados.</p>
<p>Then there was the matter of our being allowed to watch the bullfights from inside the <em>callejon</em> — the area right outside the ring where the matador and bull fight to the death.&nbsp; EF and I watched the November 2 corrida from this rare perspective, which is so close to the fighting that occasionally a bull leaps over the fence and charges at the spectators.</p>
<p>I am the kind of person who likes to spend her Sunday afternoons reading quietly on the couch, so you can imagine how agitated I felt standing just a few feet&nbsp;from an angry 500-kilo bull.</p>
<p>No, I did not get gored, <a href="http://www.cbsnews.com/stories/2008/10/16/60minutes/main4526581.shtml" target="_blank">unlike the reporter for 60 Minutes</a>.</p>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/20/waiter-theres-an-apec-fly-in-my-ceviche-classico/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Waiter, There’s an APEC Fly in My Ceviche Classico!">Waiter, There’s an APEC Fly in My Ceviche Classico!</a></h2>
			<h4>November 20th, 2008<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/20/waiter-theres-an-apec-fly-in-my-ceviche-classico/#comments" title="Comment on Waiter, There’s an APEC Fly in My Ceviche Classico!">1 Comment</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/food/" title="View all posts in Food &amp; Dining" rel="category tag">Food &amp; Dining</a>, <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/money-econ-poli/" title="View all posts in Money, Economics, Politics" rel="category tag">Money, Economics, Politics</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<p>By Barbara R. Drake, with photos by Jorge Vera</p>
<h6 class="mceTemp mceIEcenter">
<dl id="attachment_895" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 460px;">
<dt class="wp-caption-dt"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/fuerza-asiatic-front-with-mexican-inspired-causa-rear.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-895 " style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="Fuerza Asiatic, Pescados Capitales restaurant, Lima, Peru" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/fuerza-asiatic-front-with-mexican-inspired-causa-rear.jpg" alt="Civiche Fuera Asiatica, from Pescados Capitales, Lima, Peru; photo c. Jorge Vera 2008" width="450" height="303"></a></dt>
<dd class="wp-caption-dd">Ceviche Fuerza Asiatica, from Pescados Capitales, Lima, Peru</dd>
</dl>
</h6>
<p>Call it <em>haute cuisine</em> with a political bite.</p>
<p>That’s what some foreign dignitaries are tucking into at a top eatery in Lima this week, when the APEC Leader’s Summit concludes with a special focus on the world economic crisis.</p>
<p>Pescados Capitales, on the city’s gastronomic corridor of Avenida La Mar, is serving up a menu of original dishes that salute and satirize “Los Pecados de la APEC” (The Sins of APEC). Overseen by head chef Wilfredo Castillo and owner Nguyen Chávez, the APEC dishes feature “summit” gatherings of classic Peruvian foods like ceviche (raw marinated fish) and lúcuma (a sweet Amazonian fruit), paired with ingredients associated with China, Mexico, the United States and some of the other 21 visiting nations. &nbsp;</p>
<p>The big question is, said Chávez, 38, in a recent interview, will the flavors/countries dominate one another, or cooperate in the next decade?</p>
<p>“Fuerza Asiatica” (Asian Strength, equivalent US$11) is a tip of the toque to Asia’s economic leverage, the driving force behind the APEC meetings.  <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/20/waiter-theres-an-apec-fly-in-my-ceviche-classico/#more-893" class="more-link">[Read more →]</a></p>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/19/interview-with-bullfighter-jose-uceda-leal-women-like-to-see-a-man-face-death/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Interview with Bullfighter José Uceda Leal: “Women like to see a man face death”">Interview with Bullfighter José Uceda Leal: “Women like to see a man face death”</a></h2>
			<h4>November 19th, 2008<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/19/interview-with-bullfighter-jose-uceda-leal-women-like-to-see-a-man-face-death/#comments" title="Comment on Interview with Bullfighter José Uceda Leal: “Women like to see a man face death”">3 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/animals-in-peru/bullfighting/" title="View all posts in Bullfighting" rel="category tag">Bullfighting</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<h6 class="mceTemp mceIEcenter">
<dl id="attachment_881" class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 370px;">
<dt class="wp-caption-dt"><a href="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/uceda-leal-5.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-881  " style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" title="uceda-leal-5" src="http://americaninlima.com/wp-content/uploads/2008/11/uceda-leal-5.jpg" alt="Spanish bullfighter Jose Uceda Leal, photo c. Jorge Vera 2008" width="360" height="561"></a></dt>
<dd class="wp-caption-dd">Spanish bullfighter Jose Uceda Leal before his first corrida at Plaza de Acho, Lima, Nov. 1, 2008: , photo c. Jorge Vera 2008</dd>
</dl>
</h6>
<p>Born and trained in Madrid,&nbsp;José Igacio Uceda Leal ranks among&nbsp;the top toreros&nbsp;in Spain. Tall and slender, the 31-year-old bullfighter moves in the ring with an elegant, commanding presence. When he’s not dancing with bulls or stabbing them through the aorta, Uceda Leal is <a href="http://peru21.pe/impresa/noticia/jose-ignacio-uceda-leal-miedo-companero-viaje-torero-siempre/2008-11-01/228959" target="_blank">prone to giving introspective, rather philosophical answers </a>to questions from the press.</p>
<p>“In life and in bullfighting, there are moments that are real trials by fire,” he told a reporter in 1999, “some of which you cannot imagine, but which raise you up as a person.”</p>
<p>Uceda Leal has experienced his share of&nbsp;those moments. Since&nbsp;July 25, 1991, when he wore his first <em>traje de luces (</em>suit of lights) in Mont de Marsan, France, he’s fought and killed more than 450 bulls in Spain and Latin America, and he’s seen members of his own <em>cuadrilla </em>(bullfighting squad) killed in the ring as well. He fights in about 30 to 40 corridas each year, and has won a good size collection of ears and tails — the traditional <em>taurino</em> measure of success in the ring. This year alone he has fought in 38 festivals against 78 bulls and won 47 <em>orejas</em>, according to his <a href="http://www.ucedaleal.com/" target="_blank">official web site</a>.&nbsp;(Click <a href="http://www.ucedaleal.com/" target="_blank">here</a>&nbsp;for stats on Uceda Leal, in Spanish.)</p>
<p>Like other 21st-century&nbsp;bullfighters, he walks a&nbsp;precarious line between fame and infamy — revered by passionate fans, on the one hand, who mob him like a rock star wherever he fights, and on the other hand,&nbsp;reviled by animal-rights activists who&nbsp;condemn him and his fellow <em>toreros</em> as <em>asasinos </em>(assasins).&nbsp;</p>
<p>Given the intense public scrutiny he undergoes,&nbsp;Uceda Leal seeks solitude whenever his schedule permits. Last January&nbsp;he spent eight days <a href="http://www.ucedaleal.com/noticias?page=2" target="_blank">walking the&nbsp;Camino de Santiago,&nbsp;Spain’s ancient &nbsp;pilgrimage route</a>, to prepare himself mentally and physically for the upcoming bullfight season in Latin America.&nbsp; <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/19/interview-with-bullfighter-jose-uceda-leal-women-like-to-see-a-man-face-death/#more-882" class="more-link">[Read more →]</a></p>

			<h2><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/18/peru-police-arrest-man-36-grenades-apec/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Peru Police Arrest Man with 36 Grenades (for APEC?)">Peru Police Arrest Man with 36 Grenades (for APEC?)</a></h2>
			<h4>November 18th, 2008<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/18/peru-police-arrest-man-36-grenades-apec/#comments" title="Comment on Peru Police Arrest Man with 36 Grenades (for APEC?)">8 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/money-econ-poli/" title="View all posts in Money, Economics, Politics" rel="category tag">Money, Economics, Politics</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<p><a href="http://pah2.golding.id.au/images/grenade.gif"><img class=" alignright" src="http://pah2.golding.id.au/images/grenade.gif" alt="" width="185" height="266"></a></p>
<p>Just two days ago, El Fotografo and I were talking with El Hijo about the beefed-up security for the APEC Summit, which concludes this week in Lima.</p>
<p>“Are terrorists going to kill the presidents?” EH asked, as we drove by the LarcoMar shopping mall in Miraflores, which was swarming with police.</p>
<p>“Probably not,” I told him. “But you never know, so that’s why they have roadblocks and police checking things. Sometimes it can be just one crazy person who throws a bomb to get attention.”</p>
<p>“Oh,” said EH somberly. He looked out at a row of police in green uniforms guarding the entrance to the Marriot Hotel. One&nbsp;guy in combat boots held a rifle.&nbsp;</p>
<p>“I don’t want to go out of the house this week,” he announced. “I’ll just stay inside and watch TV. Why don’t we all stay in?”</p>
<p>We tried to make light of EH’s worries, but it turns out, he may have been right.</p>
<p>This came in late yesterday on the news wires:</p>
<blockquote>
<h4 class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt; line-height: 11.25pt; mso-margin-top-alt: auto; mso-margin-bottom-alt: auto;"><span style="font-size: 9pt; color: #000000; font-family: &quot;Verdana&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;; mso-fareast-font-family: 'Times New Roman'; mso-bidi-font-family: 'Times New Roman';">Peru arrests man with 36 grenades days before APEC</span></h4>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt; line-height: 11.25pt; mso-margin-top-alt: auto; mso-margin-bottom-alt: auto;"><span style="font-size: 9pt; color: #000000; font-family: &quot;Verdana&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;; mso-fareast-font-family: 'Times New Roman'; mso-bidi-font-family: 'Times New Roman';">Nov 17, 2008 11:53 EST</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt; line-height: 11.25pt; mso-margin-top-alt: auto; mso-margin-bottom-alt: auto;"><span style="font-size: 9pt; color: #000000; font-family: &quot;Verdana&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;; mso-fareast-font-family: 'Times New Roman'; mso-bidi-font-family: 'Times New Roman';">LIMA (Reuters) – Peruvian police arrested a man with 36 grenades in his backpack Monday, just days before 21 heads of state meet in Lima for the APEC summit and one day after insurgents killed three officers in southern Peru.</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt; line-height: 11.25pt; text-align: justify; mso-margin-top-alt: auto; mso-margin-bottom-alt: auto;"><span style="font-size: 9pt; color: #000000; font-family: &quot;Verdana&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;; mso-fareast-font-family: 'Times New Roman'; mso-bidi-font-family: 'Times New Roman';">Edwin Valladolid, 31, was detained in the San Martin district of the capital, police said.</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt; line-height: 11.25pt; text-align: justify; mso-margin-top-alt: auto; mso-margin-bottom-alt: auto;"><span style="font-size: 9pt; color: #000000; font-family: &quot;Verdana&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;; mso-fareast-font-family: 'Times New Roman'; mso-bidi-font-family: 'Times New Roman';">“We think he belongs to a larger group, which we are still trying to identify,” said Police Colonel Julio Vergara, who is in charge of security for the Asia Pacific Economic Cooperation forum.</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt; line-height: 11.25pt; text-align: justify; mso-margin-top-alt: auto; mso-margin-bottom-alt: auto;"><span style="font-size: 9pt; color: #000000; font-family: &quot;Verdana&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;; mso-fareast-font-family: 'Times New Roman'; mso-bidi-font-family: 'Times New Roman';">The arrest comes as suspected holdouts of the Maoist Shining Path rebel group, which led a bloody rebellion until its leaders were captured in the 1990s, have launched a series of attacks in recent weeks.</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt; line-height: 11.25pt; text-align: justify; mso-margin-top-alt: auto; mso-margin-bottom-alt: auto;"><span style="font-size: 9pt; color: #000000; font-family: &quot;Verdana&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;; mso-fareast-font-family: 'Times New Roman'; mso-bidi-font-family: 'Times New Roman';">On Sunday, the group, which has largely abandoned its ideological struggle in favor of running drugs in the world’s No. 2 cocaine producer, shot and killed three police officers and wounded another in the region of Ayacucho, about 360 miles southeast of Lima.</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt; line-height: 11.25pt; text-align: justify; mso-margin-top-alt: auto; mso-margin-bottom-alt: auto;"><span style="font-size: 9pt; color: #000000; font-family: &quot;Verdana&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;; mso-fareast-font-family: 'Times New Roman'; mso-bidi-font-family: 'Times New Roman';">The Shining Path was also blamed for blowing up an army convoy last month in the Andean region of Huancavelica, killing 15 people.</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt; line-height: 11.25pt; text-align: justify; mso-margin-top-alt: auto; mso-margin-bottom-alt: auto;"><span style="font-size: 9pt; color: #000000; font-family: &quot;Verdana&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;; mso-fareast-font-family: 'Times New Roman'; mso-bidi-font-family: 'Times New Roman';">The group, which police said stole dynamite from a mining camp of U.S.-based metals company Doe Run in October, also detonated a car bomb in front of the U.S. Embassy in Lima in 2002 before a visit by U.S. President George W. Bush.</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt; line-height: 11.25pt; text-align: justify; mso-margin-top-alt: auto; mso-margin-bottom-alt: auto;"><strong><span style="font-size: 9pt; color: #000000; font-family: &quot;Verdana&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;; mso-fareast-font-family: 'Times New Roman'; mso-bidi-font-family: 'Times New Roman';">Source:</span></strong><span style="font-size: 9pt; color: #000000; font-family: &quot;Verdana&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;; mso-fareast-font-family: 'Times New Roman'; mso-bidi-font-family: 'Times New Roman';"> <a href="http://www.reuters.com/" target="_blank"><strong><span style="color: #990000; font-family: &quot;Arial&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;; text-decoration: none; mso-bidi-font-size: 11.0pt; text-underline: none;">Reuters North American News Service</span></strong></a> (Reporting by Reporting by Diego Ore, Writing by Terry Wade; Editing by Dana Ford)</span></p>
</blockquote>
<p>San Martin, where the suspect was arrested, is an area in downtown Lima, which is a distance from where the summit meetings are being held.&nbsp;It’s about a 15-minute cabride away during normal traffic conditions, a longer ride during APEC week.</p>
<p>The suspect’s capture appears to confirm what many observers had been suspecting for weeks: holding the APEC in Lima is like waving a red flag to Peru’s homegrown terrorist groups.</p>
<p>El Hijo’s fears are justified.</p>

			<h4>November 15th, 2008<!-- by Barb --> · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/11/15/best-cevicherias-seafood-restaurants-in-lima-where-to-dine-during-apec-week/#comments" title="Comment on Best Cevicherias &amp; Seafood Restaurants in Lima: Where to Dine During APEC Week">12 Comments</a>  · <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/food/" title="View all posts in Food &amp; Dining" rel="category tag">Food &amp; Dining</a>, <a href="http://americaninlima.com/category/money-econ-poli/" title="View all posts in Money, Economics, Politics" rel="category tag">Money, Economics, Politics</a> </h4>
			<div class="entry">
				<p>An American in Lima recommends…</p>
<h6 class="mceTemp mceIEcenter">
<dl class="wp-caption aligncenter" style="width: 290px;">
<dt class="wp-caption-dt"><img style="margin: 10px 15px; border: black 5px solid;" src="http://i190.photobucket.com/albums/z306/burumun/Peru/peruvianrice.jpg" border="0" alt="" width="280" height="210"></dt>
<dd class="wp-caption-dd">Seafood and rice at La Mar</dd>
</dl>
</h6>
<p><strong>La Mar </strong>(Av. La Mar 770, Miraflores; 421-3365): Gaston Acurio’s chic cevicheria is sure to be on many APEC dignitaries’ “must eat at” lists. There’s always a line at this trendsetting restaurant, so expect even longer waits during APEC week. La Mar’s head chef tells <a href="http://americaninlima.com" target="_blank">An American in Lima </a>he’s ordering 30% more&nbsp;fish than usual to accommodate the rush. Open 12:30 p.m. to 5:30 p.m. only.*</p>
<p><strong><a href="http://americaninlima.com/2008/08/14/olympic-ceviche-with-a-political-bite-%E2%80%93-pescados-capitales/" target="_blank">Pescados Capitales </a></strong>(La Mar 1337, Miraflores; 421-8808): Top-notch, innovative ceviche served in upscale/rustic surroundings. I lurve, lurve, lurve this place, which is several blocks down the same street as Acurio’s La Mar.&nbsp; I stopped by Friday for a private tasting (lucky me!) with owner Nguyen Chavez, who gives his chefs latitude to create six new dishes each week. (This week’s dishes are inspired by APEC themes.) As of Friday, the wife of one South American president had already called to reserve a table for lunch this week, so expect the place to be full of bigwigs during APEC-o-rama. Open 12:30 – 5 p.m. only. *</p>
<p><strong>Pez Amigo </strong>(Av. La Paz 1640, Miraflores; 445-9783): This low-key, elegant restaurant isn’t patronized by the&nbsp;tour companies that parade busloads of tourists through Lima, which is exactly why it’s a good bet for a relaxing lunch. I have never been disappointed by any of the dishes I’ve ordered here; Pez Amigo (Friendly Fish) offers flavorful, well-prepared&nbsp;seafood in a relaxing, intimate&nbsp;setting. I’d recommend this locals’ favorite to visiting dignitaries who want to dine away from the media limelight.</p>
<p><strong>Punto Azul </strong>(Calle San Martin 595, Miraflores; 445-8078): Limeños adore this Miraflores restaurant, which serves very good ceviche and seafood in gigantic portions. Prices are reasonable and service is friendly. You can dine like a king here for less than US$10. Not as exclusive as La Mar or Pescados Capitales, but <em>muy rico</em>. My pick for APEC delegates with big appetites who want to mix with locals.</p>
<p><strong>Edo’s Sushi House </strong>(Calle Berlin 601, Miraflores): A friendly, buzzing sushi place with various locations throughout Lima. I go to the one on Calle Berlin, and it’s become my weekday sushi hangout. Very good maki rolls, with Peruvian fusion touches&nbsp;incorporating criolla sauces and&nbsp;sliced fruit.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>Osaka Sushi Bar </strong>(Av. Conquistadores 999, San Isidro, 222-0405): This upscale sushi bar is elegant and pricey and worth every penny of it. The&nbsp;dishes fuse Peruvian and Asian flavors, with&nbsp;distinctive combinations that make a lunch or dinner here memorable. A thrifty Peruvian might choke at the prices (US$50-$60 is common for lunch for two), but&nbsp;movers and shakers accustomed to eating in Paris or NYC won’t bat an eye at&nbsp; Osaka’s <em>cuentas</em>. The best sushi I’ve had in Lima so far.</p>
<p>AND NOW…WHERE NOT TO EAT</p>
<p>Don’t bother searching for <strong>Segundo Muelle</strong>, the highly praised cevicheria along the Miraflores Malecon, because you won’t find it: The restaurant closed in August due to an <em>escandulo</em> involving unpaid income taxes. What a pity. I thought the food there was great.</p>
<p>Every guidebook you read mentions <strong>La Rosa Nautica</strong>, but I think this classic seafood restaurant is overpriced and a bit cheesy. Don’t get me wrong: the food is good and the views are to-die-for. It’s just that the restaurant has grown into a tourist trap, with a row of souvenir shops lining the wharf to the front door and&nbsp;impersonal service inside. There are extra charges added to the <em>cuenta</em>, and the pisco sours&nbsp;are inferior. Again, the splendid setting on the Pacific Ocean might compensate for some.*</p>
<p><em>* Note: Restaurants&nbsp;marked with an asterisk have been certified by the Ministry of Health.</em></p>
