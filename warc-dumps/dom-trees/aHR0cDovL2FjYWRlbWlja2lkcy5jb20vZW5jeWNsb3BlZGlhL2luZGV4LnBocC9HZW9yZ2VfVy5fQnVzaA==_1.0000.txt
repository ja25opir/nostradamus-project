	  <div id="bodyContent">
	    <h3 id="siteSub">From Academic Kids</h3>
	    <div id="contentSub"></div>
	    	    	    <!-- start content -->
	    <p><b>George Walker Bush</b> (born <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/July_6" title="July 6">July 6</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1946" title="1946">1946</a>) is an <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Politics_of_the_United_States" title="Politics of the United States">American politician</a> and the 43rd and current <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/President_of_the_United_States_of_America" title="President of the United States of America">president</a> of the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Bush, a member of the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Republican_Party_%28United_States%29" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican Party</a>, is part of the prominent <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Bush_family" title="Bush family">Bush family</a>, which includes his grandfather (former <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">U.S. Senator</a> <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Prescott_Bush" title="Prescott Bush">Prescott Bush</a>), his father (former President <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George H. W. Bush</a>), and his brother (<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Jeb_Bush" title="Jeb Bush">Jeb Bush</a>, the current <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Governor_of_Florida" title="Governor of Florida">Florida governor</a>).
</p>

<p>Before becoming president, he was a businessman, involved in the oil industry and professional <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a>.<sup id="fn_oil_and_baseball_back" class="plainlinksneverexpand"><a href="http://academickids.com/encyclopedia/index.php/George_W._Bush#fn_oil_and_baseball" class="external" title="http://academickids.com/encyclopedia/index.php/George W. Bush#fn oil and baseball" rel="nofollow">[1]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://academickids.com/encyclopedia/index.php/George_W._Bush#fn_oil_and_baseball</i>)</span></sup> He was elected the 46th <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Governor_of_Texas" title="Governor of Texas">Governor</a> of <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, and won the nomination of the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Republican_Party_%28United_States%29" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican Party</a> in the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._presidential_election%2C_2000" title="U.S. presidential election, 2000">2000 presidential election</a>. Bush became <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a>, defeating <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a> of the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Democratic_Party_%28United_States%29" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a> in a particularly <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._presidential_election%2C_2000#Florida_election_results" title="U.S. presidential election, 2000">controversial and close</a> <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._presidential_election%2C_2000" title="U.S. presidential election, 2000">election</a>. Bush <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=U.S._presidential_election%2C_2004&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="U.S. presidential election, 2004">was re-elected</a> in 2004, running against Senator <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=John_Kerry&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="John Kerry">John Kerry</a> of <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>.
</p>

<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Personal_life.2C_service_and_education"></a><h2>Personal life, service and education</h2>
<p>Bush is the son of <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George H. W. Bush</a> and <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Barbara_Bush" title="Barbara Bush">Barbara Bush</a>. He was born in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/New_Haven%2C_Connecticut" title="New Haven, Connecticut">New Haven</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a> but grew up in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Midland%2C_Texas" title="Midland, Texas">Midland</a> and <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Houston%2C_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, with his siblings: Jeb, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Neil_Bush" title="Neil Bush">Neil</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Marvin_Bush" title="Marvin Bush">Marvin</a>, and <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Dorothy_Bush" title="Dorothy Bush">Dorothy</a> (a younger sister, Robin, died of leukemia in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1953" title="1953">1953</a> at the age of three). The family spent the summers and most holidays at the  <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Bush_Compound&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Bush Compound">Bush Compound</a> in Maine.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div style="width:182px;"><a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Image:Bush_daughers.gif" class="internal" title="George W. Bush and Laura Bush with their daughters Jenna and Barbara, 1990"><img src="/encyclopedia/images/thumb/7/7f/180px-Bush_daughers.gif" alt="George W. Bush and Laura Bush with their daughters Jenna and Barbara, 1990" width="180" height="124" longdesc="/encyclopedia/index.php/Image:Bush_daughers.gif"></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Image:Bush_daughers.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/encyclopedia/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="Enlarge"></a></div>George W. Bush and Laura Bush with their daughters Jenna and Barbara, 1990</div></div></div>
<p>Like his father, Bush attended <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Phillips_Academy" title="Phillips Academy">Phillips Academy</a> (September <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1961" title="1961">1961</a>–June <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1964" title="1964">1964</a>) and later <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> (Sept. 1964–May <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1968" title="1968">1968</a>). At Yale, he joined <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Delta_Kappa_Epsilon" title="Delta Kappa Epsilon">Delta Kappa Epsilon</a> (of which he was president from October <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1965" title="1965">1965</a> until graduation) and the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Skull_and_Bones" title="Skull and Bones">Skull and Bones</a> secret society. He was a "C" student, scoring 77 percent (with no As and one D, in astronomy) with a <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Grade_point_average" title="Grade point average">grade point average</a> of 2.35 out of 4.00. He played baseball and rugby union during his freshman and senior years. He received a Bachelor of Arts degree in history in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1968" title="1968">1968</a>.
</p><p>After graduating from Yale, Bush enlisted in the Texas <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Air_National_Guard" title="Air National Guard">Air National Guard</a> on <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/May_27" title="May 27">May 27</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1968" title="1968">1968</a>, during the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, with a commitment to serve until <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/May_26" title="May 26">May 26</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1974" title="1974">1974</a>. He was promoted once, to <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/First_lieutenant" title="First lieutenant">first lieutenant</a>, on the November <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1970" title="1970">1970</a> recommendation of Texas Air National Guard commander Lt. Col. <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Jerry_B._Killian&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Jerry B. Killian">Jerry B. Killian</a>. He served as an <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/F-102" title="F-102">F-102</a> pilot until 1972.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div style="width:182px;"><b>Missing image</b><br><i>GW-Bush-in-uniform.jpg</i>  <div class="thumbcaption">George W. Bush in his National Guard uniform.</div></div></div>
<p>In September <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1973" title="1973">1973</a>, he received permission to end his six-year commitment six months early in order to attend <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Business School</a>. He transferred to inactive reserve status shortly before being honorably discharged on <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/October_1" title="October 1">October 1</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1973" title="1973">1973</a>. <a href="http://www.usatoday.com/news/bushdocs/2-Discharge.pdf" class="external" title="http://www.usatoday.com/news/bushdocs/2-Discharge.pdf" rel="nofollow">[2]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.usatoday.com/news/bushdocs/2-Discharge.pdf</i>)</span>
</p><p>It has frequently been alleged that Bush skipped over a waiting list to receive a National Guard slot, that he did not report for required duty from 1972 to 1973, and that he was suspended from flying after he failed to take a required physical examination and drug test. These issues were publicized during the 2004 Presidential campaign by the group <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Texans_for_Truth&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Texans for Truth">Texans for Truth</a> and other Bush critics. See <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/George_W._Bush_military_service_controversy" title="George W. Bush military service controversy">George W. Bush military service controversy</a> for details.
</p><p>Bush entered Harvard Business School in 1973. He was awarded a <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Masters_of_Business_Administration" title="Masters of Business Administration">Masters of Business Administration</a> (MBA) degree in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1975" title="1975">1975</a>, and is the first U.S. president to hold an MBA degree.
</p><p>On <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/September_4" title="September 4">September 4</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1976" title="1976">1976</a>, Bush was pulled over by police near his family's summer home in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Kennebunkport%2C_Maine" title="Kennebunkport, Maine">Kennebunkport</a>, Maine. He was arrested for <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Drunk_driving_%28United_States%29" title="Drunk driving (United States)">driving under the influence of alcohol</a>, admitted his guilt, was fined $150, and had his driving license suspended for 30 days within the state. <a href="http://archives.cnn.com/2000/ALLPOLITICS/stories/11/02/bush.dui/" class="external" title="http://archives.cnn.com/2000/ALLPOLITICS/stories/11/02/bush.dui/" rel="nofollow">[3]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://archives.cnn.com/2000/ALLPOLITICS/stories/11/02/bush.dui/</i>)</span>, <a href="http://www.thesmokinggun.com/archive/bushdui1.html" class="external" title="http://www.thesmokinggun.com/archive/bushdui1.html" rel="nofollow">[4]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.thesmokinggun.com/archive/bushdui1.html</i>)</span> News of the arrest was uncovered by the press five days before the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._presidential_election%2C_2000" title="U.S. presidential election, 2000">2000 presidential election</a>.
</p><p>Bush has described his days before his religious conversion as his "nomadic" period and "irresponsible youth." and admitted to drinking "too much" in those years; he says that although he never joined Alcoholics Anonymous, he gave up drinking for good shortly after waking up with a hangover after his 40th birthday celebration: "I quit drinking in 1986 and haven't had a drop since then." He ascribed the change in part to a 1985 meeting with The Rev. Billy Graham. [<a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-srv/politics/campaigns/wh2000/stories/bushtext072599.htm" class="external" title="http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-srv/politics/campaigns/wh2000/stories/bushtext072599.htm" rel="nofollow">[5]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-srv/politics/campaigns/wh2000/stories/bushtext072599.htm</i>)</span>], [<a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-srv/politics/campaigns/wh2000/stories/bush072599.htm" class="external" title="http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-srv/politics/campaigns/wh2000/stories/bush072599.htm" rel="nofollow">[6]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-srv/politics/campaigns/wh2000/stories/bush072599.htm</i>)</span>]. In taped recordings of a conversation with an old friend, author Doug Wead, Bush said: “I wouldn’t answer the marijuana question. You know why? Because I don’t want some little kid doing what I tried.” When Wead reminded Bush that the latter had publicly denied using cocaine, Bush replied, "I haven't denied anything." [<a href="http://news.bbc.co.uk/1/hi/world/americas/4282799.stm" class="external" title="http://news.bbc.co.uk/1/hi/world/americas/4282799.stm" rel="nofollow">[7]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://news.bbc.co.uk/1/hi/world/americas/4282799.stm</i>)</span>], [<a href="http://www.msnbc.msn.com/id/6999665/" class="external" title="http://www.msnbc.msn.com/id/6999665/" rel="nofollow">[8]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.msnbc.msn.com/id/6999665/</i>)</span>].
</p><p>Bush married <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Laura_Bush" title="Laura Bush">Laura Welch</a> in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1977" title="1977">1977</a>. They had twin daughters, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Barbara_and_Jenna_Bush" title="Barbara and Jenna Bush">Barbara and Jenna Bush</a>, born in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1981" title="1981">1981</a>. In <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1986" title="1986">1986</a>, at the age of 40, he left the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Episcopal_Church" title="Episcopal Church">Episcopal Church</a> and joined his wife's denomination, the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_Methodist_Church" title="United Methodist Church">United Methodist Church</a>.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Business_and_early_political_career"></a><h2>Business and early political career</h2>
<p>In <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1978" title="1978">1978</a>, Bush ran for the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">U.S. House of Representatives</a> but lost to a <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Texas_Legislature" title="Texas Legislature">State Senator</a>, Democrat <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Kent_Hance" title="Kent Hance">Kent Hance</a>.
</p><p>Bush began his career in the oil industry in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1979" title="1979">1979</a> when he established <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Arbusto_Energy" title="Arbusto Energy">Arbusto Energy</a>, an oil and gas exploration company he formed in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1977" title="1977">1977</a> with leftover funds from his education trust fund and money from other investors. The <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1979_energy_crisis" title="1979 energy crisis">1979 energy crisis</a> hurt Arbusto and, after a name change to Bush Exploration Co., Bush sold the company in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1984" title="1984">1984</a> to <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Spectrum_7&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Spectrum 7">Spectrum 7</a>, another Texas oil and gas exploration firm. Under the terms of the sale, Spectrum 7 made Bush its Chief Executive Officer. Spectrum 7 lost money, and in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1986" title="1986">1986</a> it was merged into <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Harken_Energy" title="Harken Energy">Harken Energy</a> Corporation, with Bush becoming a director of Harken.
</p><p>After working on his father's successful <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._presidential_election%2C_1988" title="U.S. presidential election, 1988">1988 presidential campaign</a>, he was told by a friend, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=William_DeWitt%2C_Jr.&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="William DeWitt, Jr.">William DeWitt, Jr.</a>, that another family friend, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Eddie_Chiles&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Eddie Chiles">Eddie Chiles</a>, wanted to sell the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Texas_Rangers&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Texas Rangers">Texas Rangers</a>, his <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Arlington%2C_Texas" title="Arlington, Texas">Arlington</a>-based <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> franchise. In April <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1989" title="1989">1989</a>, Bush assembled a group of investors from his father's close friends; the group bought 86% of the Rangers for $75 million. (Bush later appointed one of these partners, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Tom_Schieffer" title="Tom Schieffer">Tom Schieffer</a>, to the post of Ambassador to <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.) Bush received a two percent share by investing $606,302, of which $500,000 was a bank loan. Bush paid off the loan by selling $848,000 worth of stock in Harken Energy in 1990. As Harken Energy reported significant financial losses within a year of this sale (as did much of the energy industry due to the recession of the early 1990s), the fact that Bush was advised by his own counsel not to sell his shares later fueled allegations of insider trading. 
(See <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush_insider_trading_allegations&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="George W. Bush insider trading allegations">George W. Bush insider trading allegations</a> for more information.)  The federal <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a> concluded: "it appears that Bush did not engage in illegal insider trading," but noted that its memo "must in no way be construed as indicating that the party has been exonerated or that no action may ultimately result."
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div style="width:202px;"><b>Missing image</b><br><i>Bushtexas.jpg</i>  <div class="thumbcaption">Bush's official gubernatorial portrait, hanging in the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Texas_State_Capitol" title="Texas State Capitol">Texas State Capitol</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Bush served as managing general partner of the Rangers for five years.  He was active in the team's media relations and in securing the construction of a new stadium, which opened in 1994 as <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Ameriquest_Field_in_Arlington" title="Ameriquest Field in Arlington">The Ballpark in Arlington</a>. <a href="http://www.joenickp.com/texas/teamplayer.html" class="external" title="http://www.joenickp.com/texas/teamplayer.html" rel="nofollow">[9]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.joenickp.com/texas/teamplayer.html</i>)</span> Bush's prominent role with the Rangers gave him valuable goodwill and name recognition throughout Texas. <a href="http://espn.go.com/mlb/bush/friday.html" class="external" title="http://espn.go.com/mlb/bush/friday.html" rel="nofollow">[10]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://espn.go.com/mlb/bush/friday.html</i>)</span>
</p><p>In <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1994" title="1994">1994</a>, Bush took a leave of absence from the Rangers to run for <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Governor_of_Texas" title="Governor of Texas">Governor of Texas</a> against the popular <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Incumbent" title="Incumbent">incumbent</a>, Democrat <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Ann_Richards" title="Ann Richards">Ann Richards</a>. On <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/November_8" title="November 8">November 8</a>, 1994, he defeated Richards, 53% to 46%.
</p><p>As Governor, Bush forged a legislative alliance with powerful Texas Lt. Governor <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Bob_Bullock&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Bob Bullock">Bob Bullock</a>, a longtime Democrat, who held similar political views. Bush went on to become, in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1998" title="1998">1998</a>, the first Texas governor to be elected for two consecutive four-year terms. (Until <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1975" title="1975">1975</a>, Texas governors served two-year terms.)
</p><p>During Bush's terms as Governor, he undertook significant legislative changes in the areas of criminal justice, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Tort" title="Tort">tort</a> law, and school financing. Bush took a hard line on capital punishment, and received much criticism for it. More convicts were executed under his terms than any other Texas governor, although the rate of executions was not unusual for Texas. Although there is much consensus that Bush effected significant changes, there is little consensus as to whether these changes were positive or negative in nature. If nothing else, Bush's transformative agenda, in combination with his political and family pedigree, catapulted him onto the national political radar. As the campaigns to succeed <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> as president began in earnest, Bush emerged as a key figure.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Presidential_campaigns"></a><h2>Presidential campaigns</h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div style="width:182px;"><b>Missing image</b><br><i>Bush_43_10-19-04_Stpete.jpg</i>  <div class="thumbcaption">George W. Bush speaks at a campaign rally in 2004.</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/George_W._Bush_presidential_campaign%2C_2000" title="George W. Bush presidential campaign, 2000">Bush's 2000 presidential election campaign</a>, he declared himself to be a <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Compassionate_conservativism&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Compassionate conservativism">"compassionate conservative"</a>. He campaigned on, among other issues, allowing <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Religion" title="Religion">religious</a> <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Charity" title="Charity">charities</a> to participate in federally funded programs, tax cuts, promoting the use of <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Education_voucher" title="Education voucher">education vouchers</a>, supporting oil drilling in the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Arctic_National_Wildlife_Refuge" title="Arctic National Wildlife Refuge">Arctic National Wildlife Refuge</a>, maintaining a balanced federal budget, and restructuring of the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=United_States_armed_forces&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="United States armed forces">armed forces</a>. In foreign policy, he stated that he was against using the U.S. armed forces in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Nation_building" title="Nation building">nation building</a> attempts abroad.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div style="width:182px;"><a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Image:GoreBush.jpg" class="internal" title="Al Gore greets President-Elect Bush at the White House in late December of 2000."><img src="/encyclopedia/images/thumb/4/4d/180px-GoreBush.jpg" alt="Al Gore greets President-Elect Bush at the White House in late December of 2000." width="180" height="148" longdesc="/encyclopedia/index.php/Image:GoreBush.jpg"></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Image:GoreBush.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/encyclopedia/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="Enlarge"></a></div>Al Gore greets President-Elect Bush at the White House in late December of 2000.</div></div></div>
<p>Bush faced Democratic candidate <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a>. Bush took 271 <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._Electoral_College" title="U.S. Electoral College">electoral votes</a> to Gore's 266, including the electoral votes of 30 of the 50 <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">states</a>. Neither candidate received a majority of the popular vote -- Bush took 47.9 percent; Gore, 48.4 percent -- but Gore received about 540,000 more of the 105 million votes cast, a margin of about one-half of one percent. Most of the votes that neither Bush nor Gore won went to <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Green_Party_%28United_States%29" title="Green Party (United States)">Green Party</a> candidate <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a> (2,695,696 votes/2.7%), <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Reform_Party_of_the_United_States_of_America" title="Reform Party of the United States of America">Reform Party</a> candidate <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Pat_Buchanan" title="Pat Buchanan">Pat Buchanan</a>, (449,895/0.4%), and <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Libertarian_Party_%28United_States%29" title="Libertarian Party (United States)">Libertarian Party</a> candidate <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Harry_Browne" title="Harry Browne">Harry Browne</a> (386,024 votes/0.4%). 
</p><p>It was the first presidential election since <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._presidential_election%2C_1888" title="U.S. presidential election, 1888">1888</a> in which the winning candidate received fewer popular votes than his opponent, the first since <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._presidential_election%2C_1876" title="U.S. presidential election, 1876">1876</a> in which the winner of the electoral vote was in dispute, and the first ever to be directly affected by a <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a> decision.
</p><p>The Florida vote, which favored Bush by a slim margin in the initial count, was heavily contested after concerns were raised about flaws and irregularities in the voting and tabulation processes.  There ensued a series of contentious court cases regarding the legality of county-specific and statewide recounts. After machine and manual recounts in four counties still showed Bush as the winner, the Florida Supreme Court ordered a statewide manual recount of all counties. The Bush campaign appealed to the U.S. Supreme Court, which, in its mid-December decision in <i><a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Bush_v._Gore" title="Bush v. Gore">Bush v. Gore</a></i>, overturned the decision and halted all recounts.  Gore then conceded the election.
</p><p>In the final official count, Bush won Florida by 537 votes, giving him the state's 25 electoral votes and the presidency. See <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._presidential_election%2C_2000" title="U.S. presidential election, 2000">U.S. presidential election, 2000</a>. (Also see <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._presidential_election%2C_2000#The_Florida_Ballot_Project_recounts" title="U.S. presidential election, 2000">The 2000 Florida Ballot Project</a>.) Bush was inaugurated President on <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/January_20" title="January 20">January 20</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2001" title="2001">2001</a>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=U.S._presidential_election%2C_2004&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="U.S. presidential election, 2004">2004 election</a>, Bush won a second term, an electoral majority, and also received 3.5 million popular votes more than his Democratic challenger, Senator <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=John_Kerry&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="John Kerry">John Kerry</a>. Bush was the first presidential candidate since his father, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/George_H.W._Bush" title="George H.W. Bush">George H.W. Bush</a> in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._presidential_election%2C_1988" title="U.S. presidential election, 1988">1988</a> to receive a majority of the popular vote. (The intervening elections had seen stronger showings by non-major party candidates such as <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Ross_Perot" title="Ross Perot">Ross Perot</a> and <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a>.) His margin over Kerry of about 3 percent was the smallest popular vote margin for a re-elected President since <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a>'s <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._presidential_election%2C_1916" title="U.S. presidential election, 1916">1916 victory</a>. As in the 2000 election, there were charges raised alleging <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=2004_U.S._presidential_election_controversy_and_irregularities&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="2004 U.S. presidential election controversy and irregularities">voting irregularities</a>, especially in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>. In 2004 they did not lead to recounts that were expected to affect the result. After a <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=2004_U.S._presidential_election_recounts_and_legal_challenges&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="2004 U.S. presidential election recounts and legal challenges">congressional electoral contest</a> -- the second in American history -- failed, a <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Moss_v._Bush&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Moss v. Bush">lawsuit</a> challenging the result in Ohio was withdrawn, because the congressional certification of the electoral votes had rendered the case <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Mootness" title="Mootness">moot</a>. 
</p><p>Bush was inaugurated for his second term on <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/January_20" title="January 20">January 20</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2005" title="2005">2005</a>; the oath was administered by Chief Justice <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/William_Rehnquist" title="William Rehnquist">William Rehnquist</a>. Bush's inaugural speech centered mainly on a theme of spreading freedom and democracy around the world. George W. Bush is the only President to win re-election after losing the popular vote in his first election. Of the three other Presidents who lost the popular vote, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/John_Quincy_Adams" title="John Quincy Adams">John Quincy Adams</a> and <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Benjamin_Harrison" title="Benjamin Harrison">Benjamin Harrison</a> were defeated in their bids for a second term, and <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Rutherford_B._Hayes" title="Rutherford B. Hayes">Rutherford B. Hayes</a> did not seek re-election.
</p>

<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Presidency"></a><h2>Presidency</h2>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Foreign_policy_and_security"></a><h3>Foreign policy and security</h3>
<div>
<p><i>Main article: <b><a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Foreign_policy_of_the_George_W._Bush_administration&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Foreign policy of the George W. Bush administration">Foreign policy of the George W. Bush administration</a></b></i>
</p>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div style="width:182px;"><b>Missing image</b><br><i>Bush_Persson_Prodi.jpg</i>  <div class="thumbcaption">Bush poses with <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> Prime Minister <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=G%3F_Persson&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="G? Persson">G? Persson</a> and former European Union Commission President <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Romano_Prodi" title="Romano Prodi">Romano Prodi</a> at <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Gunnebo_Slott&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Gunnebo Slott">Gunnebo Slott</a> near <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Gothenburg" title="Gothenburg">Gothenburg</a>, Sweden on <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/June_14" title="June 14">June 14</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2001" title="2001">2001</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>During his first presidential visit to Europe in June 2001, Bush came under harsh criticism from European leaders for his rejection of the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>, which is aimed at reducing <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> emissions that contribute to <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. 
In 1997, while representatives of the United States and other countries were still negotiating the Kyoto Protocol, the U.S. Senate had, by a vote of 95-0, opposed any global warming treaty that did not require binding commitments from developing nations.  Although the Kyoto Protocol was symbolically signed by <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Peter_Burleigh&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Peter Burleigh">Peter Burleigh</a>, the acting U.S. Ambassador to the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, in 1998, the Clinton administration never presented it to the Senate for ratification.  <a href="http://www.aaas.org/spp/cstc/pne/pubs/stc/bulletin/articles/12-98/kyoto.htm" class="external" title="http://www.aaas.org/spp/cstc/pne/pubs/stc/bulletin/articles/12-98/kyoto.htm" rel="nofollow">[11]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.aaas.org/spp/cstc/pne/pubs/stc/bulletin/articles/12-98/kyoto.htm</i>)</span> In 2002, Bush came out strongly against the treaty as harmful to economic growth, stating: "My approach recognizes that economic growth is the solution, not the problem." <a href="http://www.whitehouse.gov/news/releases/2002/02/20020214-5.html" class="external" title="http://www.whitehouse.gov/news/releases/2002/02/20020214-5.html" rel="nofollow">[12]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.whitehouse.gov/news/releases/2002/02/20020214-5.html</i>)</span>  The administration also disputed the scientific basis for the treaty. <a href="http://www.alternet.org/story/11054/" class="external" title="http://www.alternet.org/story/11054/" rel="nofollow">[13]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.alternet.org/story/11054/</i>)</span> In November 2004, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> ratified the treaty, giving it the required minimum of nations to put it into force without ratification by the United States.
</p><p>Bush's imposition of a <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Steel_tariff" title="Steel tariff">tariff on imported steel</a> and on Canadian soft lumber was controversial in light of his pursuit of other free market policies, and attracted criticism both from his fellow conservatives and from nations affected. The steel tariff was later rescinded under pressure from the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a>.
</p><p>In July of <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2002" title="2002">2002</a>, Bush cut off all funding to the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_Nations_Population_Fund" title="United Nations Population Fund">United Nations Population Fund</a> (UNFPA). Bush claimed that the UNFPA supported forced abortions and sterilizations in China.
</p><p>During his campaign, Bush's foreign policy platform included support of a stronger economic and political relationship with Latin America, especially Mexico, and a reduction in involvement in "nation-building" and other small-scale military engagements. However, after the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/September_11%2C_2001_Terrorist_Attacks" title="September 11, 2001 Terrorist Attacks">September 11, 2001 Terrorist Attacks</a>, the administration focused much more on foreign policy in the Middle East.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div style="width:202px;"><b>Missing image</b><br><i>01-Bush.png</i>  <div class="thumbcaption">Bush reading "<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/The_Pet_Goat" title="The Pet Goat">The Pet Goat</a>" in a classroom after being informed of the attack on the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/World_Trade_Center" title="World Trade Center">World Trade Center</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Shortly after the attacks, Bush launched a war against <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> to topple the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Taliban" title="Taliban">Taliban</a>, which he charged with harboring <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Osama_bin_Laden" title="Osama bin Laden">Osama bin Laden</a>. This action had strong international support, and the Taliban government folded quickly after the invasion. Subsequent nation-building efforts in concert with the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> under Afghan president <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Hamid_Karzai" title="Hamid Karzai">Hamid Karzai</a> have had mixed results; bin Laden was not apprehended or killed, and (<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=As_of_2005&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="As of 2005">as of 2005</a>) is still at large. A sizeable contingent of troops and advisors remains into 2005. See <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._invasion_of_Afghanistan" title="U.S. invasion of Afghanistan">U.S. invasion of Afghanistan</a> for details. <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Afghan_presidential_election%2C_2004" title="Afghan presidential election, 2004">Democratic elections</a> were held on <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/October_9" title="October 9">October 9</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2004" title="2004">2004</a>. There were allegations of flawed registration and validation, and 15 of the 18 presidential candidates threatened to withdraw, but international observers called the elections "fairly democratic" at the "overall majority" of polling centers. 
</p>

The rest of the world hears you. And the people who knocked these buildings down will hear all of us soon."</div></div></div>
<p>On <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/December_14" title="December 14">December 14</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2001" title="2001">2001</a>, Bush withdrew from the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1972" title="1972">1972</a> <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Anti-Ballistic_Missile_Treaty" title="Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty">Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty</a>, which had been a bedrock of U.S.-<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> nuclear stability during the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, arguing it was no longer relevant. Bush has since then focused resources on a ballistic missile defense system. The proposed system has been the subject of much <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/National_Missile_Defense#July_2003_Report_of_the_American_Physical_Society" title="National Missile Defense">scientific criticism</a>. Field tests have been mixed, with both some successes and failures. It is scheduled to start deployment in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2005" title="2005">2005</a>. A ballistic missile defense system will not stop cruise missiles, or missiles transported by boat or land vehicle. Hence, many critics of the system believe it is an expensive mistake, built for the least likely attack, a nuclear tipped ballistic missile. Bush has also increased spending on military research and development and the modernization of weapons systems, but cancelled programs such as the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/XM2001_Crusader" title="XM2001 Crusader">Crusader</a> self-propelled artillery system. The administration also began initial research into bunker-busting nuclear missiles.
</p>

<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Iraq"></a><h4>Iraq</h4>
<p>Since the 1998 enactment of the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Iraq_Liberation_Act" title="Iraq Liberation Act">Iraq Liberation Act</a>, stated U.S. policy had been to remove <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Saddam_Hussein" title="Saddam Hussein">Saddam Hussein</a> from power in Iraq. After the 9/11 attacks, the Bush administration argued that the Iraq situation had now become urgent. The stated premise was that Saddam's regime had tried to acquire nuclear material and had not properly accounted for biological and chemical material it was known to possess, potential <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Weapons of mass destruction">weapons of mass destruction</a> (WMD) in violation of U.N. sanctions. There is debate between supporters and opponents of the war about whether the U.S. had any evidence that Iraq possessed WMD and whether they had any evidence of ties between Iraq and Al-Qaeda. <a href="http://www.cia.gov/cia/reports/iraq_wmd/Iraq_Oct_2002.htm" class="external" title="http://www.cia.gov/cia/reports/iraq wmd/Iraq Oct 2002.htm" rel="nofollow">[14]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.cia.gov/cia/reports/iraq_wmd/Iraq_Oct_2002.htm</i>)</span>,<a href="http://www.timesonline.co.uk/article/0,,2087-1593607,00.html" class="external" title="http://www.timesonline.co.uk/article/0,,2087-1593607,00.html" rel="nofollow">[15]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.timesonline.co.uk/article/0,,2087-1593607,00.html</i>)</span> See <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Iraq_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Iraq and weapons of mass destruction">Iraq and weapons of mass destruction</a> and <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Saddam_Hussein_and_Al-Qaeda&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Saddam Hussein and Al-Qaeda">Saddam Hussein and Al-Qaeda</a> for full coverage.
</p><p>Bush contended that Saddam might deliver WMD to terrorists such as <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Al_Qaeda" title="Al Qaeda">al Qaeda</a>.  Beginning in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2002" title="2002">2002</a> and escalating in spring <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2003" title="2003">2003</a>, Bush pressed the UN to act on its disarmament mandates to Iraq, precipitating a <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Iraq_disarmament_crisis" title="Iraq disarmament crisis">diplomatic crisis</a>. He began by pushing for UN weapons inspections in Iraq, which the UN instituted under <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/UN_Security_Council_Resolution_1441" title="UN Security Council Resolution 1441">UN Security Council Resolution 1441</a>. <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Hans_Blix" title="Hans Blix">Hans Blix</a> and <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Mohamed_ElBaradei" title="Mohamed ElBaradei">Mohamed ElBaradei</a> led UN weapons inspectors in Iraq. There were occasional lapses in cooperation and limits on inspections set by the Iraqi government, leading to intense debate over the efficacy of inspections. Increasing pressure from the United States in the spring of 2003 forced the UN weapons inspectors to discontinue inspections. After Saddam's capture, interrogators asked him, "If you had no weapons of mass destruction then why not let the U.N. inspectors into your facilities?" Saddam replied, "We didn’t want them to go into the presidential areas and intrude on our privacy."
</p><p>Within the Bush administration, Secretary of State <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Colin_Powell" title="Colin Powell">Colin Powell</a> urged that the United States not go to war without UN approval.  The administration examined the possibility of seeking a <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/UN_Security_Council" title="UN Security Council">Security Council</a> resolution to authorize the use of military force (in pursuance of <a href="http://www.worldpress.org/specials/iraq/chapterVII.htm" class="external" title="http://www.worldpress.org/specials/iraq/chapterVII.htm" rel="nofollow">Chapter VII</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.worldpress.org/specials/iraq/chapterVII.htm</i>)</span> of the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_Nations_Charter" title="United Nations Charter">United Nations Charter</a>), but abandoned the idea in the face of opposition from the majority of Security Council members and the public threat of a veto from <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/France" title="France">France</a> (cf. <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/The_UN_Security_Council_and_the_Iraq_war" title="The UN Security Council and the Iraq war">The UN Security Council and the Iraq war</a>). Instead, the United States assembled a group of about forty nations, including the United Kingdom, Spain, Italy, and Poland, which Bush called the "<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Coalition_of_the_willing" title="Coalition of the willing">coalition of the willing</a>".
</p><p>The coalition invaded Iraq in March, citing many Security Council resolutions regarding Iraq (<a href="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1441%20(2002)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC" class="external" title="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1441 (2002)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC" rel="nofollow">1441</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1441%20(2002)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC</i>)</span>, <a href="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1205%20(1998)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC" class="external" title="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1205 (1998)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC" rel="nofollow">1205</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1205%20(1998)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC</i>)</span>, <a href="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1137%20(1997)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC" class="external" title="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1137 (1997)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC" rel="nofollow">1137</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1137%20(1997)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC</i>)</span>, <a href="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1134%20(1997)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC" class="external" title="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1134 (1997)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC" rel="nofollow">1134</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1134%20(1997)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC</i>)</span>, 
<a href="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1115%20(1997)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC" class="external" title="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1115 (1997)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC" rel="nofollow">1115</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1115%20(1997)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC</i>)</span>, 
<a href="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1060%20(1996)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC" class="external" title="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1060 (1996)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC" rel="nofollow">1060</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/1060%20(1996)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC</i>)</span>, 
<a href="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/949%20(1994)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC" class="external" title="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/949 (1994)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC" rel="nofollow">949</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/949%20(1994)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC</i>)</span>, 
<a href="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/778%20(1992)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC" class="external" title="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/778 (1992)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC" rel="nofollow">778</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/778%20(1992)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=UNDOC</i>)</span>, 
<a href="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/715%20(1991)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=RESOLUTION" class="external" title="http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/715 (1991)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=RESOLUTION" rel="nofollow">715</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://daccess-ods.un.org/access.nsf/Get?Open&amp;DS=S/RES/715%20(1991)&amp;Lang=E&amp;Area=RESOLUTION</i>)</span>), the current and past lack of Iraqi cooperation with those resolutions, Saddam's intermittent refusal to co-operate with UN weapons inspectors, Saddam's alleged attempt to assassinate former president George Bush in Kuwait, and Saddam's violation of the 1991 cease fire agreement. The coalition argued that these resolutions authorized the use of force.  Other world leaders, such as <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Secretary_General_of_the_United_Nations" title="Secretary General of the United Nations">U.N. Secretary General</a> <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Kofi_Annan" title="Kofi Annan">Kofi Annan</a>, disagreed and called the war illegal.  The primary stated goal of the war was to stop Iraq from deploying and developing WMD by removing Saddam from power. See <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">2003 invasion of Iraq</a> for full coverage. 
</p>

Bush addresses sailors and the nation from the flight deck of the USS Abraham Lincoln off the coast of San Diego, California, where he delivers his famous <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Mission_Accomplished%21&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Mission Accomplished!">Mission Accomplished!</a> speech to declare victory and the end of the Iraq War, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/May_1" title="May 1">May 1</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2003" title="2003">2003</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The coalition was very successful against the conventional Iraqi armed forces, and soon controlled the entire country.  After the declared end of major combat operations on <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/May_1" title="May 1">May 1</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2003" title="2003">2003</a>, however, an insurgency caused substantially more problems than U.S. leaders had anticipated.  The American public's support for Bush's handling of Iraq declined as the combat wore on.  In addition, a bipartisan intelligence review found no credible evidence that Saddam Hussein possessed WMD, although the report did conclude that Hussein's government was actively attempting to acquire technology that would allow Iraq to produce WMD's as soon as U.N. sanctions were lifted. The report also found "no collaborative relationship" between Saddam Hussein and Al-Qaeda. Bush has defended his decision, arguing that "The world is safer today." <a href="http://www.boston.com/news/nation/washington/articles/2003/09/28/blair_echoes_bush_no_regrets_over_iraq_war?mode=PF" class="external" title="http://www.boston.com/news/nation/washington/articles/2003/09/28/blair echoes bush no regrets over iraq war?mode=PF" rel="nofollow">[16]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.boston.com/news/nation/washington/articles/2003/09/28/blair_echoes_bush_no_regrets_over_iraq_war?mode=PF</i>)</span> Other disputed issues have included questions about the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Confirmation_bias" title="Confirmation bias">biased selection and/or distortion</a> of pre-war intelligence reports, democratization of the Middle East, relationship to the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/War_on_Terror" title="War on Terror">War on Terror</a>, effect on the United States' relationship with European powers and on the role and function of the United Nations, debate over nation building, and the impact on nearby countries such as Iran, Syria, Lebanon, and Turkey.
</p><p>The decision-making process of the Bush administration was the subject of a classified British document from 2002, known as the "<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Downing_Street_memo&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Downing Street memo">Downing Street memo</a>", which became public in May 2005. In it, the British Head of the Secret Intelligence Service, Sir <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Richard_Dearlove&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Richard Dearlove">Richard Dearlove</a>, reported on his visit to Washington, D.C. in the summer of 2002:
</p>
<dl><dd>There was a perceptible shift in attitude. Military action was now seen as inevitable. Bush wanted to remove Saddam, through military action, justified by the conjunction of terrorism and WMD. But the intelligence and facts were being fixed around the policy. The NSC had no patience with the UN route, and no enthusiasm for publishing material on the Iraqi regime's record. There was little discussion in Washington of the aftermath after military action.
</dd></dl>

<p>Of the $2.4 trillion budgeted for <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2005" title="2005">2005</a>, about $401 billion <a href="http://www.defenselink.mil/releases/2004/nr20040202-0301.html" class="external" title="http://www.defenselink.mil/releases/2004/nr20040202-0301.html" rel="nofollow">[17]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.defenselink.mil/releases/2004/nr20040202-0301.html</i>)</span> is planned to be spent on defense. Adjusted for inflation, this sum is the highest military budget since the late 1990s, but is roughly comparable to the average during the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>. <a href="http://www.d-n-i.net/charts_data/evolution_of_the_fy_2003_budget.htm" class="external" title="http://www.d-n-i.net/charts data/evolution of the fy 2003 budget.htm" rel="nofollow">[18]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.d-n-i.net/charts_data/evolution_of_the_fy_2003_budget.htm</i>)</span>
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Political_ideology"></a><h4>Political ideology</h4>
<p>Bush's political ideology is generally referred to as <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Conservatism" title="Conservatism">conservative</a> or <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Compassionate_conservatism" title="Compassionate conservatism">compassionate conservative</a>, the latter being a term he has used to describe himself; conservatives have criticized Bush for his willingness to incur large budget deficits.
</p><p>In his 2005 inaugural address he outlined his new foreign policy set forth in the
<a href="http://www.whitehouse.gov/nsc/nss.pdf" class="external" title="http://www.whitehouse.gov/nsc/nss.pdf" rel="nofollow">National Security Strategy of the United States of America (pdf)</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.whitehouse.gov/nsc/nss.pdf</i>)</span>. Supporters of Bush see this policy as a necessary rejection of "balance of power" politics and a redefinition of America's role in the international forum. Critics of Bush see it as a withdrawal of America from the international forum.
</p><p>There is some evidence that Bush's foreign policy is heavily influenced by the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Neoconservatism_%28United_States%29&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Neoconservatism (United States)">neo-conservative</a> <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tank</a> <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Project_for_the_New_American_Century" title="Project for the New American Century">Project for the New American Century</a> (PNAC). In <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1998" title="1998">1998</a>, for instance, PNAC wrote to then President Bill Clinton saying "American policy cannot continue to be crippled by a misguided insistence on unanimity in the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/UN_Security_Council" title="UN Security Council">UN Security Council</a>" urging the overthrow of Saddam Hussein. Many members of PNAC later had prominent positions in the Bush administration which invaded Iraq at a time when other permanent members of the Security Council opposed military action against Iraq.
</p>

<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Faith-based_initiatives"></a><h4>Faith-based initiatives</h4> 
<p>In early 2001, Bush worked with Republicans in Congress to pass legislation changing the way the federal government regulated, taxed and funded charities and non-profit initiatives run by religious organizations. Although prior to the legislation it was possible for these organizations to receive federal assistance, the new legislation removed reporting requirements that required the organizations to separate their charitable functions from their religious functions. Bush also created the <a href="http://www.whitehouse.gov/government/fbci/" class="external" title="http://www.whitehouse.gov/government/fbci/" rel="nofollow">White House Office of Faith-Based and Community Initiatives</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.whitehouse.gov/government/fbci/</i>)</span>. Several organizations such as the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/American_Civil_Liberties_Union" title="American Civil Liberties Union">American Civil Liberties Union</a> have criticized Bush's faith-based initiative program.  Though it does not violate constitutional law, they argue that it opposes <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=The_Establishment_Clause&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="The Establishment Clause">The Establishment Clause</a>.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Diversity"></a><h4>Diversity</h4>
<p>Bush is opposed to the legal recognition of same-sex marriages and supports the establishment of civil unions ("I don't think we should deny people rights to a civil union, a legal arrangement" - ABC News <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/October_26" title="October 26">October 26</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2004" title="2004">2004</a>), and has endorsed the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Federal_Marriage_Amendment" title="Federal Marriage Amendment">Federal Marriage Amendment</a>, a proposed amendment to the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a> that would define marriage as being the union of one man and one woman. Bush reiterated his disagreement with the Republican Party platform that opposed civil unions, and said that the issue of civil unions should be left up to individual <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">states</a>. In his <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/February_2" title="February 2">February 2</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2005" title="2005">2005</a> State of the Union address he repeated his support for the constitutional amendment.
</p><p>During Bush's first term, his nominee as ambassador to Romania, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Michael_E._Guest&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Michael E. Guest">Michael E. Guest</a>, became the first openly gay man to be confirmed by the Senate as a U.S. ambassador. (The first openly gay ambassador, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=James_Hormel&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="James Hormel">James Hormel</a>, received a <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Recess_appointment" title="Recess appointment">recess appointment</a> from <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> after the Senate failed to confirm the nomination.)  <a href="http://www.commondreams.org/news2001/0925-01.htm" class="external" title="http://www.commondreams.org/news2001/0925-01.htm" rel="nofollow">[19]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.commondreams.org/news2001/0925-01.htm</i>)</span>
</p><p>Bush has opposed most forms of <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Affirmative_action" title="Affirmative action">affirmative action</a>, but expressed appreciation for the Supreme Court's ruling upholding the selection of college applicants for purposes of diversity. Bush has met with the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/National_Urban_League" title="National Urban League">National Urban League</a> as President, but has not yet met with the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/NAACP" title="NAACP">NAACP</a> as a group since he became president, though he did address the NAACP at its 2000 convention in Baltimore as a presidential candidate, and he met with outgoing NAACP President <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Kweisi_Mfume&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Kweisi Mfume">Kweisi Mfume</a> on December 21, 2004. Colin Powell became the first African-American man to serve as Secretary of State during Bush's first term in office. In 2005 he was succeeded by Condoleezza Rice, who became the first African-American woman to hold the post.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Economy"></a><h4>Economy</h4>
<p>During his first term Bush sought and obtained Congressional approval for three major tax cuts, which increased the standard income tax deduction for married couples, eliminated the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Estate_tax" title="Estate tax">estate tax</a>, and reduced marginal tax rates, and are scheduled to expire a decade after passage. Bush has asked Congress to make the tax cuts permanent.
</p><p>According to the Center on Budget and Policy Priorities, by 2003 these tax cuts had reduced total federal revenue, as a percentage of the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Gross_Domestic_Product" title="Gross Domestic Product">Gross Domestic Product</a> (GDP), to the lowest level since 1959. <a href="http://www.cbpp.org/10-21-03tax.htm" class="external" title="http://www.cbpp.org/10-21-03tax.htm" rel="nofollow">[20]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.cbpp.org/10-21-03tax.htm</i>)</span>
</p><p>The effect of the tax cuts and simultaneous increases in spending was to create record budget deficits. In the last year of the Clinton administration, the federal budget showed an annual surplus of more than $230 <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Billion" title="Billion">billion</a>. <a href="http://archives.cnn.com/2000/ALLPOLITICS/stories/09/27/clinton.surplus/" class="external" title="http://archives.cnn.com/2000/ALLPOLITICS/stories/09/27/clinton.surplus/" rel="nofollow">[21]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://archives.cnn.com/2000/ALLPOLITICS/stories/09/27/clinton.surplus/</i>)</span> Under Bush, however, the government returned to deficit spending. The annual deficit reached record current-dollar levels of $374 billion in 2003 and $413 billion in 2004, though as a percentage of GDP these deficits are lower than the post-<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> record set under the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> administration in the 1980s. <a href="http://www.cbsnews.com/stories/2003/08/26/politics/main570166.shtml" class="external" title="http://www.cbsnews.com/stories/2003/08/26/politics/main570166.shtml" rel="nofollow">[22]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.cbsnews.com/stories/2003/08/26/politics/main570166.shtml</i>)</span>, <a href="http://www.usatoday.com/news/washington/2004-10-14-deficit_x.htm" class="external" title="http://www.usatoday.com/news/washington/2004-10-14-deficit x.htm" rel="nofollow">[23]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.usatoday.com/news/washington/2004-10-14-deficit_x.htm</i>)</span>
</p><p>In an open letter to Bush in 2004, more than 100 professors of business and economics at U.S. business schools ascribed this "fiscal reversal" to Bush's "policy of slashing taxes - primarily for those at the upper reaches of the income distribution". <a href="http://www.yubanet.com/artman/publish/article_14283.shtml" class="external" title="http://www.yubanet.com/artman/publish/article 14283.shtml" rel="nofollow">[24]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.yubanet.com/artman/publish/article_14283.shtml</i>)</span> Bush's supporters have countered that, primarily because of the doubling of the value of the child tax credit, "7.8 million low and middle-income families had their entire income tax liabilities erased by the cuts." <a href="http://www.taxfoundation.org/publications/show/102.html" class="external" title="http://www.taxfoundation.org/publications/show/102.html" rel="nofollow">[25]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.taxfoundation.org/publications/show/102.html</i>)</span>
</p><p>According to the "baseline" forecast of federal revenue and spending by the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Congressional_Budget_Office&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Congressional Budget Office">Congressional Budget Office</a> (in its January 2005 Baseline Budget Projections, <a href="http://www.cbo.gov/showdoc.cfm?index=6060&amp;sequence=2" class="external" title="http://www.cbo.gov/showdoc.cfm?index=6060&amp;sequence=2" rel="nofollow">[26]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.cbo.gov/showdoc.cfm?index=6060&amp;sequence=2</i>)</span>), the trend of growing deficits under Bush's first term will become shrinking deficits in his second term.  In this projection the deficit will fall to $368 billion in 2005, $261 billion in 2007, and $207 billion in 2009, with a small surplus by 2012.  The CBO noted, however, that this projection "omits a significant amount of spending that will occur this year--and possibly for some time to come--for U.S. military operations in Iraq and Afghanistan and for other activities related to the global war on terrorism."  The projection also assumes that the Bush tax cuts "will expire as scheduled on December 31, 2010."  If, as Bush has urged, the tax cuts were to be extended, then "the budget outlook for 2015 would change from a surplus of $141 billion to a deficit of $282 billion."
</p><p>After the last jobs report before the 2004 election was released, Kerry supporters continued to criticize Bush as the first American president since <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Herbert_Hoover" title="Herbert Hoover">Herbert Hoover</a> to preside over a net loss of jobs during his term.  With the subsequent November and December numbers, however, Bush ended up with a net gain of jobs during his first term.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Social_Security"></a><h4>Social Security</h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div style="width:302px;"><b>Missing image</b><br><i>George_W._Bush_Omaha_panel_(cropped).jpg</i>  <div class="thumbcaption">Shortly after his second inauguration, Bush (here seen with a panel in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Omaha%2C_Nebraska" title="Omaha, Nebraska">Omaha</a>) toured the nation to promote his proposal for <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Social_Security_%28United_States%29" title="Social Security (United States)">Social Security</a> private accounts.</div></div></div>
<p>Bush has called for major changes in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Social_Security_%28United_States%29" title="Social Security (United States)">Social Security</a>, identifying the issue as a priority for his second term. In 2005, Bush offered a proposal incorporating reductions in benefit levels and partial privatization (allowing individual workers to invest a portion of their Social Security taxes in personal retirement accounts). Most Democrats and some Republicans are critical of such ideas, partly because of the large federal borrowing the plan would require ($1 trillion or more) to finance the transition, and partly because of the problems encountered by the United Kingdom's privatized pension plan. See <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Social_Security_debate_%28United_States%29&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Social Security debate (United States)">Social Security debate (United States)</a>.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Health"></a><h4>Health</h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div style="width:252px;"><b>Missing image</b><br><i>PBAsigning.jpg</i>  <div class="thumbcaption">George W. Bush signing the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Partial-Birth_Abortion_Ban_Act" title="Partial-Birth Abortion Ban Act">Partial-Birth Abortion Ban Act</a> of 2003, surrounded by senators and congressmen.</div></div></div>
<p>Bush signed the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Medicare_Prescription_Drug%2C_Improvement%2C_and_Modernization_Act" title="Medicare Prescription Drug, Improvement, and Modernization Act">Medicare Act of 2003</a>, which added prescription drug coverage to <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Medicare_%28United_States%29" title="Medicare (United States)">Medicare</a>, subsidized pharmaceutical corporations, and prohibited the Federal government from negotiating discounts with drug companies.
</p><p>Bush is <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Pro-life" title="Pro-life">pro-life</a>; his aim, in his words, is to "promote a <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Culture_of_life&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Culture of life">culture of life</a>."
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Education"></a><h4>Education</h4>
<p>In January of <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2002" title="2002">2002</a>, Bush signed the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/No_Child_Left_Behind_Act" title="No Child Left Behind Act">No Child Left Behind Act</a>, which targets supporting early learning, measures student performance, gives options over failing schools, and ensures more resources for schools. Critics (including Senator Kerry and the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/National_Education_Association" title="National Education Association">National Education Association</a>) say schools were not given the resources to help meet new standards, although the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=U.S._House_Committee_on_Education_and_the_Workforce&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="U.S. House Committee on Education and the Workforce">House Committee on Education and the Workforce</a> said in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/June" title="June">June</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2003" title="2003">2003</a> that in three years under the Bush administration the Education Department's overall funding would have increased by $13.2 billion <a href="http://edworkforce.house.gov/press/press108/06jun/edspending062003.htm" class="external" title="http://edworkforce.house.gov/press/press108/06jun/edspending062003.htm" rel="nofollow">[27]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://edworkforce.house.gov/press/press108/06jun/edspending062003.htm</i>)</span>. Some state governments are refusing to implement provisions of the act as long as they are not adequately funded.<a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/articles/A52720-2004Feb18.html" class="external" title="http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/articles/A52720-2004Feb18.html" rel="nofollow">[28]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/articles/A52720-2004Feb18.html</i>)</span> In January of <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2005" title="2005">2005</a>, <i><a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> reported that the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_States_Department_of_Education" title="United States Department of Education">United States Department of Education</a> had paid $240,000 to conservative political commentator <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Armstrong_Williams&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Armstrong Williams">Armstrong Williams</a> "to promote the law on his nationally syndicated television show and to urge other black journalists to do the same." <a href="http://www.usatoday.com/news/washington/2005-01-06-williams-whitehouse_x.htm" class="external" title="http://www.usatoday.com/news/washington/2005-01-06-williams-whitehouse x.htm" rel="nofollow">[29]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.usatoday.com/news/washington/2005-01-06-williams-whitehouse_x.htm</i>)</span> Williams did not disclose the payments.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Science"></a><h4>Science</h4>
<p>Scientists have repeatedly criticized the Bush administration for reducing funding for scientific research, setting restrictions on federal funding of embryonic <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Stem_cell_research" title="Stem cell research">stem cell research</a> (Bush's supporters have responded that he is also the first president to give funding to "adult" stem cell research), ignoring scientific consensus on <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, and hampering cooperation with foreign scientists by enforcing deterring immigration and visa restrictions. In February <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2004" title="2004">2004</a>, over 5,000 scientists (including 48 Nobel Prize winners) from the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Union_of_Concerned_Scientists" title="Union of Concerned Scientists">Union of Concerned Scientists</a> signed a statement "opposing the Bush administration's use of scientific advice". They stated that "the Bush administration has ignored unbiased scientific advice in the policy-making that is so important for our collective welfare." <a href="http://www.ucsusa.org/global_environment/rsi/page.cfm?pageID=1320" class="external" title="http://www.ucsusa.org/global environment/rsi/page.cfm?pageID=1320" rel="nofollow">[30]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.ucsusa.org/global_environment/rsi/page.cfm?pageID=1320</i>)</span> <a href="http://msnbc.msn.com/id/5722898/" class="external" title="http://msnbc.msn.com/id/5722898/" rel="nofollow">[31]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://msnbc.msn.com/id/5722898/</i>)</span>
</p><p>On <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/January_14" title="January 14">January 14</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2004" title="2004">2004</a>, Bush announced the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Vision_for_Space_Exploration" title="Vision for Space Exploration">Vision for Space Exploration</a>, calling for a return to the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> by <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2020" title="2020">2020</a>, the completion of the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> by <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2010" title="2010">2010</a> and eventually sending astronauts to Mars. <a href="http://www.cnn.com/2004/TECH/space/01/14/bush.space/index.html" class="external" title="http://www.cnn.com/2004/TECH/space/01/14/bush.space/index.html" rel="nofollow">[32]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.cnn.com/2004/TECH/space/01/14/bush.space/index.html</i>)</span> Although the plan was met with a largely tepid reception (<a href="http://www.mercurynews.com/mld/mercurynews/news/world/8572141.htm?1c" class="external" title="http://www.mercurynews.com/mld/mercurynews/news/world/8572141.htm?1c" rel="nofollow">[33]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.mercurynews.com/mld/mercurynews/news/world/8572141.htm?1c</i>)</span>), the budget eventually passed with a few minor changes after the November elections. In January <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2005" title="2005">2005</a> the White House released a new <a href="http://www.ostp.gov/html/SpaceTransFactSheetJan2005.pdf" class="external" title="http://www.ostp.gov/html/SpaceTransFactSheetJan2005.pdf" rel="nofollow">Space Transportation Policy fact sheet</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.ostp.gov/html/SpaceTransFactSheetJan2005.pdf</i>)</span> which outlined the administration's space policy in broad terms and tied the development of space transport capabilities to national security requirements.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Environment"></a><h4>Environment</h4>
<p>Bush's environmental record has been attacked by environmentalists, who charge that his policies cater to industry demands to weaken environmental protections. He signed the Great Lakes Legacy Act of 2002, authorizing the federal government to begin cleaning up pollution and contaminated sediment in the Great Lakes, as well as the Brownfields Legislation in 2002, accelerating the cleanup of abandoned industrial sites, or brownfields. In December 2003, Bush signed legislation implementing key provisions of his <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Healthy_Forests_Initiative&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Healthy Forests Initiative">Healthy Forests Initiative</a>; environmental groups have charged that the plan is simply a giveaway to timber companies. Another subject of controversy is Bush's <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Clear_Skies_Initiative&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Clear Skies Initiative">Clear Skies Initiative</a>; opponents say that the initiative will in fact allow utilities to pollute more than they do currently. Bush has opposed the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> saying it would harm the U.S. economy. Environmental groups note that many Bush Administration officials, in addition to Bush and Cheney, have ties to the energy industry, automotive industry, and other groups that have fought against environmental protections. However, Bush claims his reason for not supporting the Kyoto Protocol is that it is unfairly strict on the U.S. while being unduly lenient with other countries, especially China.  He has also questioned the science behind the global warming phenomenon, insisting that more research be done to determine its validity. (See <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Kyoto_protocol#Position_of_the_United_States" title="Kyoto protocol">America's Kyoto protocol position</a>.)
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Immigration"></a><h4>Immigration</h4>
<p>Bush proposed an immigration bill that would have greatly expanded the use of guest worker visas.
</p>

<p>Bush's cabinet included the largest number of minorities of any U.S. federal cabinet to date, including the first Asian-American female cabinet secretary (Chao). This gives it the distinction of being both the most racially diverse, and, according to the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Guinness_Book_of_World_Records" title="Guinness Book of World Records">Guinness Book of Records</a>, the wealthiest cabinet ever. 
</p><p>There is one non-Republican present in Bush's cabinet: <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_States_Secretary_of_Transportation" title="United States Secretary of Transportation">Secretary of Transportation</a> <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Norman_Mineta" title="Norman Mineta">Norman Mineta</a>, the first Asian-American cabinet secretary, who had previously served as <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_States_Secretary_of_Commerce" title="United States Secretary of Commerce">Secretary of Commerce</a> under <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a>, is a Democrat.
</p><p>His cabinet included figures prominent in past administrations, notably <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Colin_Powell" title="Colin Powell">Colin Powell</a>, who had served as <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_States_National_Security_Advisor" title="United States National Security Advisor">United States National Security Advisor</a> under <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> and <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Chairman_of_the_Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff" title="Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff">Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff</a> under George H. W. Bush and Clinton, and <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">Secretary of Defense</a> <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Donald_Rumsfeld" title="Donald Rumsfeld">Donald Rumsfeld</a>, who had served in the same position under <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a>.<br> Also, Vice President <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Richard_Cheney" title="Richard Cheney">Richard Cheney</a> served as <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">Secretary of Defense</a> under George H. W. Bush.
</p>

<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Public_perception_and_assessments"></a><h2>Public perception and assessments</h2>
<p>Bush has been the subject of both high praise and stringent criticism.  His supporters have focused on matters such as the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=Domestic_policy_of_the_George_W._Bush_administration&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="Domestic policy of the George W. Bush administration">economy</a>, homeland security, and especially his leadership after the September 11 attacks.  His detractors have disagreed on those subjects and have also criticized the passage of the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Patriot_Act" title="Patriot Act">Patriot Act</a>, the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._presidential_election%2C_2000" title="U.S. presidential election, 2000">controversial 2000 election</a>, and the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Occupation_of_Iraq" title="Occupation of Iraq">occupation of Iraq</a>.  The magazine <i><a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=TIME&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="TIME">TIME</a></i> named Bush as its <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Person_of_the_Year" title="Person of the Year">Person of the Year</a> for <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2000" title="2000">2000</a> and for <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2004" title="2004">2004</a>. This award is traditionally given to the person considered by the editors to be the most important newsmaker of the year.
</p>
<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Domestic"></a><h3> Domestic </h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div style="width:177px;"><a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Image:Time_2004_poty.jpg" class="internal" title="Bush as TIME Person of the Year 2004."><img src="/encyclopedia/images/thumb/0/06/175px-Time_2004_poty.jpg" alt="Bush as TIME Person of the Year 2004." width="175" height="230" longdesc="/encyclopedia/index.php/Image:Time_2004_poty.jpg"></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify" style="float:right"><a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Image:Time_2004_poty.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="/encyclopedia/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="Enlarge"></a></div>Bush as <i>TIME</i> Person of the Year 2004.</div></div></div>
<p>In the time of national crisis following the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/September_11%2C_2001_attacks" title="September 11, 2001 attacks">September 11, 2001 attacks</a>, Bush enjoyed approval ratings of greater than 85%. He maintained these extraordinary ratings (the highest approval ratings of any president since such regular polls began in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1938" title="1938">1938</a>) for some months following the attack. They gradually dropped to lower levels, but stayed above 50% for two and half years <a href="http://www.hist.umn.edu/~ruggles/Approval.htm" class="external" title="http://www.hist.umn.edu/~ruggles/Approval.htm" rel="nofollow">[35]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.hist.umn.edu/~ruggles/Approval.htm</i>)</span>.
</p><p>During the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2002" title="2002">2002</a> midterm congressional elections, Bush had the highest approval rating of any president during a midterm election since <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Dwight_Eisenhower" title="Dwight Eisenhower">Dwight Eisenhower</a>. In an unusual deviation from the historical trend of midterm elections, the Republican Party retook control of the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a> and added to their majority in the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a>; typically, the President's party loses Congressional seats in the midterm elections, and 2002 marked only the third midterm election since the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a> that the party in control of the White House gained seats in both houses of Congress (others were <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1902" title="1902">1902</a> and <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/1934" title="1934">1934</a>).
</p><p>In <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2003" title="2003">2003</a>, Bush's approval ratings slowly fell, except for a spike upward at the time of the invasion of Iraq. By late <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2003" title="2003">2003</a>, his approval numbers were in the low to middle 50s, still solid for the third year of a Presidency, when opponents typically begin their campaigns in earnest. Most polls tied the decline to growing concern over the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=U.S.-led_occupation_of_Iraq&amp;action=edit" class="new" title="U.S.-led occupation of Iraq">U.S.-led occupation of Iraq</a> and a slow recovery from the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2001" title="2001">2001</a> recession. Polls of May <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2004" title="2004">2004</a> showed anywhere from a 53 percent approval rating <a href="http://www.rasmussenreports.com/Bush_Job_Approval.htm" class="external" title="http://www.rasmussenreports.com/Bush Job Approval.htm" rel="nofollow">[36]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.rasmussenreports.com/Bush_Job_Approval.htm</i>)</span> to a 46 percent approval rating. <a href="http://www.usatoday.com/news/politicselections/nation/polls/usatodaypolls.htm" class="external" title="http://www.usatoday.com/news/politicselections/nation/polls/usatodaypolls.htm" rel="nofollow">[37]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.usatoday.com/news/politicselections/nation/polls/usatodaypolls.htm</i>)</span> A recent Zogby poll showed Bush's approval rating a 46% for the month of March, 2005 - the lowest Bush had ever received, but with the exception of <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>, the highest low-point rating of any President since polls began. 
</p><p>Over the course of Bush's presidency, tensions between him and former president <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> have eased. Although Bush and his predecessor have great ideological differences, the two appear to have formed a friendship.
</p>

<div class="editsection" style="float:right;margin-left:5px;">[<a href="/encyclopedia/index.php?title=George_W._Bush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="George W. Bush">edit</a>]</div><a name="Outside_the_United_States"></a><h3> Outside the United States </h3>
<p>In general, Bush is less popular abroad than he is in the United States.  Although the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/U.S._invasion_of_Afghanistan" title="U.S. invasion of Afghanistan">U.S. invasion of Afghanistan</a> was widely supported, the <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">2003 invasion of Iraq</a> particularly provoked charges of <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Unilateralism" title="Unilateralism">unilateralism</a>. Polls indicate that the invasion was followed by erosion of support among Europeans for Bush. <a href="http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/europe/3081254.stm" class="external" title="http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/europe/3081254.stm" rel="nofollow">[38]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/europe/3081254.stm</i>)</span> A survey in 2004 found a negative view of him held by a majority of people in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Politics_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Politics of the United Kingdom">Britain</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Politics_of_France" title="Politics of France">France</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Politics_of_Italy" title="Politics of Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Politics_of_Germany" title="Politics of Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Politics_of_Mexico" title="Politics of Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Politics_of_Spain" title="Politics of Spain">Spain</a>, and <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Politics_of_Canada" title="Politics of Canada">Canada</a>. <a href="http://www.cbsnews.com/stories/2004/03/04/world/main604135.shtml" class="external" title="http://www.cbsnews.com/stories/2004/03/04/world/main604135.shtml" rel="nofollow">[39]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.cbsnews.com/stories/2004/03/04/world/main604135.shtml</i>)</span> In Muslim countries Bush's unfavorability ratings are particularly high, often over 90%. <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2004/08/02/opinion/02wright.html" class="external" title="http://www.nytimes.com/2004/08/02/opinion/02wright.html" rel="nofollow">[40]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.nytimes.com/2004/08/02/opinion/02wright.html</i>)</span> Among the non-U.S. nations polled in a worldwide study, Bush's popularity was highest in <a href="/encyclopedia/index.php/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, where 62% reported favorable views.  <a href="http://www.cbc.ca/news/america/poll.html" class="external" title="http://www.cbc.ca/news/america/poll.html" rel="nofollow">[41]</a><span class="urlexpansion">&nbsp;(<i>http://www.cbc.ca/news/america/poll.html</i>)</span> Before the 2004 election, Kerry was preferred to Bush, sometimes by a wide margin, in 30 out of 35 countries polled. After the election, majorities in most countries said that they expected Bush's second term to have a negative impact on peace and security.
</p>
