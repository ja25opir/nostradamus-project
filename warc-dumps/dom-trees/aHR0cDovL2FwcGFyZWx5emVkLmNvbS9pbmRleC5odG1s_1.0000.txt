          <td valign="top"><h1>Spinal 
              Cord Injury : Quadriplegic and Paraplegic Injuries</h1>
            <h4><strong>Paraplegic</strong> and <strong>Quadriplegic</strong> (<b>Tetraplegic</b>) 
              are terms used to describe the medical condition, for a person who has been paralysed due to 
              a <a href="paralysis.html" title="Types of spinal cord injury"><strong>spinal 
              cord injury</strong></a>. This classification depends on the level 
              and severity of a persons <a href="spinalcord.html#spinal-nerves" title="Levels of spinal cord injury"><strong>paralysis</strong></a>, and how it affects their limbs. </h4>
            <h4 align="left">This website provides <a href="support.html" title="Spinal Injury patient support"><b>Patient 
              Information</b></a> about acute spinal cord injuries, as well as 
              treatment, symptoms, information on long term rehabilitation issues 
              and <a href="/forums" title="Spinal Cord Injury support forum"><b>Peer Support</b></a>, to help improve the 
              quality of life of those affected by a spinal cord injury.</h4>
            <h2 align="left"><strong>What is a Spinal 
              Cord Injury ?</strong></h2>
            <h4 align="left">A <b>Spinal Cord Injury</b> 
              (<acronym title="Spinal Cord Injury">SCI</acronym>) is typically defined as damage or trauma to the spinal cord that in 
              turn results in a loss or impaired function resulting in reduced 
              mobility or feeling. </h4>
            <h4 align="left">Typical common causes of damage to the spinal cord, are trauma 
              (car/motorcycle accident, gunshot, falls, sports injuries, etc), 
              or disease (Transverse Myelitis, Polio, Spina Bifida, Friedreich's 
              Ataxia, etc.). The resulting damage to the spinal cord is known 
              as a <a href="paralysis.html#lesion" title="Types of spinal cord injury lesion"><strong>lesion</strong></a>, 
              and the paralysis is known as <a href="quadriplegia.html"><strong>Quadriplegia</strong></a> 
              or Quadraplegia / Tetraplegia if the injury is in the <a href="support/functionality/c1-c3.html"><strong>Cervical</strong></a> 
              (neck) region, or as <a href="paralysis.html"><strong>Paraplegia</strong></a> 
              if the injury is in the <a href="support/functionality/t1-t4.html" title="Functional ability of a paraplegic"><strong>Thoracic</strong></a>, 
              <a href="support/functionality/l1-s5.html" title="Function of a lumbar spinal cord injury"><strong>Lumbar</strong></a> 
              or <a href="support/functionality/l1-s5.html"><strong>Sacral</strong></a> 
              region.</h4>
            <h4 align="left">It is possible for someone to suffer a <a href="broken-neck.html"><strong>Broken 
              Neck</strong></a>,or a <a href="broken-back.html"><strong>Broken 
              Back</strong></a> without becoming paralysed. This occurs when there 
              is a fracture or dislocation of the vertebrae, but the spinal cord 
              has not been damaged. </h4>
            <h2 align="left">What is a Complete and Incomplete Spinal 
              Cord Injury</h2>
            <h4 align="left">There are typically two types of lesions 
              associated with a spinal cord injury, these are known as a complete spinal cord injury
              and an <a href="paralysis.html#incomplete"><strong>incomplete spinal cord injury</strong></a>. A complete type of injury means the person is completely paralysed 
              below their lesion. Whereas an incomplete injury, means only part 
              of the spinal cord is damaged. A person with an incomplete injury 
              may have sensation below their lesion but no movement, or visa versa. 
              There are many types in incomplete spinal cord injuries, and no 
              two are the same.</h4>
            <h4 align="left">Such injuries are known as <a href="spinal-cord-injury/brown-sequard-syndrome.html" title="Brown Sequard spinal cord injury"><b>Brown 
              Sequard Syndrome</b></a>, <a href="spinal-cord-injury/central-cord-syndrome.html" title="Central Cord spinal cord injury"><b>Central 
              Cord Syndrome</b></a>, <a href="spinal-cord-injury/anterior-cord-syndrome.html" title="Anterior spinal cord injury"><b>Anterior 
              Cord Syndrome</b></a> and Posterior Cord Syndrome. </h4>
            <h2 align="left"><strong>What is Spinal Cord Injury 
              Rehabilitation</strong></h2>
            <h4>Someone with a spinal cord injury 
              will have a long road of rehabilitation ahead of them, usually at 
              a<a href="rehabilitation-recovery.html"> <b><strong>Spinal Cord 
              Injury Treatment Unit and Rehabilitation Centre</strong></b></a> 
              or <a href="spinalunits.html" title="Spinal cord injury unit"><b>Spinal 
              Injury Unit</b></a>, and it is important that they keep their sense 
              of humor 
              on their bad days to help them maintain a positive attitude.</h4>
            <h4>Generally, <strong>Paraplegics</strong> will be in hospital for 
              around 5 months, where as <strong>Quadriplegics</strong> can be 
              in hospital for around 6 - 8 months, whilst they undergo rehabilitation. 
              Both Paraplegics and Quadriplegics should have some kind of rehabilitation 
              and physiotherapy before they are discharged from hospital, to help 
              maximise their potential, or help them get used to life in a wheelchair, 
              and to help teach techniques which make everyday life easier.</h4>
            <h4><b>Disabled sports</b>, and <b>wheelchair based sports</b> can 
              be an excellent way to build stamina, and help in rehabilitation 
              by giving confidence and better social skills. The ultimate reward 
              for many disabled sportsmen and women, is to win at the<a href="london-paralympic-games/index.html" title="Paralympic games"><b> 
              Paralympic Games</b></a>, which will be coming to London in 2012.</h4>
            <h2><strong>Spinal Cord Injury Cure and Treatment</strong></h2>
            <h4 align="left">A cure for long term paralysis is still some years 
              in the future, but clinical trials are taking place with <b><a href="olfactoryensheathingcells.html" title="Spinal cord injury treatment">Olfactory 
              Ensheathing Glial (OEG) cells</a></b> and<a href="embryonic-stem-cell-therapy.html"><strong> Embryonic Stem Cell based Therapy</strong></a>. </h4>
            <h2> Paraplegic and Quadriplegic Discussion 
              Forum</h2>
            <h4>If you have any spinal cord injury 
              related questions, please visit our <a href="forums/" title="Spinal cord injury support forum"><strong>Discussion 
              Forums</strong></a> and join in on the many topics there. We will 
              do our best to help you, or at the very least, put you in contact 
              with someone who can if we can't. The discussion forum is intended 
              to be a free flow of information between spinally injured people, 
              carers, and their friends, and everyone is welcome.</h4>
            <h4>Even if you don't have any questions, take a look at the forum 
              anyway, as you may be able offer help and advice to others who have 
              questions. <a href="forums/">More &gt;&gt;&gt;</a></h4>
            <hr> <h2 align="left">Quadriplegic, Tetraplegic, 
              Paraplegic and What it Means</h2>
            <h4 align="left"> Quadraplegic is derived 
              from two separate words from two different languages, Latin and 
              Greek. The word “Quadra”, meaning “four” 
              which is derived from latin, relates to the number of limbs. “Plegic”, 
              is derived from the Greek word “Plegia”, meaning paralysis.</h4>
            <h4 align="left">Put the two together, and you have “Quadraplegia”.</h4>
            <h4 align="left">“Tetra” is derived from the Greek word 
              for “Four”. “Para” is derived from the Greek 
              word for "two" Hence: Tetraplegic and Paraplegic.</h4>
            <h4 align="left"> In Europe, the term for 4 limb paralysis has always 
              been tetraplegia. The Europeans would never dream of combining a 
              Latin and Greek root in one word. </h4>
            <h4 align="left">In 1991, when the American Spinal Cord Injury Classification 
              system was being revised, the difference in names was discussed. 
              The British are more aware of Greek versus Latin names. Since Plegia 
              is a greek word and quadri is Latin, the term quadriplegia mixes 
              language sources. Upon review of the literature, it was recommended 
              that the term tetraplegia be used by the American Spinal Cord Association 
              so that there are not two different words in English referring to 
              the same thing.</h4>
