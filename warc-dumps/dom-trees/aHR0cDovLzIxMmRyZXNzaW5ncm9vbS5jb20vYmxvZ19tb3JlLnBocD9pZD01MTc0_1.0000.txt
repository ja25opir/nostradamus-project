<font size="3"><font face="Arial"><strong>TWO WORTHWHILE / NEW COOKBOOKS<br>
ROSES'S HEAVENLY CAKES / GOURMET TODAY<br>
</strong></font></font><strong><font size="3" face="Arial"> BY / JENIFER LANG</font></strong><strong><font size="3" face="Arial"><br>
</font></strong><br>
<div align="justify"><font size="2" face="Arial">We don’t need cookbooks as much as we did in the old days. As I type this, I am surrounded by over 10,000 volumes on food and wine – one of the larger collections in the country. I am an avid semi-professional cook, and yet, whenever I need a recipe (usually daily) I don’t reach for one of these books but I go to google, or the three or four recipe websites I trust implicitly. Even if it’s a recipe I know well, from a book published 20 years ago that sits on my shelf, I find it online. </font><br>
<br>
<font size="2" face="Arial">Yet cookbooks continue to be published. People collect them, maybe in the same way they collect matches from restaurants even if they don’t smoke. (The analogy is apt – I have been told by publishers that about half of the people who buy cookbooks never cook from them, but only read them for entertainment.) After a dismal few years, cookbook sales are increasingly slightly: sales of books in the cooking/entertaining category were up 4% in the first four months of 2009 over the same period last year, while the sales of adult nonfiction overall sank 9%. Comfort food books, including books on baking, are doing especially well.</font><br>
<br>
<font size="2" face="Arial">In my world, for a cookbook to be meaningful and worthwhile, it has to be more than a whole bunch of recipes between two covers. It has to have a story, a philosophy, a strong point of view. Ideally, it should have many beautiful pictures. If I’m going to pore over delicious-sounding recipes fantasizing about making and eating the dishes, I would like to see what the finished product should look like.</font><br>
<br>
<font size="2" face="Arial">Two new big, heavy cookbooks have grabbed my attention. I think they both have a reason for living. They both cost $40, by the way – if I had to guess, I would say that that seems to be the ceiling for big, flossy books these days, just as any entrée over $40 in a restaurant signals a daunting price point.</font><br>
<strong><font size="3"><br>
</font><font size="3" face="Arial">ROSE'S HEAVENLY CAKES<br>
BY / ROSE LEVY BERANBAUM </font></strong><br>
<br>
<font size="2" face="Arial">This author’s baking skill is legendary, and the reverence she engenders among bakers worldwide is much-deserved. Her outsized reputation as a gifted baker and author was cemented with her previous books, notably The Cake Bible and The Bread Bible. This latest book (her ninth), which she calls “my dream book”, is a logical extension of all of her previous work. When I first received it, I found myself – to quote Rose describing someone else’s reaction – gasping my way through page after page of stunning pictures, depicting desserts that look delicious and inviting. Almost every single recipe in the book (99%) has a picture to accompany it. </font><br>
<br>
<font size="2" face="Arial">Rose’s gift is that she approaches baking with transparent analysis. To say that she is a baking chemist, physicist, and engineer is only half the story. She is able to document her work in such a linear way that it is completely understandable by even the most inexperienced and nervous baker. She leaves nothing to chance. As with Julia Child’s first books, the recipes may seem long-winded, but there is a reason. If you are patient enough to spend the five extra minutes reading the instructions, you will be rewarded by a foolproof recipe and an ideal result.</font><br>
<br>
<font size="2" face="Arial">More than that, though: Rose really has good taste, and a great baking weathervane. This book is filled with cakes that sound just right, right now. I’ve been wanting to make a Tres Leches cake for the longest time – that’s the Mexican cake that’s made with three types of milk. To quote Rose, “the first time I tasted Tres Leches was one of those ‘best things I’ve ever tasted’ moments.” Rose’s version has four types of milk – nonfat milk, whole milk, sweetened condensed milk and heavy cream – that are used to soak the cake. The recipe is perfect, and the result way beyond the church-basement-bake-sale category of many of these home recipes.</font><br>
<br>
<font size="2" face="Arial">There is a whole section in this book called “Baby Cakes” that is more than just cupcakes (what is it with the endless cupcake phase we seem to be in right now??!! – the whole thing is lost on me). It’s little, two- or three-bite cakes and fruit tarts, like Baby Lemon Cheesecakes and Marionberry Shortcake – no, not named after the former mayor of Washington DC but after a berry from Marion County, Oregon.</font><br>
<br>
<font size="2" face="Arial">Rose has a lively, extremely popular food blog called <a class="blog-content-inside-link" href="http://www.realbakingwithrose.com/" target="_blank">REAL BAKING WITH ROSE</a> where, for years, she has been able to have an interchange with her many, many fans. This website has enhanced her work, and provided an outlet for her avid readers. Rose’s fame and website have resulted in quirky responses, from “Julie and Julia”-style marathon feats (baking every recipe in one of her books in one year) to directing her to a like-minded baker-technician who has become her long-distance assistant. It’s also an avenue for Rose’s prodigious energy. Soon she will upload monthly posts called “Outcakes” – all of the things that didn’t make it into the book.</font><br>
<strong><font size="3" face="Arial"><br>
GOURMET TODAY<br>
EDITED BY / RUTH REICHL </font><font size="3" face="Arial"><br>
</font></strong><br>
<font size="2" face="Arial">The bigger of the two books (8 pounds vs. 6.5 pounds for the previous book) is the ironically-timed book from the late Gourmet Magazine.</font><br>
<br>
<font size="2" face="Arial">What to say about the fact that Gourmet Magazine was shut down last week? Of all of the people who were shocked, shocked by the announcement, how many have actually read the magazine in the past few years? Realtors buy copies of it to place on coffee tables for open houses, maybe your mother still had a subscription left over from the old days. But, really, did you use Gourmet or just go to <a class="blog-content-inside-link" href="http://epicurious.com" target="_blank">EPICURIOUS</a>? Did you look forward to its latest issue?</font><br>
<br>
<font size="2" face="Arial">Some wag online said that the NY Times dining section article on restaurant matches last week was so Gourmet-esque – are there no more food subjects to write about? What were they thinking? Did we really need that article?</font><br>
<br>
<font size="2" face="Arial">The gossipy food world is endlessly discussing what will become of Ruth Reichl. But, in the same way that Conde Nast is reserving the right to market Gourmet as a brand in the future, perhaps not as a magazine but for other food-related things, Ruth Reichl is also a brand. She will be fine – she is a talented food heavyweight (figuratively – she lost much weight years ago early in her career and has kept it off). If you follow her on <a class="blog-content-inside-link" href="http://twitter.com/ruthReichl" target="_blank">TWITTER</a>, you’ll see that her life seems to have already moved on to normalcy.</font><br>
<br>
<font size="2" face="Arial">So, this giant book comes out just as its namesake forebear goes to the great kitchen in the sky. Is there a reason for this book now?&nbsp; There is only one quote (from Ruth) on the back of the book, “You aren’t eating the way you used to. None of us are. That realization made us understand that the time had come to create a new cookbook, one dedicated to the way we are eating right now.” Talk about weathervanes! Bingo, she is completely right.</font><br>
<br>
<font size="2" face="Arial">This book is filled with recipes that seem of the moment, and yet timeless. Before I knew how to cook, years ago, I used a copy of Joy of Cooking to look up how to bake a potato (400 degree oven, scrub the skin, etc.). In that same way, Gourmet Today will give us recipes for things that we may not know how to make but probably should, like Chinese Beef with Broccoli or Braised Celery Hearts.</font><br>
<br>
<font size="2" face="Arial">There are no photographs in this book (just some funky drawings), but there are more than 1000 recipes. For the reason that most of us prized Gourmet magazine over the years, for the good and sometimes great recipes, this book is welcome. Ruth may not see it this way, but by publishing this book at the same time as the magazine is closed, she is succinctly describing the new way of food, for now and the future. We need recipes, and sometimes a big book to leaf through for inspiration, when scrolling just won’t do. But we probably don’t need a retro magazine any more, alas.</font><br>
<br>
<font size="2" face="Arial"><strong>BONUS IRONY:</strong> There is a sticker on the front of Gourmet Today that says “A subscription&nbsp; to Gourmet magazine is included with the purchase of this book.” The publisher has agreed to substitute a subscription to Bon Appetit instead, but if you contact them to say that you don’t want Bon Appetit they will give you a $12 refund – which lowers the price of the book considerably!</font><br>
