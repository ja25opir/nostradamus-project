    <div class="field field-type-text field-field-byline"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item odd"><div class="field-label-inline-first">By:&nbsp;</div>Chris Chaplow</div></div></div><h2>ICANN Brussels, June 2010&nbsp;</h2>
<p>
ICANN (Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers) held its 38th Public Meeting at Square Brussels Meeting Centre in June. As always, a large number of issues affecting business and domain owners are currently under consideration. Chris Chaplow outlines some of the more important ones.&nbsp;
</p>
<h2>International Domains </h2>
<p><img src="/sites/andaluciaws.com/files/MVC1000230144intdomains.jpg" alt="Celebration as first Chinese script domains are approved." width="505" height="225"><br>
<font size="1">Celebration as first Chinese script domains are approved.</font></p>
<p>
Chinese internet users should soon find it easier to browse the web, as domain names written in Chinese have been approved.  At the board meeting on Friday, the top-level domains of .china, .hongkong, and .taiwan were passed in both traditional and modern Chinese script, much to the delight of the Chinese delegates (see Video <a href="http://www.youtube.com/user/andaluciavideo#p/u/5/4pqI4BmHdSA" target="_blank">http://www.youtube.com/user/andaluciavideo#p/u/5/4pqI4BmHdSA</a> Where the board approves the Taiwan NIC application). Companies in China, Hong Kong and Taiwan will soon be issuing domains for people and organisations within their countries written with all-Chinese scripts.
</p>
<p>
The decision goes some way towards addressing the contradiction that a considerable number of people use non-Latin script, yet the dominant language online is English, together with other Latin-script languages. We‚Äôre going to have to get used to seeing domain names written in ‚Äòsquiggles‚Äô which we don‚Äôt understand. For instance, the official website of the Dubai government is now <a href="http://xn--ngbn6f.xn--mgbaam7a8h/" target="_blank">ÿØÿ®Ÿä.ÿßŸÖÿßÿ±ÿßÿ™ </a> (Dubai.emarat).
</p>
<p>
This approval builds on earlier work to create internationalised domain names (IDNs) using non-Latin characters in .countryname space managed by the existing country code sponsors and supported by the appropriate governments.
</p>
<p>
Other IDNs previously approved, and now introduced ‚Äòinto the root‚Äô and functioning in the DNS (Domain Name System), were - the dot follows the IDN, since Arabic scripts write from right to left - ŸÖÿµÿ±. (.Egypt in Arabic), ÿßŸÑÿ≥ÿπŸàÿØŸäÿ©. (.Saudi_Arabia), and ÿßŸÖÿßÿ±ÿßÿ™.  (.United_Arab_Emirates).  In these three zones, domain registrations have already begun.   .—Ä—Ñ (.russia in Russian Cyrillic) is the other domain functioning and accepting registrations. The first domain was http://–ø—Ä–µ–∑–∏–¥–µ–Ω—Ç.—Ä—Ñ
</p>
<p>
There are IDNs in another 20 languages currently seeking approval, with Thai and Tamil expected to follow soon.
</p>
<h2>Green Light for .xxx </h2>
<p><table border="0" cellspacing="0" cellpadding="0" align="left">
<tbody>
<tr>
<td><img src="/sites/andaluciaws.com/files/MVC1000220066xxxStuartLawle.jpg" alt="Stuart Lawley, chairman of ICM Registry " width="250" height="350"></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td height="10" align="left" valign="top"><font size="1">Stuart Lawley, chairman of ICM Registry</font></td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</p><p>The .xxx domain, which will cater to adult websites, won approval, but with a number of caveats.&nbsp;When the board passed this motion the mood was very somber, and it was not clear to me whether this was an approval at all. Stuart Lawley, chairman of ICM Registry, put forward the plans for .xxx. Speaking to him later in the day, he was quietly optimistic that it was a ‚Äústep in the right direction‚Äù, and that he might be selling domains by the end of the year.
</p>
<p>
The board would have been guided by the public comments on the ICANN website forum. There were a staggering 19,000 opinions to sift through, but most were opinions for or against the domain, rather than opinion on the complex contractual and legal position in which ICANN finds itself.
</p>
<p>
ICANN had previously given the domain the go-ahead in 2005, but reversed the decision two years later amidst a firestorm of protest from conservative groups, predominantly in the US, which opposed the plan on moral grounds. An independent arbitration panel of retired judges at the International Dispute Resolution Centre in London ruled that the plan should be revisited after analysing evidence about the protests.
</p>
<p>
The scheme is intended to create a place for pornography on the internet. "Those that do want to see it can; those that don't can filter it out," explained Lawley.
</p>
<p>
The problem is that the scheme is purely voluntary and adult sites will still be able to use other domains such as .com. It will only meet its objectives if internet users are able to exert pressure in the future and somehow ensure adult material is kept together in one location. The adult industry is not known for working together. Meanwhile pre-registration has begun for both Adult Content and Non Adult defensive registrations.
</p>
<h2>
Inter-registrar Transfer Policy<br>
(IRTP)</h2>
<p>
Here is an example of the bread-and-butter work carried out by ICANN that goes on unnoticed by the media. As you probably know, you can transfer a domain from one registrar to another. But what happens if somebody hijacks your domain? This can occur when somebody gets email control of your registration account, or because you have nominated a partner or employee as an admin contact, and you are now in dispute with them.
</p>
<p>
<img src="/sites/andaluciaws.com/files/MVC1000220557micheleneylon.jpg" alt="Michele Neylon chairman of the IRTP-B work group" width="505" height="225"><br>
<font size="1">Michele Neylon chairman of the IRTP-B work group</font>
</p>
<p>
If your domain directs users to another server during such a hijack or dispute, then it can be an expensive loss. Should there be an urgent system to return the domain to the pre-transfer state while the matter is resolved? This working group, of which I am a member, has produced an initial report which proposes a new policy called the EDRP (Expedited Domain Return Policy). The <a href="http://www.icann.org/en/public-comment/#irtp-b-initial-report" target="_blank">public comment</a> period is now open and all stakeholders (any internet users or business requiring a secure and stable internet presence) are encouraged to comment.
</p>
<h2>Payment for the Chairman of the Board </h2>
<p>
People are always surprised when they hear that the members of the ICANN board (and other similar councils) are volunteers and that, in spite of the tremendous workload, there is no payment except for reimbursement of travel expenses. After the topic was supported almost unanimously in the public forum, the Board voted to pay the Chairman $70,000 a year, starting from 2011.
</p>
<h2>gTLD‚Äôs</h2>
<p>
<img src="/sites/andaluciaws.com/files/MVC1000220538gtldbrandforum.jpg" alt="Brand management for the new gTLDs discussion panel" width="505" height="225"> <br>
<font size="1">Brand management for the new gTLDs discussion panel</font>
</p>
<p>
This meeting did not deliver any headlines with respect to the new gTLD (generic top-level domain) program. The 4th and probably penultimate edition of the Draft Applicants Guidebook was published in advance of the meeting.
</p>
<p>
The governments were pleased to see that the .countryname will not be accepted at all. The attention turned to the ‚ÄòMOPO‚Äô (Morality and Public Order) issue. What if somebody applies for an offensive name? The ICANN staff were tasked with finding a theoretical foundation for dealing with such a situation .NAZI, .CHILDPORN, .KILLTHEINFIDEL and suchlike are repugnant to just about everyone, but it‚Äôs difficult to set specific restrictions. The central problem, which ICANN faces again and again, is that it is an international organization, whereas morality - and law - are intensely local.
</p>
<p>
Applicant security checks were included, and would be grounds for application rejection. Some delegates in the public meeting questioned the wisdom and practicality of including ‚Äòterrorism‚Äô as a ground for rejection.
</p>
<p>
ICANN is still estimating that the number of applications will be in the order of 500. One group which hasn`t gone public in its intention to apply is the business community, and the so-called .brand .canon and .deloitte are about the only ones who have. One very interesting panel discussion in Brussels was the ‚ÄúBrand management for the new gTLDs‚Äù (listen to the mp3 recording here <a href="http://brussels38.icann.org/node/12533" title="http://brussels38.icann.org/node/12533">http://brussels38.icann.org/node/12533</a>).
</p>
<p>
Multinational companies like Nestl√© saw the gTLDs as problematic; Nestl√© have lots of brands with different names and they do not necessarily pair the name with the sub-brands. For them, it will be more about whether one should make a defensive application, or rely on dispute procedures. That option may not be available to those using acronyms such as BBC or HSBC.
</p>
<p>
One group which may surprise ICANN by their numbers worldwide are the medium-sized organisations who want to see themselves on the centre of the (internet) world stage. RIBA (Royal Association of British Architects) are looking for a marketing opportunity and the gTLD may be it. The cost, at several hundred thousand US dollars, will not be for SMEs, but how many organisations or billionaires in the world who would be prepared to throw this money at recording their name in history, in a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity?
</p>
<p>
As yet there is still no published timeline, and as a result of pressure from many groups, including the .gal (Galicia) and .bsq (Basque) contingent, the Board decided to dedicate its upcoming retreat to setting a timeline.
</p>
<p>
While there's always a chance that the timing will slip, ICANN's next meeting, in early December 2010 in Cartagena, Colombia, will probably produce a starting date for new gTLDs. Or, failing that, the North America meeting in March 2011.
</p>
